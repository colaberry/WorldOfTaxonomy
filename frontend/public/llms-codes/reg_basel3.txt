[1_1] Credit Risk - Standardised Approach -- The Standardised Approach under Basel III/IV sets out a uniform method for banks to calculate credit-risk capital using external ratings and prescribed risk weights. It applies predefined weights to asset classes such as sovereigns, corporates and retail loans, replacing internal models for many exposures and ensuring consistency across institutions. This framework helps regulators compare banks' risk profiles globally.

[1_3] Market Risk - Fundamental Review of Trading Book -- The Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB) is a Basel III/IV component that overhauls market-risk capital standards for banks' trading activities, introducing more granular risk factors, stricter model validation, and a standardized approach to replace legacy methods, thereby improving risk sensitivity and comparability.
[1_4] Operational Risk - Standardised Measurement Approach -- The Standardised Measurement Approach (SMA) is the Basel III/IV method for quantifying operational risk capital. It combines a financial-size based Business Indicator with an empirical loss component to produce a single risk-sensitive capital charge, replacing previous advanced and standardised approaches. The SMA aims to provide a more transparent and comparable assessment across banks.
[1_5] Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) Risk -- CVA risk is the potential loss from changes in the creditworthiness of counterparties affecting the value of derivative contracts. Under Basel III/IV banks must hold capital against CVA risk, using standardized or internal models to calculate exposure and apply risk-weight adjustments.
[1_6] Output Floor -- The Output Floor is a key component of the Basel III/IV framework that establishes a minimum level of risk-weighted assets derived from the standardized approach, limiting how much banks can lower capital requirements using internal models. It requires that at least 72.5 % of a bank's capital charge be based on this floor, a rule that began phasing in from 2023.
[1_7] Capital Buffers (CCyB, G-SIB, D-SIB) -- Capital Buffers are extra capital requirements that banks must hold to absorb losses during periods of stress. They include the Countercyclical Capital Buffer, the Global Systemically Important Bank surcharge, and the Domestic Systemically Important Bank surcharge, each calibrated by regulators to enhance resilience.
[2_1] ICAAP - Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment -- The ICAAP is a bank's internal process for assessing whether its capital is sufficient to cover its risk profile, as required by the Basel III/IV framework. It involves identifying, measuring and aggregating risks, conducting stress tests, and documenting governance and capital planning to demonstrate ongoing compliance.
[2_2] ILAAP - Internal Liquidity Adequacy Assessment -- The ILAAP is a bank's internal Liquidity Adequacy Assessment Process, required under the Basel III/IV framework to evaluate liquidity risk, quantify needed high-quality liquid assets, and ensure the institution can meet cash-flow demands during stress scenarios. It documents governance, methodology, and contingency plans for maintaining adequate liquidity.
[2_3] Supervisory Stress Testing -- Supervisory stress testing under the Basel III/IV framework requires banks to assess resilience to adverse economic scenarios using standardized and forward-looking models to evaluate capital adequacy. Regulators conduct periodic reviews, compare results against thresholds, and may require remedial actions if banks show vulnerabilities.
[2_4] Interest Rate Risk in the Banking Book (IRRBB) -- The IRRBB entry outlines the Basel III/IV standards for identifying, measuring, monitoring and controlling interest-rate risk arising from a bank's non-trading positions. It requires banks to assess how shifts in market rates affect earnings and economic value, implement robust risk-management processes and maintain adequate capital buffers to cover potential losses.
[3_1] Regulatory Disclosure Requirements -- The entry details the mandatory disclosure obligations that banks must meet under Basel III/IV, covering capital adequacy, risk exposures, governance and liquidity metrics. It outlines the reporting frequency, formats and supervisory review processes to promote transparency and market discipline.
[3_2] Key Metrics and Overview -- The 3_2 entry presents a concise overview of the principal quantitative measures used under Basel III/IV, outlining capital adequacy ratios, leverage and liquidity requirements, and the calibration of risk-weighted assets. It summarizes the minimum thresholds for Common Equity Tier 1, total capital, the Liquidity Coverage Ratio and Net Stable Funding Ratio, illustrating how banks must monitor and report these metrics to meet the international regulatory standards.
[3_3] Composition of Capital Disclosures -- The entry details the Basel III/IV requirement that banks publicly disclose the makeup of their regulatory capital, specifying eligible Tier 1 and Tier 2 instruments, the proportion of common equity, and criteria for qualification, to promote transparency and comparability for supervisors and market participants.
[lev] Leverage Ratio -- The leverage ratio is a non-risk-based capital metric that requires banks to hold capital equal to at least a set percentage of their total exposure, including on-balance-sheet assets and certain off-balance-sheet items. It serves as a backstop to risk-based ratios, limiting excessive leverage and enhancing resilience under the Basel III/IV framework.

[lev_2] G-SIB Leverage Ratio Buffer -- The G-SIB Leverage Ratio Buffer is an additional capital requirement imposed on Global Systemically Important Banks under the Basel III/IV framework. It mandates that these banks hold a higher leverage ratio, beyond the standard minimum, to reinforce resilience and limit excessive leverage in the financial system.
[liq] Liquidity Standards -- The "liq" entry defines the Basel III/IV liquidity standards that require banks to hold sufficient high-quality liquid assets to meet short-term cash-flow stresses, measured by the Liquidity Coverage Ratio, and to maintain stable funding profiles over a longer horizon, measured by the Net Stable Funding Ratio.
[liq_1] Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) -- The Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) is a Basel III/IV metric requiring banks to hold high-quality liquid assets sufficient to cover net cash outflows over a 30-day stress period, ensuring short-term resilience to funding shocks. It standardizes liquidity risk management by setting a minimum ratio of 100 percent, with periodic adjustments to reflect market conditions and regulatory updates.
[liq_2] Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) -- The Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) is a Basel III/IV metric that requires banks to maintain a stable funding profile in relation to the liquidity characteristics of their assets over a one-year horizon. It is calculated as the ratio of available stable funding to required stable funding, with a minimum target of 100 percent.
[liq_3] Liquidity Monitoring Tools -- Liquidity Monitoring Tools are regulatory instruments used by banks to assess and manage short-term and long-term liquidity positions, ensuring sufficient high-quality liquid assets to meet obligations under stress. Under Basel III/IV they include the liquidity coverage ratio, net stable funding ratio, and internal dashboards that track cash flows, funding gaps, and stress-scenario outcomes.
[pillar_1] Pillar 1 - Minimum Capital Requirements -- Pillar 1 sets the quantitative capital standards banks must meet, requiring them to hold a minimum amount of Tier 1 and total capital equal to a percentage of risk-weighted assets. It defines the risk categories-credit, market, operational and other risks-and prescribes the formulas for calculating the corresponding risk weights and capital buffers. Compliance ensures banks maintain sufficient loss-absorbing capacity under normal and stressed conditions.
[pillar_2] Pillar 2 - Supervisory Review Process -- Pillar 2 is the supervisory review process that requires banks to assess their own capital adequacy and for regulators to evaluate the banks' risk management and internal capital assessment. It complements Pillar 1's minimum capital requirements and Pillar 3's market-disclosure framework.
[pillar_3] Pillar 3 - Market Discipline -- Pillar 3 - Market Discipline requires banks to regularly disclose comprehensive data on their risk profile, capital adequacy, and governance practices, enabling market participants and regulators to assess financial health. This transparency complements the quantitative standards of Pillar 1 and the supervisory review process of Pillar 2, fostering sound risk management through public scrutiny.
