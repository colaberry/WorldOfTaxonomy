[ch_i] Chapter I - General Provisions -- Chapter I of SOLAS 1974 establishes the convention's purpose, scope and definitions, outlines the obligations of contracting parties, and sets out the procedures for amendment and entry into force, forming the legal foundation for maritime safety standards.
[ch_ii_1] Chapter II-1 - Construction (Subdivision and Stability) -- Chapter II-1 of the 1974 SOLAS Convention sets mandatory requirements for a ship's structural design, subdivision and stability to ensure it remains afloat and upright after damage. It defines standards for watertight bulkheads, damage stability calculations, and load line compliance, aiming to protect lives by limiting flooding and preserving vessel integrity under adverse conditions.
[ch_ii_2] Chapter II-2 - Fire Protection, Detection and Extinction -- Chapter II-2 of SOLAS 1974 establishes mandatory standards for fire protection, detection and extinction on board ships, detailing construction requirements, fire-resistant materials, and the design, testing and maintenance of fixed and portable fire-fighting systems. It also prescribes the installation of alarm and detection equipment, fire pumps, extinguishers, sprinkler and foam systems, and outlines crew training and emergency procedures to minimise fire hazards at sea.
[ch_iii] Chapter III - Life-Saving Appliances -- Chapter III of the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea sets standards for life-saving appliances on ships, specifying requirements for lifeboats, life-rafts, personal flotation devices and launch equipment, as well as procedures for inspection, maintenance and crew training to ensure effective emergency evacuation.
[ch_iv] Chapter IV - Radiocommunications -- Chapter IV of SOLAS 1974 establishes the mandatory standards for maritime radiocommunications, specifying the types of radio equipment, satellite systems and the Global Maritime Distress and Safety System that vessels must carry, as well as the training and procedures required to ensure reliable distress, safety and operational communications at sea.
[ch_ix] Chapter IX - Management for Safe Operation (ISM Code) -- Chapter IX of SOLAS mandates the International Safety Management (ISM) Code, which sets standards for the safe operation of ships and pollution prevention through a formal safety management system, documented procedures, and regular audits, ensuring that ship owners and operators maintain consistent, documented safety practices.
[ch_v] Chapter V - Safety of Navigation -- Chapter V of the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS 1974) establishes mandatory standards for safe navigation, covering ship-borne equipment, chart requirements, traffic separation schemes and navigation procedures intended to prevent collisions, groundings and other navigational hazards, and it outlines the responsibilities of vessel masters and crews to comply with international rules.
[ch_vi] Chapter VI - Carriage of Cargoes -- Chapter VI of the 1974 SOLAS Convention establishes uniform requirements for the safe stowage, securing and handling of cargoes on board ships. It defines the duties of ship owners and operators, prescribes construction and maintenance standards for cargo-handling gear, and mandates procedures to prevent shifting, damage or loss that could endanger life at sea.
[ch_vii] Chapter VII - Carriage of Dangerous Goods -- Chapter VII of the 1974 International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea establishes mandatory rules for the safe carriage of dangerous goods by sea, covering classification, packaging, labeling, documentation, stowage, segregation and ship equipment, and requires vessels to comply with approved standards and inspections to protect crew, passengers and the environment.
[ch_xi_1] Chapter XI-1 - Special Measures to Enhance Maritime Safety -- Chapter XI-1 of the 1974 International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea sets out special measures to improve maritime safety, covering safety-management systems, ship-board emergency plans, and requirements for training, drills and equipment to reduce accidents and protect lives at sea.
[ch_xi_2] Chapter XI-2 - Special Measures to Enhance Maritime Security (ISPS Code) -- Chapter XI-2 of the 1974 SOLAS Convention establishes the International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code, outlining mandatory security measures for ships and port facilities. It requires the development of security plans, the designation of ship and port security officers, and the implementation of compliance, verification and certification procedures to protect maritime transport from threats.
[ch_xii] Chapter XII - Additional Safety Measures for Bulk Carriers -- Chapter XII of the SOLAS 1974 Convention sets out additional safety measures specifically for bulk carriers, covering requirements for hull strength, subdivision, cargo securing, stability, fire-protection systems, ventilation and damage control to reduce the risk of loss of stability, fire or flooding during operation. It mandates regular inspections, certification and crew training to ensure compliance with these heightened standards.
[ch_xiv] Chapter XIV - Safety Measures for Polar Code Ships -- Chapter XIV of the 1974 SOLAS Convention sets out mandatory safety measures for vessels operating in polar regions, incorporating the Polar Code requirements. It specifies design and construction standards for hull strength, ice-class certification, specialized navigation and communication equipment, crew training for ice navigation, and emergency preparedness including life-saving appliances and response plans tailored to extreme cold and remote conditions.
[ism_1] ISM Code - Safety management objectives -- The ISM Code establishes a shipboard safety management system with objectives to provide safe operation of vessels and protect the marine environment, to define responsibilities, to promote continuous improvement, and to ensure compliance with international regulations. It requires each ship and company to develop documented procedures, conduct internal audits, and undergo certification by recognized organizations.
[ism_2] ISM Code - Safety and environmental protection policy -- The ISM Code obliges ship owners and operators to create a written safety and environmental protection policy that declares their commitment to safe operation, compliance with all applicable regulations, and continuous improvement of safety performance. This policy must be integrated into the vessel's Safety Management System, communicated to the crew, and reviewed periodically.
[ism_3] ISM Code - Company responsibilities -- The ism_3 entry outlines the responsibilities of ship owners and operators under the ISM Code, requiring them to develop, implement and maintain a Safety Management System that ensures safe operation and environmental protection, appoint a designated person ashore, allocate necessary resources and conduct regular reviews and audits.
[ism_4] ISM Code - Designated Person Ashore -- The Designated Person Ashore (DPA) is a senior company official appointed under the ISM Code to ensure that a ship's safety management system is implemented and that safe and environmentally responsible operation is maintained. The DPA serves as the primary link between shipboard crew and shore management, receives reports of non-conformities, and takes corrective action when required.
[isps_1] ISPS Code - Ship security assessment -- The ISPS Code ship security assessment, required by SOLAS 1974, evaluates a vessel's security measures, identifies vulnerabilities, and determines compliance with international maritime security standards. Conducted by the ship's master or designated security officer, it forms the basis for the ship security plan and the issuance of a security certificate.
[isps_2] ISPS Code - Ship security plan -- The ISPS Code ship security plan (entry code isps_2) is a mandatory component of SOLAS 1974 that requires vessel owners to develop and maintain documented security measures. It specifies risk assessments, contingency procedures, crew duties and safeguards to protect ships from terrorism, piracy and other security threats while at sea and in port.
[isps_3] ISPS Code - Port facility security plan -- The ISPS Code mandates that each port facility prepare a security plan that details measures to prevent terrorism and unlawful interference, assigns responsibilities, conducts risk assessments, controls access, and defines emergency response procedures. The plan must be approved by the vessel's flag state, coordinated with national authorities, and regularly reviewed to ensure continued compliance with SOLAS 1974.
[isps_4] ISPS Code - Security levels -- The ISPS Code sets three security levels for ships and ports: Level 1 applies when there is no specific threat or risk, Level 2 is used when a heightened risk of terrorism or piracy is identified, and Level 3 is activated during an imminent or actual security incident, requiring the most stringent protective measures.
