[accidental_release] Accidental Release Prevention (Sec 112(r)) -- Under the Clean Air Act, Section 112(r) requires facilities that use or store listed hazardous substances to develop and implement an accidental-release prevention plan, which includes risk-management measures, safety procedures, employee training and regular inspections to minimize releases that could endanger public health or the environment.
[aircraft_emissions] Aircraft Emission Standards (Sec 231-234) -- The entry outlines the Clean Air Act provisions governing aircraft emissions, specifically sections 231-234, which set permissible limits for pollutants from aircraft engines and establish certification, testing, and compliance requirements for manufacturers and operators to reduce the impact of aviation on air quality.
[cafe] Clean Alternative Fuels (Sec 241-250) -- The "cafe" entry under the Clean Air Act (42 USC 7401 et seq.) covers sections 241-250, which establish the Clean Alternative Fuels program. It authorizes research, development, and deployment of low-emission fuels, provides grants and tax incentives for alternative-fuel vehicles, and sets performance standards to reduce air pollution from transportation.
[fuel_standards] Fuel and Fuel Additive Standards (Sec 211) -- The Clean Air Act authorizes the EPA to set national fuel and fuel-additive standards under Section 211, establishing limits on sulfur content, aromatics, and oxygenates in gasoline, diesel and alternative fuels to reduce emissions and protect public health. Compliance is mandatory for refiners and importers, with periodic updates to reflect advancing technology and air-quality goals.
[hap_list] Hazardous Air Pollutant List (189 HAPs) (Sec 112(b)) -- The Hazardous Air Pollutant List, authorized by Section 112(b) of the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. 7401 et seq.), enumerates 189 substances identified as posing serious health or environmental risks when released into the atmosphere. The list serves as the basis for establishing source-specific emission standards and other control measures.
[hfc_phasedown] HFC Phasedown (AIM Act Amendments) -- The Clean Air Act entry "hfc_phasedown" refers to the statutory provisions that implement the American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act amendments, mandating a gradual reduction of hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) production and consumption in the United States to curb greenhouse gas emissions and promote climate-friendly alternatives.
[mact] Maximum Achievable Control Technology (MACT) (Sec 112(d)) -- Maximum Achievable Control Technology (MACT) under Section 112(d) of the Clean Air Act sets emission standards for major sources of hazardous air pollutants, requiring them to apply the most effective control methods that are technologically and economically feasible. The rule is tailored to each pollutant and source category, using performance data to define the highest level of emission reduction that can be achieved in practice.
[mobile_sources] Motor Vehicle Emission Standards (Sec 202-219) -- This entry captures the Clean Air Act provisions governing motor vehicle emission standards, specifically sections 202 through 219. It sets allowable limits for pollutants from cars, trucks, and other mobile sources, mandates testing and compliance by manufacturers, and outlines enforcement mechanisms to reduce tailpipe emissions and protect air quality.
[monitoring_reporting] Continuous Emission Monitoring (CEM) -- This entry covers requirements for continuous emission monitoring systems used to track pollutant levels from stationary sources under the Clean Air Act. It outlines performance specifications, data reporting, and compliance verification for real-time emissions data.
[naaqs] National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) (Sec 108-109) -- The entry refers to the National Ambient Air Quality Standards established under the Clean Air Act, sections 108-109, which set federally enforceable limits for key pollutants to protect public health and the environment. These standards are periodically reviewed and updated based on the latest scientific evidence.
[neshap] National Emission Standards for HAPs (NESHAP) (Sec 112) -- The Clean Air Act's Section 112 establishes National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP), mandating that sources of listed pollutants install technology that achieves the maximum degree of emission reduction practicable. These standards, known as MACT (Maximum Achievable Control Technology) limits, apply nationwide to control toxic air emissions from industrial and commercial facilities.
[nonattainment] Nonattainment Area Requirements (Sec 171-193) -- The Clean Air Act's nonattainment classification applies to geographic areas that fail to meet National Ambient Air Quality Standards. Sections 171-193 require states to develop and implement State Implementation Plans, enforce emission reductions, and meet specific deadlines until the area attains the required air quality levels.
[nox_reduction] NOx Reduction Program -- The NOx Reduction Program is a component of the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. 7401 et seq.) that establishes federal standards and incentives to lower nitrogen-oxide emissions from power plants, industrial facilities, and mobile sources, aiming to improve air quality and protect public health.
[nsps] New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) (Sec 111) -- New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) are technology-based emissions limits established under Section 111 of the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. § 7401 et seq.) for specific categories of newly constructed or modified industrial sources, ensuring that new facilities adopt the latest feasible pollution control measures.
[operating_permits] Operating Permit Requirements (Sec 501-507) -- The entry refers to the Clean Air Act's operating-permit provisions found in sections 501-507. It outlines the requirements for obtaining, issuing, and enforcing permits for major sources of air pollutants, including application procedures, public notice, compliance monitoring, and enforcement actions.
[ozone_depleting] Ozone-Depleting Substance Phaseout (Sec 601-618) -- The entry refers to the Clean Air Act provisions that mandate the phaseout of ozone-depleting substances, covering sections 601 through 618. It outlines the regulatory schedule, required reductions, and enforcement mechanisms for chemicals such as CFCs, halons, and related compounds to protect the stratospheric ozone layer.
[permit_fees] Permit Fees (Sec 502(b)(3)) -- Permit Fees under the Clean Air Act, Sec 502(b)(3), authorize the EPA to assess fees to cover the costs of reviewing, processing, and issuing air-pollution permits for major stationary sources, ensuring that permitting activities are financially supported without reliance on general funds.
[psd] Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD) (Sec 160-169B) -- The PSD program, codified in sections 160-169B of the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. 7401 et seq.), sets permitting requirements for major new or modified sources in areas that already meet National Ambient Air Quality Standards, ensuring any increase in emissions does not cause significant deterioration of air quality. This entry is indexed under the code "psd."
[residual_risk] Residual Risk Standards (Sec 112(f)) -- Residual Risk Standards under Section 112(f) of the Clean Air Act require the EPA to set additional emission limits for hazardous air pollutants when existing technology-based standards leave an unacceptable health risk. The agency must assess the remaining risk to sensitive populations and, if necessary, impose stricter controls to protect public health and the environment.
[sip] State Implementation Plans (SIPs) (Sec 110) -- State Implementation Plans (SIPs) are state-developed strategies required by Section 110 of the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. § 7401 et seq.) to demonstrate how each state will attain and maintain the National Ambient Air Quality Standards, outlining emissions controls, monitoring, and enforcement measures.
[so2_allowances] SO2 Allowance Trading Program -- The SO₂ Allowance Trading Program, established under the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. § 7401 et seq.), creates a market for sulfur dioxide emission permits to help utilities meet national caps on acid-rain precursors. Entities receive or purchase allowances that represent one ton of SO₂, and they may trade these allowances to achieve compliance cost-effectively while the overall emissions limit remains enforced.
[title_1] Title I - Air Pollution Prevention and Control -- Title I establishes a comprehensive federal framework for air pollution prevention and control, authorizing the EPA to set National Ambient Air Quality Standards, require state implementation plans, and enforce emissions limits for major sources. It also provides for citizen suits and funding for research.
[title_2] Title II - Emission Standards for Moving Sources -- Title II establishes federal emission limits for mobile sources such as cars, trucks, buses, and non-road equipment, mandating manufacturers to meet specific caps on pollutants like nitrogen oxides, carbon monoxide, hydrocarbons and particulates. The standards are periodically tightened and enforced through certification, testing and compliance programs to curb transportation-related air pollution.
[title_3] Title III - Hazardous Air Pollutants -- Title III of the Clean Air Act establishes the hazardous air pollutant program, setting standards and permitting requirements to control emissions of toxic substances from major sources and to protect public health and the environment.
[title_4] Title IV - Acid Deposition Control (Acid Rain Program) -- The Clean Air Act's Title IV authorizes the Acid Deposition Control program, commonly called the Acid Rain Program, which establishes a market-based system for reducing sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides emissions from power plants. It sets emissions caps, allocates emission allowances, and requires monitoring, reporting, and compliance verification to achieve national goals for acid rain mitigation.
[title_5] Title V - Permits -- Title V of the Clean Air Act creates a comprehensive permitting program for major stationary sources of air pollutants, requiring each source to obtain an operating permit that consolidates all applicable federal, state and local requirements. The permits include emission limits, monitoring, recordkeeping, reporting and compliance schedules, and are subject to public notice, comment and periodic renewal.
[title_6] Title VI - Stratospheric Ozone Protection -- Title VI of the Clean Air Act creates the U.S. program to protect the stratospheric ozone layer by phasing out the production and use of ozone-depleting substances, establishing timelines, reporting requirements and enforcement mechanisms.
[visibility] Visibility Protection (Regional Haze) (Sec 169A-169B) -- The visibility entry under the Clean Air Act concerns regional haze, requiring states to develop plans that improve sightlines in protected Class I federal areas. It mandates monitoring, emission reductions and periodic reporting to restore and preserve scenic vistas.
