[agency_duties] Consumer Reporting Agency Duties -- The Fair Credit Reporting Act requires consumer reporting agencies to maintain accurate, complete, and up-to-date information, protect data against unauthorized access, and provide consumers with the ability to review and dispute their credit files. Agencies must also certify compliance with the Act's standards when furnishing reports.
[consumer_reports] Consumer Reports and Permissible Purposes -- The entry explains that under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 USC 1681 et seq.) consumer reports-including credit history, employment, insurance and other personal data-may be obtained only for permissible purposes such as credit transactions, employment decisions, insurance underwriting, government benefits, or other legally authorized uses.
[consumer_rights] Consumer Rights -- The consumer_rights entry under the Fair Credit Reporting Act outlines the protections afforded to individuals regarding their personal credit information. It details the right to access, correct and dispute inaccurate data, obtain a free annual credit report, and be notified of adverse actions based on credit reports. It also specifies procedures for requesting disclosures and limiting the sharing of credit information.
[enforcement] Enforcement and Penalties -- The Enforcement and Penalties entry details how the Fair Credit Reporting Act is upheld, specifying the authorities responsible for overseeing compliance, the investigative and litigation processes, and the civil and criminal sanctions imposed for violations, including fines, damages, and possible imprisonment for willful non-compliance.
[furnisher_duties] Furnisher Responsibilities -- Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, furnishers of credit information must ensure the accuracy of data they provide, investigate consumer disputes, and promptly correct any errors. They may supply consumer information only when permissible, must protect the data's security, and are required to respond to consumer requests within the statutory time limits.
[purpose] Findings, Purpose, and Definitions -- The entry "purpose" under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 USC 1681 et seq.) outlines the Act's findings, its overarching objectives, and key definitions. It explains that the law aims to promote accuracy, fairness, and privacy in consumer credit information, and it defines terms such as consumer report, credit bureau, and user to guide implementation and enforcement.
[sec_602] Sec 602 - Congressional Findings and Purpose -- Section 602 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act records Congress's findings and purpose, stating that accurate credit information is essential for consumer protection, economic stability, and fair lending practices. It underscores the need for reliable reporting to prevent fraud, promote competition, and safeguard the public's interest in a transparent credit system.
[sec_603] Sec 603 - Definitions (Consumer Report, CRA, etc.) -- Section 603 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act provides the core definitions used throughout the law, including terms such as consumer report, consumer reporting agency, user of a consumer report, and other related concepts. These definitions set the scope of the Act and dictate the responsibilities of entities that collect, compile, or use consumer credit information.
[sec_604] Sec 604 - Permissible Purposes of Consumer Reports -- Section 604 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act outlines the specific circumstances in which a consumer report may be obtained, limiting access to parties with a lawful purpose such as credit transactions, employment decisions, insurance underwriting, or government functions, and requiring users to certify the permissible purpose before disclosure.
[sec_604b] Sec 604(b) - Use by Employers -- Section 604(b) of the Fair Credit Reporting Act sets the rules for employers who obtain consumer reports for employment decisions. It requires a legitimate, written purpose, that the employer certify the report will be used only for a permissible employment-related reason, and that they notify the applicant of any adverse action based on the report.
[sec_605] Sec 605 - Requirements on Information in Reports -- Section 605 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act mandates that consumer reporting agencies furnish comprehensive and accurate information in credit reports, limit the inclusion of outdated or irrelevant data, and establish procedures for consumers to dispute and correct errors, ensuring the integrity and reliability of reported credit histories.
[sec_605a] Sec 605A - Identity Theft Prevention - Fraud Alerts -- Section 605A of the Fair Credit Reporting Act establishes procedures for fraud alerts to help prevent identity theft. It allows a consumer who suspects misuse of personal information to request a temporary alert on their credit file, requiring credit reporting agencies to notify any future users of the report and to provide the consumer with a free copy of the report after an alert is placed. This alert remains in effect for 90 days, or longer if an extended alert is approved.
[sec_605b] Sec 605B - Block of Information from Identity Theft -- Section 605B of the Fair Credit Reporting Act requires credit reporting agencies to block the reporting of information that results from identity theft. Upon receiving a valid identity-theft report, agencies must place a block on the fraudulent data, preventing it from appearing in a consumer's credit file.
[sec_607] Sec 607 - Compliance Procedures -- Section 607 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act outlines the compliance procedures that consumer reporting agencies must follow, requiring them to maintain confidentiality, provide consumer access to reports, and implement internal controls. It also mandates that agencies correct inaccurate information and respond promptly to consumer disputes.
[sec_609] Sec 609 - Disclosures to Consumers -- Section 609 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act grants consumers the right to obtain a full disclosure of their credit file from a consumer reporting agency, including all information contained in the report, the sources of any adverse entries, and the procedures used to compile the file. It also requires the agency to provide the consumer with any disclosures required by law regarding the use and restrictions on the report.
[sec_610] Sec 610 - Conditions for Disclosure to Consumers -- Section 610 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act sets the rules for when consumer reporting agencies may reveal a consumer's credit information. It requires that disclosures be made only to a person with a permissible purpose, such as a lender or employer, and that the consumer be notified of the source and purpose of the report. The section also mandates procedures for obtaining the consumer's written consent when required.
[sec_611] Sec 611 - Procedure for Disputed Information -- Section 611 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act sets out the procedure for consumers to dispute inaccurate information on their credit reports. It obligates consumer reporting agencies to investigate the dispute within 30 days, correct or delete any erroneous data, and provide the consumer with a written summary of the investigation's findings.
[sec_612] Sec 612 - Charges for Disclosures -- Section 612 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act sets the rules for fees that consumer reporting agencies may charge when they disclose credit reports or related information. It allows reasonable, disclosed charges but bars excessive, discriminatory, or hidden fees, ensuring consumers know the cost of any requested disclosure.
[sec_615] Sec 615 - Requirements on Users Taking Adverse Actions -- Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, users who take adverse actions based on consumer reports must give the consumer written notice, disclose the source of the report, and allow the consumer a chance to dispute any inaccurate information. The rule also requires the user to retain documentation of the decision and the report for a specified period.
[sec_616] Sec 616 - Civil Liability for Willful Noncompliance -- Section 616 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act imposes civil liability on organizations that willfully fail to comply with the Act's requirements. Violators may be subject to actual damages, punitive damages, and attorney's fees, and the statute permits private individuals and the Federal Trade Commission to sue for enforcement.
[sec_617] Sec 617 - Civil Liability for Negligent Noncompliance -- Section 617 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act establishes civil liability for parties that negligently fail to comply with the Act's requirements, permitting affected individuals to sue for actual damages, statutory damages, punitive damages and reasonable attorney fees, and authorizing courts to award injunctive relief.
[sec_621] Sec 621 - Administrative Enforcement (CFPB/FTC) -- Section 621 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act empowers the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and the Federal Trade Commission to enforce the law, authorizing investigations, issuance of subpoenas, and assessment of civil penalties against entities that violate FCRA provisions.
[sec_623] Sec 623 - Responsibilities of Furnishers of Information -- Section 623 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act outlines the duties of entities that provide consumer information to credit reporting agencies. Furnishers must ensure data accuracy, correct errors promptly, follow dispute-resolution procedures, and limit disclosures to permitted purposes.
[sec_624] Sec 624 - Affiliate Sharing Notices -- Section 624 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act mandates that consumer reporting agencies give consumers a clear notice when their credit information is shared with affiliates of the reporting company. The notice must explain the nature of the sharing, the types of affiliates involved, and the consumer's right to opt out, ensuring transparency and consumer control over their data.
[sec_625] Sec 625 - Relation to State Laws -- Section 625 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act governs the interaction between the federal law and state statutes, allowing states to adopt or enforce provisions that are consistent with or provide greater consumer protection while prohibiting state laws that conflict with the Act's requirements, thereby clarifying the scope of preemption.
