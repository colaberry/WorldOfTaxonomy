[gp_1] GP 1 - States must protect against human rights abuse -- GP 1 outlines the state duty to protect human rights by preventing, investigating and remedying abuses associated with business activities. It calls on states to adopt appropriate policies, laws and enforcement mechanisms and to ensure victims have effective access to justice.
[gp_10] GP 10 - Multilateral institutions -- GP 10 outlines the duties of multilateral institutions-including the United Nations, World Bank, and regional development banks-to respect human rights, avoid causing adverse impacts through their policies and programs, and to offer guidance and remedies for businesses operating under their auspices. It urges these bodies to embed the UN Guiding Principles in their governance, monitor compliance, and support capacity-building so enterprises can meet human-rights standards.
[gp_11] GP 11 - Corporate responsibility to respect human rights -- GP 11 states that businesses have the responsibility to respect human rights by avoiding actions that cause or contribute to human rights abuses, and by addressing any adverse impacts they are linked to through appropriate policies, due diligence processes, and effective remediation mechanisms.
[gp_13] GP 13 - Responsibility to avoid causing or contributing to adverse impacts -- GP 13 details the corporate responsibility to avoid causing or contributing to adverse human-rights impacts, requiring businesses to identify, assess and mitigate risks throughout their operations and value chains. It calls for proactive due-diligence and preventive measures consistent with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights.
[gp_15] GP 15 - Policy commitment -- GP 15 outlines the requirement for businesses to adopt a formal policy committing to respect human rights, defining the organization's scope, principles and responsibilities. The policy must be publicly communicated, endorsed by senior leadership, and integrated into corporate governance and internal processes.
[gp_17] GP 17 - Human rights due diligence -- GP 17 outlines the requirement for businesses to conduct human-rights due diligence, meaning they must identify, prevent, mitigate and account for actual or potential adverse human-rights impacts linked to their operations, products, services or business relationships, and to integrate and monitor corrective actions.
[gp_18] GP 18 - Assessing actual and potential human rights impacts -- GP 18 calls for businesses to conduct a thorough assessment of both existing and foreseeable human rights impacts linked to their operations, products and relationships, using risk-based analysis and stakeholder input. The assessment should be ongoing, informing mitigation strategies and reporting.
[gp_2] GP 2 - States should set clear expectations for businesses -- Principle 2 requires governments to articulate specific duties for companies to respect human rights, offering clear guidance on due-diligence, reporting and remediation. It calls for policies, regulations or standards that define expectations and ensure business accountability throughout their operations.
[gp_20] GP 20 - Tracking effectiveness of responses -- GP 20 addresses the need to monitor and assess the effectiveness of corporate and governmental responses to identified human rights impacts. It calls for systematic tracking of outcomes, using indicators and regular reporting to determine whether remediation actions achieve their intended results and to guide continuous improvement.
[gp_21] GP 21 - Communication -- GP 21 addresses the need for businesses to communicate clearly and transparently about their human-rights policies, practices and performance. It calls for regular, accessible reporting to stakeholders, including disclosures of due-diligence processes and outcomes, and for establishing channels that allow affected parties to raise concerns and receive timely information. Effective communication underpins accountability and enables dialogue between enterprises, victims, civil society and governments.
[gp_22] GP 22 - Remediation -- GP 22 focuses on remedial mechanisms when businesses cause or contribute to human rights abuses, requiring them to provide effective grievance-handling, cooperate with victims, and support access to remedy through judicial or non-judicial means.
[gp_25] GP 25 - State-based judicial mechanisms -- GP 25 covers state-based judicial mechanisms such as national courts and tribunals that can adjudicate claims of human rights abuses connected to business activities, providing a formal avenue for victims to seek redress, enforce legal duties and hold corporations accountable under domestic law.
[gp_26] GP 26 - State-based non-judicial mechanisms -- GP 26 refers to the state-based non-judicial mechanisms outlined in the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, which include national grievance-handling bodies, ombudsman offices, and other public institutions that receive, investigate, and resolve complaints against businesses without resorting to courts. These mechanisms are intended to provide accessible, transparent, and effective remedies, complementing judicial avenues and contributing to the overall corporate accountability framework.
[gp_27] GP 27 - Non-State-based mechanisms -- GP 27 covers non-state-based mechanisms for addressing business-related human rights impacts, outlining the role of NGOs, industry grievance procedures and other voluntary processes that provide access to remedy when state avenues are unavailable or insufficient.
[gp_28] GP 28 - Operational-level grievance mechanisms -- GP 28 outlines how businesses should establish grievance mechanisms at the operational level that give affected people accessible, timely and transparent ways to raise concerns about human-rights impacts. It stresses independence, confidentiality and the provision of appropriate remedies, and requires these mechanisms to be integrated into the company's overall human-rights management system.
[gp_3] GP 3 - Regulatory and policy functions -- GP 3 outlines the state's regulatory and policy responsibilities to protect human rights, requiring the adoption, implementation and enforcement of laws, standards and policies that prevent businesses from causing or contributing to rights abuses. It calls for monitoring mechanisms, oversight and effective remedies to ensure the regulatory framework is clear, accessible and enforced.
[gp_31] GP 31 - Effectiveness criteria for non-judicial mechanisms -- GP 31 sets out the key criteria for judging the effectiveness of non-judicial grievance mechanisms under the UN Guiding Principles, covering aspects such as legitimacy, accessibility, predictability, equity, rights-based outcomes, and transparency, and provides guidance on how entities can assess and improve these mechanisms to ensure they adequately address business-related human rights impacts.
[gp_4] GP 4 - State-business nexus -- GP 4 examines the state-business nexus within the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, detailing how governments must create and enforce policies that prevent corporate human rights violations, promote responsible business conduct, and provide effective remedy mechanisms. It outlines the duties of the state to regulate, monitor, and hold businesses accountable while fostering an environment that respects human rights through legislation, oversight, and collaborative engagement with the private sector.
[gp_7] GP 7 - Conflict-affected areas -- GP 7 addresses the responsibilities of businesses operating in conflict-affected areas, urging them to conduct thorough human-rights due diligence, avoid contributing to violence, and mitigate any adverse impacts on local populations. It emphasizes the need for transparent policies, risk-based mitigation measures, and coordination with authorities and civil-society actors to promote peace and protect rights.
[pillar_1] Pillar I - State Duty to Protect Human Rights -- Pillar I outlines the state's obligation to protect human rights by preventing, investigating and remedying abuses caused by third parties, including corporations, through appropriate laws, policies and regulations. It requires governments to conduct risk-based due diligence, enforce standards and provide effective grievance mechanisms.
[pillar_2] Pillar II - Corporate Responsibility to Respect -- The Pillar II of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights sets out the corporate responsibility to respect human rights, requiring companies to carry out due-diligence, identify, prevent, mitigate and account for adverse impacts, and to embed these findings into policies and operational processes.
[pillar_3] Pillar III - Access to Remedy -- Pillar III emphasizes the right of individuals harmed by business activities to obtain effective remedies. It urges states to ensure accessible legal channels, requires companies to set up grievance mechanisms, and calls for collaboration to deliver timely, transparent and just outcomes.
