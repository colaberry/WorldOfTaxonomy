[assurance] Assurance and Audit -- The assurance and audit entry specifies that sustainability disclosures under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive must be independently verified, providing users with confidence in the data's reliability and comparability. Companies are required to obtain either limited or reasonable assurance from accredited auditors, following defined standards for scope, methodology, and reporting timelines.
[digital] Digital Tagging and Access -- The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (EU) 2022/2464 mandates that disclosed sustainability data be digitally tagged to enable machine-readable extraction and cross-border comparability. The digital tagging and access requirement ensures that information is structured according to a standardized taxonomy, facilitating automated analysis, public access and integration with digital reporting platforms.
[double_mat] Double Materiality Assessment -- The Double Materiality Assessment required by the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (2022/2464) evaluates a company's impact on the environment and society as well as how sustainability issues affect its financial performance, ensuring disclosures reflect both dimensions.
[esrs_1] ESRS 1 - General Requirements -- ESRS 1 outlines the overarching principles and scope for sustainability reporting under the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, defining materiality, double-materiality and the structure of disclosures. It sets reporting boundaries, stakeholder-engagement requirements and the need for balanced, comparable and reliable information, establishing the basic framework that all subsequent ESRS standards build upon.
[esrs_2] ESRS 2 - General Disclosures -- ESRS 2 establishes the general disclosure requirements for entities reporting under the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, outlining the principles, governance, strategy, materiality assessment and data quality expectations that ensure consistent, comparable sustainability information across organisations.
[esrs_e1] ESRS E1 - Climate Change -- ESRS E1 under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive requires entities to disclose how climate change affects their business and how they impact the climate. It covers governance, strategy, risk management, targets and performance metrics, transition plans and resilience to physical climate risks, ensuring transparent reporting on mitigation and adaptation efforts.
[esrs_e2] ESRS E2 - Pollution -- ESRS E2 - Pollution establishes the EU reporting requirements for companies to disclose information on pollution from their operations, products and services, covering emissions to air, water, soil and waste as well as related management policies, targets and performance. It enables stakeholders to evaluate environmental impacts and the effectiveness of mitigation measures.
[esrs_e3] ESRS E3 - Water and Marine Resources -- ESRS E3 requires entities to disclose information on their use of water and impacts on marine resources, including consumption, discharge, and pollution, as well as the related risks and opportunities. It aims to show how companies manage water-related dependencies, protect aquatic ecosystems, and comply with the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive.
[esrs_e4] ESRS E4 - Biodiversity and Ecosystems -- The ESRS E4 standard under the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive requires companies to disclose how their activities affect biodiversity and ecosystems, including impacts on species, habitats, protected areas and ecosystem services. It also demands information on the entity's dependencies, risks, targets and governance measures related to biodiversity loss and ecosystem degradation.
[esrs_e5] ESRS E5 - Resource Use and Circular Economy -- ESRS E5 is the European Sustainability Reporting Standard that implements the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive by requiring entities to disclose information on their use of natural resources, material efficiency, waste generation and management, and circular-economy practices, showing how these factors affect environmental performance and stakeholder decision-making.
[esrs_g1] ESRS G1 - Business Conduct -- ESRS G1 sets out the EU's sustainability reporting requirements for business conduct, requiring entities to disclose policies, due-diligence processes and outcomes related to ethics, anti-corruption, fair competition and respect for human rights. It aims to provide stakeholders with transparent information on how companies manage conduct risks and promote responsible behaviour.
[esrs_s1] ESRS S1 - Own Workforce -- ESRS S1 is the European Sustainability Reporting Standard that requires entities to disclose information about their own workforce, including employment numbers, labour practices, health and safety, diversity, training and development, and employee engagement. The standard aims to provide transparent data on how companies manage and support the people who work for them.
[esrs_s2] ESRS S2 - Workers in the Value Chain -- ESRS S2 requires companies to disclose how they manage and respect workers' rights throughout their value chain, including labour conditions, health and safety, and fair remuneration. It mandates reporting on relevant policies, due-diligence processes, outcomes and any targets set for improving supply-chain labour practices.
[esrs_s3] ESRS S3 - Affected Communities -- The ESRS S3 standard, part of the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive 2022/2464, requires companies to disclose the impacts of their activities on local communities, including social, economic and environmental effects. It guides firms to report on community engagement, risk mitigation, benefit-sharing and the resilience of affected populations, enabling stakeholders to assess how business operations influence community well-being.
[esrs_s4] ESRS S4 - Consumers and End-Users -- ESRS S4 requires companies subject to the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive to disclose how their products and services affect consumers and end-users, including issues such as product safety, data privacy, accessibility, consumer welfare and responsible marketing, and to outline measures taken to mitigate adverse impacts.
[large_co] Large Companies (from FY 2024) -- The "large_co" entry designates large companies that fall under the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (2022/2464) and must comply from fiscal year 2024. It applies to businesses that meet the directive's size thresholds for mandatory environmental, social and governance reporting, such as turnover, balance-sheet total or employee count.

[listed_sme] Listed SMEs (from FY 2026) -- Listed SMEs are companies that are small or medium-sized enterprises but have securities admitted to a regulated market in the EU. Under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive they will be required to produce sustainability reports beginning in fiscal year 2026, applying the same basic reporting standards as larger listed firms while benefiting from proportionate disclosure requirements.
[management_report] Sustainability Statement in Management Report -- The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (EU) 2022/2464 requires companies to embed a sustainability statement within their management report, presenting material environmental, social and governance information in a concise, integrated format. The statement must cover the entity's sustainability strategy, targets, performance outcomes, risk management and the impact of its activities on the transition to a climate-neutral economy.
[non_eu] Non-EU Companies (from FY 2028) -- The entry notes that, beginning with fiscal year 2028, companies based outside the European Union will fall under the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (2022/2464). These non-EU firms will need to meet the same sustainability disclosure requirements as EU companies, aligning their reporting with the CSRD standards.
[reporting] Reporting Requirements -- The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (EU) 2022/2464 mandates that qualifying companies prepare annual sustainability reports covering environmental, social and governance matters, applying the double-materiality principle and standardized European sustainability reporting standards. Reports must be audited, published alongside financial statements, and made available in a digital, machine-readable format to facilitate comparability and accessibility across the EU.
[scope] Scope and Phased Application -- The scope entry sets out which companies and groups must comply with the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, specifying size, turnover and public-interest thresholds, and delineates the phased rollout schedule that brings larger entities into reporting obligations first, followed by medium-sized firms in later years.

The data must cover emissions, resource use, human-rights risks and other material sustainability factors across the entire value chain.
