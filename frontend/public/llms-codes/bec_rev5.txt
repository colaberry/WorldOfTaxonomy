[1] Food and beverages -- The Food and beverages category groups all economic activities related to the production, processing, distribution and retail of edible goods and drinks, encompassing agriculture, livestock, food manufacturing, beverage brewing and bottling, as well as wholesale and retail trade that supplies households and institutions.
[1.1] Primary food and beverages -- The 1.1 Primary food and beverages category groups together economic activities that produce, process, or distribute raw agricultural products and basic drink items, such as crops, livestock, dairy, meat, grains, fruits, vegetables, water, and non-alcoholic beverages. It excludes downstream manufacturing of processed foods and alcoholic drinks.
[1.1.1] Primary food and beverages - mainly for industry -- The 1.1.1 category covers the extraction and initial processing of raw foodstuffs and drink ingredients that are destined primarily for further industrial use rather than direct consumer sales. It includes activities such as growing crops, raising livestock, fishing, and basic treatments like cleaning, grading, or drying that prepare these products for manufacturing into processed foods, beverages, or other downstream goods.
[1.1.2] Primary food and beverages - mainly for household consumption -- Code 1.1.2 in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5 groups together the production of basic foodstuffs and drinks that are primarily intended for direct household consumption, such as staple grains, dairy, meat, fruits, vegetables and non-alcoholic beverages, rather than processed or commercial-grade products.
[1.2] Processed food and beverages -- The 1.2 code in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, covers businesses that transform raw agricultural inputs into prepared food and drink products. It includes activities such as canning, baking, bottling, freezing, and other processing steps that create ready-to-eat or ready-to-drink items for consumers.
[1.2.1] Processed food and beverages - mainly for industry -- The 1.2.1 category groups establishments that manufacture processed foods and beverages primarily for industrial customers rather than direct consumer sales. It includes bulk production of food ingredients, intermediate food products, and beverage concentrates used by other manufacturers in further processing or as components in other goods. This segment focuses on large-scale, B2B output rather than retail-oriented food and drink production.

[2] Industrial supplies not elsewhere specified -- Code 2 in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, groups industrial supplies that do not fit into any other specific category, covering items such as lubricants, adhesives, safety gear, cleaning agents and other miscellaneous components used in manufacturing and production processes.
[2.1] Primary industrial supplies -- The 2.1 Primary industrial supplies category groups goods that are directly used in manufacturing and construction, such as raw materials, components, and auxiliary equipment. It includes items like steel, chemicals, machinery parts, and basic tools that facilitate production processes but are not final consumer products. This classification helps differentiate essential inputs from finished goods in economic analysis.
[2.2] Processed industrial supplies -- Code 2.2 refers to processed industrial supplies, which are manufactured or refined materials used as inputs in production processes. It includes chemicals, metal alloys, plastics, electronic components and other intermediate goods that have been transformed from raw commodities for industrial use.
[3] Fuels and lubricants -- Code 3 groups all economic activities involving the extraction, processing, distribution and use of energy substances such as gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, natural gas, coal, bio-fuels and related lubricating oils. It includes operations from mining and refining to wholesale, retail and end-use consumption for transportation, industrial, commercial and residential purposes.
[3.1] Primary fuels and lubricants -- The Broad Economic Categories Revision 5 code 3.1 groups together the consumption of primary energy sources such as coal, petroleum, natural gas and other raw fuels, together with the lubricants required for machinery and transport. It is used to capture the direct input of these energy products in industrial, commercial and residential economic activity.
[3.2] Processed fuels and lubricants -- The 3.2 category groups refined petroleum products, synthetic fuels and related lubricants that have been processed for use as energy sources or friction-reducing agents. It covers gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, fuel oil, heating oil, as well as motor oils, greases and specialty lubricants, and is used to track consumption and trade in broad economic statistics.

[3.2.2] Other processed fuels and lubricants -- Code 3.2.2 in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, groups together refined petroleum products that are not listed in other categories, such as gasoline, diesel, kerosene, and various lubricating oils. These items are considered processed fuels and lubricants used for transport, industry, and machinery operation.
[4] Capital goods (except transport equipment) and parts and accessories -- Category 4 comprises manufactured capital goods used in production processes, such as machinery, equipment, and industrial components, but excludes transport equipment. It also includes the parts and accessories needed to maintain or upgrade those capital goods.
[4.1] Capital goods (except transport equipment) -- The 4.1 category groups capital goods that are used to produce other goods and services, such as machinery, equipment, and industrial structures, but it specifically excludes transport equipment like vehicles and aircraft. These items are typically long-lasting, durable, and essential for manufacturing, construction, and other productive processes.
[4.2] Parts and accessories for capital goods -- The 4.2 category groups together components, spare parts and accessories that support, repair or upgrade capital goods such as machinery, equipment, vehicles and other durable assets. It includes both consumable and non-consumable items used to keep these assets functional or to improve their performance.
[5] Transport equipment and parts and accessories -- This category groups economic activities involved in producing motor vehicles, aircraft, ships, rail vehicles and their components, plus related accessories such as tires, batteries and safety equipment. It also includes manufacturing, assembly, repair and refurbishment of transport equipment and parts.
[5.1] Passenger motor cars -- Code 5.1 in the Broad Economic Categories Revision 5 groups passenger motor cars, encompassing the manufacture, sale and operation of automobiles intended mainly for transporting people. It excludes commercial vehicles such as trucks, buses and specialized transport units.
[5.2] Other transport equipment -- The 5.2 category groups together transport assets that are not classified as motor vehicles, such as aircraft, ships, railway rolling stock, and specialized equipment used for moving goods or people. It encompasses both commercial and private transport devices that fall outside the primary road-vehicle categories.
[5.2.1] Industrial transport equipment -- The 5.2.1 category covers machinery and vehicles that move goods or passengers within industrial operations, including trucks, forklifts, conveyors, railway cars and related handling equipment. It is part of the transport sector in the Broad Economic Categories system, grouping production and services tied to industrial logistics and material handling.
[5.2.2] Non-industrial transport equipment -- The 5.2.2 entry groups all transport equipment that is used outside of industrial production, such as passenger cars, trucks, buses, motorcycles, bicycles and related accessories. It excludes machinery intended for manufacturing, construction or other industrial processes. This category tracks the economic activity of providing personal and commercial mobility services.
[5.3] Parts and accessories for transport equipment -- The 5.3 category covers components and supplementary items that support the operation, repair, or improvement of transport equipment such as vehicles, aircraft, ships and related machinery. It includes spare parts, replacement pieces and accessories like tires, batteries, lighting and navigation equipment.
[6] Consumer goods not elsewhere specified -- Code 6 in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, groups consumer goods that are not covered by any other specific category. It captures miscellaneous durable and nondurable household items, ranging from small appliances to personal accessories, that are purchased by individuals for personal use and are not classified elsewhere.
[6.1] Durable consumer goods -- The 6.1 category of the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, groups durable consumer goods-items such as appliances, electronics, furniture and automobiles that are designed to be used repeatedly over several years. These products typically involve higher purchase costs, longer lifespans, and are accounted for separately from non-durable household items.
[6.2] Semi-durable consumer goods -- Code 6.2 in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, groups semi-durable consumer goods-items that last longer than a year but are not expected to endure for many years, such as clothing, footwear, small appliances, and household textiles. These products sit between non-durable goods like food and fully durable goods like automobiles.
[6.3] Non-durable consumer goods -- The 6.3 category of the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5, groups together non-durable consumer goods-items that are used up or replaced quickly, such as food, beverages, clothing, toiletries, and other household consumables. These products are distinguished from durable goods by their short lifespan and frequent purchase cycles.
[7] Goods not elsewhere specified -- Code 7 in the Broad Economic Categories, Revision 5 groups together all goods that cannot be assigned to any of the more specific categories, serving as a catch-all for miscellaneous or uncoded items. It is used in economic statistics to capture trade, production or consumption of products that are otherwise not classified, ensuring that total values remain complete even when detailed classification is absent.
