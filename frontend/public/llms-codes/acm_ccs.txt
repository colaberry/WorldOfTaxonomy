[CCS-APP] Applied Computing -- Applied Computing covers the use of computing techniques to address real-world problems across domains such as health, business, engineering, and education. It includes the development and deployment of software, algorithms, and systems that meet practical needs, often integrating interdisciplinary knowledge. The category groups research and applications that translate theory into usable technology.
[CCS-APP-001] Life and medical sciences applications -- The ACM Computing Classification System entry "Life and medical sciences applications" groups research that applies computing methods to biology, health care, and medicine, including data analysis, simulation, bioinformatics, clinical decision support, and the development of software and hardware tools for diagnostics, treatment planning, and wellness monitoring.
[CCS-APP-002] Physical sciences and engineering applications -- The ACM Computing Classification System entry CCS-APP-002 covers research that applies computing techniques to problems in the physical sciences and engineering. It includes algorithmic modeling, simulation, data analysis, and computational tools used in fields such as physics, chemistry, materials science, and mechanical or civil engineering.
[CCS-APP-003] Social and behavioral sciences applications -- The Social and behavioral sciences applications category groups research that applies computing techniques to the study of human behavior, societies, and related phenomena. It includes tools for data analysis, modeling, simulation, and visualization that support disciplines such as psychology, sociology, economics, and anthropology.
[CCS-APP-004] Law, social and behavioral sciences -- The ACM Computing Classification System's "Law, social and behavioral sciences" category groups research that applies computing methods to legal studies, social science inquiry, and behavioral analysis. It includes topics such as legal informatics, computational social science, human-centered data analysis, and the design of systems that support policy, governance, and the study of human behavior.
[CCS-APP-005] Arts and humanities applications -- The Arts and humanities applications area covers computing techniques that support the creation, analysis, preservation, and sharing of artistic and cultural works, including digital art tools, multimedia archives, and computational methods for literature, history, and other humanities research.
[CCS-APP-006] Computers in other domains -- The "Computers in other domains" category encompasses the application of computing technologies and methods to fields outside core computer science, such as biology, medicine, economics, and the arts. It highlights interdisciplinary research where computers serve as tools for analysis, simulation, visualization, and problem-solving in diverse scientific, engineering, and societal contexts.
[CCS-COMP] Computer Systems Organization -- Computer Systems Organization addresses the design, structure, and operation of computer hardware and system-level software, including processors, memory hierarchies, I/O mechanisms, and parallel architectures. It also encompasses topics such as system performance, reliability, power management, and emerging hardware technologies.
[CCS-COMP-001] Architectures - processor and memory architectures -- The entry covers the design and organization of central processing units and memory subsystems, including instruction set architectures, pipeline and superscalar techniques, cache structures, virtual memory, and the overall memory hierarchy that supports efficient data storage and retrieval. It addresses how processors and memory components interact to achieve performance, power, and scalability goals.

The area covers design, analysis, verification and implementation of such integrated hardware-software systems, including automotive, medical, industrial automation and Internet-of-Things applications.

It includes fault modeling, redundancy, detection, diagnosis, and mechanisms that ensure safe, continuous service when components fail.
[CCS-GEN] General and Reference -- The General and Reference category groups broad, cross-disciplinary topics and foundational resources that support computing research and education. It encompasses surveys, tutorials, dictionaries, standards, histories and other reference works that provide overarching context for the field.
[CCS-GEN-001] Cross-computing tools and techniques -- CCS-GEN-001 covers tools, methods and frameworks that enable computing systems to interoperate across diverse platforms, devices or environments. It includes middleware, APIs, virtualization, distributed runtimes and other techniques that facilitate seamless sharing of data, resources and functionality between heterogeneous computing resources.
[CCS-HARD] Hardware -- The Hardware category covers the design, architecture, and organization of computer components such as processors, memory, storage, and I/O devices, as well as related topics like hardware description languages and hardware-software integration.
[CCS-HARD-001] Printed circuit boards and hardware components -- It covers design, fabrication, testing, and integration of printed circuit boards and associated hardware components, focusing on physical layout, material selection, and assembly techniques, as well as reliability and performance considerations in computing systems.
[CCS-HARD-002] Integrated circuits and chip design -- Integrated circuits and chip design focuses on the conception, modeling, synthesis, layout, verification, and testing of semiconductor devices and micro-architectures. It includes circuit topology, physical design methodologies, fabrication processes, and performance optimization for chips ranging from simple logic gates to complex system-on-chip platforms. This area supports hardware engineering, computer architecture, and electronic design automation within the ACM Computing Classification System.
[CCS-HARD-003] Power and energy management (hardware) -- Power and energy management (hardware) addresses techniques for controlling, monitoring, and optimizing electrical power consumption, distribution, and conversion within computing hardware. It includes power-aware circuit design, dynamic voltage and frequency scaling, power-gating, energy-harvesting, and thermal management strategies that improve efficiency, reliability, and overall system performance.
[CCS-HARD-004] Very large scale integration design -- Very large scale integration design covers methods and tools for creating integrated circuits containing millions of transistors on a single chip. It includes architectural planning, circuit layout, physical design automation and verification techniques that enable high-density, high-performance semiconductor devices.
[CCS-HARD-005] Emerging hardware technologies -- Emerging hardware technologies covers research on novel computing substrates and devices that extend beyond conventional silicon processors, including quantum, neuromorphic, photonic, spintronic, and other unconventional architectures. The entry addresses design principles, fabrication methods, performance evaluation, and potential applications of these forward-looking hardware paradigms.
[CCS-HCI] Human-Centered Computing -- Human-centered computing studies the design, implementation, and evaluation of interactive systems that support people's activities, emphasizing usability, user experience, and the integration of social, cognitive, and contextual factors. It spans areas such as human-computer interaction, computer-supported cooperative work, user interface design, and accessibility, and draws on disciplines ranging from computer science to psychology and design.
[CCS-HCI-001] Human computer interaction (HCI) -- Human-computer interaction (HCI) is the interdisciplinary study of designing, implementing, and evaluating interactive computer systems for human use, focusing on how people perceive, learn, and act with technology. It draws from computer science, psychology, design, and ergonomics to improve usability, accessibility, and overall user experience.
[CCS-HCI-002] Interaction design -- Interaction design, a subarea of Human-Computer Interaction in the ACM Computing Classification System, concerns the creation of user-centered interfaces and interactive experiences. It emphasizes understanding user behavior, needs, and contexts to shape effective, intuitive, and aesthetically coherent digital products, balancing functionality, usability, and visual appeal.

It encompasses context-aware systems, wearable devices, pervasive networking, and interaction techniques designed for mobile platforms.
[CCS-HCI-005] Visualization -- Visualization encompasses the design, analysis, and evaluation of visual representations of data, information, and abstract concepts to support understanding, reasoning, and communication. It covers techniques for interactive and static visual displays, visual analytics, and the study of how visual encodings and human perception affect user performance and insight.
[CCS-HCI-006] Accessibility -- Accessibility refers to the study and practice of designing computing systems, software, and interfaces that can be used effectively by people with a wide range of abilities and disabilities. It includes guidelines, assistive technologies, and evaluation methods that ensure inclusive access to digital content.

[CCS-MATH] Mathematics of Computing -- CCS-MATH, Mathematics of Computing, covers the mathematical foundations that support computer science, including algorithm analysis, computational complexity, numerical methods, discrete mathematics, and formal models of computation. It links theoretical insights to practical algorithm design and performance evaluation across hardware and software systems.
[CCS-MATH-001] Continuous mathematics -- Continuous mathematics deals with mathematical concepts that involve continuously varying quantities, such as calculus, differential equations, real analysis, and related topics. It provides the theoretical foundation for modeling and solving problems where change is smooth rather than discrete, supporting many areas of computing, engineering, and scientific research.

[CCS-MATH-003] Mathematical software -- The CCS-MATH-003 entry covers software tools that support mathematical computation, modeling, and analysis. [CCS-MATH-004] Probability and statistics -- The ACM Computing Classification System's "Probability and statistics" area encompasses the mathematical foundations for modeling uncertainty, including probability theory, statistical inference, hypothesis testing, estimation, and stochastic processes, as well as their application to algorithm analysis, machine learning, performance evaluation, and data-driven computing. It addresses both theoretical development and practical techniques for extracting insights from data in computing contexts.

[CCS-SEC] Security and Privacy -- Security and privacy covers the principles, techniques, and mechanisms for protecting information and computing systems from unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration, and destruction, while ensuring that personal data is managed responsibly. It includes topics such as cryptography, authentication, access control, anonymity, privacy-preserving data mining, and legal and ethical aspects of safeguarding digital assets. This area spans both theoretical foundations and practical applications across hardware, software, and network environments.
[CCS-SEC-001] Cryptography -- Cryptography is the scientific discipline that designs and analyzes algorithms for securing data through encryption, authentication, and integrity mechanisms. It encompasses classical ciphers, modern public-key systems, cryptographic protocols, and cryptanalysis, providing the mathematical foundations for protecting information in computer systems and networks.
[CCS-SEC-002] Formal methods and theory of security -- This area covers mathematical techniques for specifying, analyzing, and verifying security properties of systems, including logical formalisms, models, and proof methods. [CCS-SEC-003] Security services -- Security services are the functional mechanisms that enforce protection goals such as confidentiality, integrity, availability, authentication, and non-repudiation. They include encryption, access control, intrusion detection, and auditing, providing the means for safeguarding data and system resources against unauthorized actions.
[CCS-SEC-004] Intrusion and anomaly detection and malware mitigation -- The entry covers techniques for detecting unauthorized system use, unusual behavior, and malicious software, encompassing signature-based, statistical, and machine-learning methods, as well as strategies for responding to and limiting the impact of attacks.
[CCS-SEC-005] Security in hardware -- Security in hardware addresses techniques and mechanisms that protect the physical components of computing systems from tampering, side-channel attacks, and other threats. It includes design of trusted hardware, secure microarchitectures, hardware encryption modules, and methods for detecting hardware faults or malicious modifications.
[CCS-SEC-006] Systems security -- Systems security encompasses the design, implementation, and evaluation of measures that protect computer systems from unauthorized access, abuse, and failure. [CCS-SOCIAL] Social and Professional Topics -- The Social and Professional Topics category of the ACM Computing Classification System addresses computing's influence on society, professional practice, and related ethical, legal and policy concerns. [CCS-SOCIAL-001] Professional topics (computing profession, computing education) -- This category includes research and discussion about the computing profession, its practice, ethics, career development, and related professional issues, as well as topics in computing education such as curricula, pedagogy, and the training of computing professionals.
[CCS-SOCIAL-002] Computing and society (impact, ethics, governance) -- This category addresses how computing technologies influence society, examining ethical issues, social impacts, and governance mechanisms. [CCS-SW] Software and Its Engineering -- The ACM Computing Classification System 2012 category CCS-SW addresses software and its engineering, covering the design, development, testing, maintenance and management of software systems. It includes topics such as programming languages, software architecture, project management and quality assurance.
[CCS-SW-001] Software creation and management -- Software creation and management encompasses the methodologies, tools, and activities involved in designing, developing, testing, maintaining, and evolving software systems throughout their lifecycle. It includes requirements engineering, programming, version control, configuration management, and project planning, addressing how software is built, organized, and kept functional over time.
[CCS-SW-002] Software notations and tools -- Software notations and tools refer to the languages, formalisms, and development environments used to create, transform, or analyze software, including programming languages, modeling notations, compilers, interpreters, and integrated development environments that support the software engineering process.
[CCS-SW-003] Software organization and properties -- Software organization and properties covers the study of how software components are structured, modularized, and characterized, including aspects such as architecture, design patterns, and quality attributes. It addresses issues of code organization, reuse, maintainability, and the measurement of software properties.
[CCS-SW-004] Software system structures -- Software system structures describe the architectural organization of software components, their relationships, and interaction patterns. The area covers modularization, layering, component-based design, and the models used to represent how parts of a system are composed and communicate.
[CCS-THEORY] Theory of Computation -- CCS-THEORY addresses the fundamental principles of computation, encompassing algorithms, complexity, formal languages, automata, and computability. It examines computational models, proof techniques, and the intrinsic difficulty of problems, providing the theoretical framework that underpins many other areas of computer science.
[CCS-THEORY-001] Formal languages and automata theory -- The Formal languages and automata theory area investigates the mathematical structures that define how symbols can be processed and recognized, covering the classification of languages, grammars, and the design of computational models such as finite automata, pushdown automata, and Turing machines, which form the theoretical basis for compilers, complexity analysis, and verification.
[CCS-THEORY-002] Computational complexity and cryptography -- This area focuses on the intersection of computational complexity theory and cryptography, exploring how hardness assumptions, complexity classes and reductions underpin the security of cryptographic primitives and protocols. It includes the study of secure computation, cryptographic proof techniques, and the classification of problems according to their computational difficulty.
[CCS-THEORY-003] Logic and verification -- Logic and verification encompasses the study and application of formal logical methods to prove the correctness of software and hardware systems, including theorem proving, model checking, type systems, and formal specification techniques that ensure programs behave as intended.
[CCS-THEORY-004] Design and analysis of algorithms -- The entry covers the creation of algorithmic methods and the theoretical study of their efficiency, correctness, and resource usage. It includes techniques for proving bounds, analyzing worst-case and average-case behavior, and developing novel algorithmic frameworks across computational problems.
[CCS-THEORY-005] Randomness, geometry and discrete mathematics -- This area studies the role of random processes and probabilistic techniques in geometric and combinatorial problems, including random graphs, stochastic geometry, and discrete structures such as lattices and tilings. It also examines algorithmic and theoretical aspects of randomness in discrete mathematics.
