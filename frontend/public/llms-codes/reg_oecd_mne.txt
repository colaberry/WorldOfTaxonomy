[ch_1] Chapter I - Concepts and Principles -- Chapter I of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises sets out the fundamental concepts and principles of responsible business conduct, defining the framework for multinational companies' duties to respect human rights, contribute to sustainable development, and operate with transparency and integrity.
[ch_10] Chapter X - Competition -- Chapter X of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises addresses competition. It outlines the expectations for enterprises to respect competition law, avoid anti-competitive behavior, and promote market fairness. The chapter provides guidance on compliance, cooperation with authorities, and the role of responsible business conduct in supporting competitive markets.
[ch_11] Chapter XI - Taxation -- Chapter XI of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises addresses taxation, urging companies to comply with applicable tax laws and regulations, avoid aggressive tax planning, and contribute fairly to public finances. It promotes transparency, cooperation with tax authorities, and responsible tax practices that support sustainable development.
[ch_12] Chapter XII - Digital Considerations -- Chapter XII of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises addresses digital considerations, outlining how companies should apply the broader responsible-business principles to emerging technologies such as artificial intelligence, big data, and online platforms. It stresses transparency, privacy protection, cybersecurity, respect for human rights and the need for effective stakeholder engagement in the digital sphere.
[ch_2] Chapter II - General Policies -- Chapter II of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises outlines the overarching policy framework that multinational companies should follow to ensure responsible business conduct. It sets out principles on human rights, labor standards, environment, anti-corruption and consumer interests, and requires firms to integrate these policies into corporate strategies and daily operations.
[ch_3] Chapter III - Disclosure -- Chapter III of the OECD Guidelines outlines the disclosure obligations for multinational enterprises, urging them to communicate transparently about their policies, risk assessments, material impacts and performance to stakeholders. It encourages timely, accurate reporting on environmental, social and governance matters to foster accountability and informed decision-making.
[ch_4] Chapter IV - Human Rights -- Chapter IV of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises sets out the duty of companies to respect human rights, obliging them to prevent or mitigate adverse impacts, to carry out due-diligence assessments, and to offer effective grievance mechanisms when violations occur.
[ch_5] Chapter V - Employment and Industrial Relations -- Chapter V of the OECD Guidelines covers employment and industrial-relations issues, emphasizing respect for fundamental labour rights, non-discrimination, and the right to collective bargaining. It urges multinational enterprises to promote safe working conditions, fair wages, and constructive dialogue with employees and trade unions.
[ch_6] Chapter VI - Environment -- Chapter VI of the OECD Guidelines outlines multinational enterprises' responsibilities for environmental stewardship, urging them to adopt precautionary measures, minimize pollution, conserve natural resources and promote sustainable development. It emphasizes transparent reporting, compliance with local laws, and proactive engagement with stakeholders to mitigate adverse environmental impacts.
[ch_7] Chapter VII - Combating Bribery -- Chapter VII of the OECD Guidelines directs multinational enterprises to adopt policies and procedures that prevent bribery in all forms, requiring transparent accounting, due-diligence on partners and robust internal controls, as well as training, whistle-blower mechanisms and cooperation with law-enforcement to detect and deter illicit payments.
[ch_8] Chapter VIII - Consumer Interests -- Chapter VIII of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises focuses on consumer interests, requiring companies to provide safe, high-quality products and services, ensure truthful advertising, respect consumer privacy and data protection, and establish effective mechanisms for handling complaints and redress, thereby promoting fair and responsible market practices.
[ch_9] Chapter IX - Science and Technology -- Chapter IX of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises addresses science and technology, urging companies to foster innovation while respecting human rights, environmental safeguards and consumer safety. It advises firms to cooperate with governments and research institutions, ensure responsible development and deployment of new technologies, and maintain transparency about research activities and potential impacts. The chapter also highlights the importance of capacity-building, knowledge-sharing and ethical standards in scientific advancement.
[dd] Due Diligence Guidance -- The OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises include a Due Diligence Guidance that helps companies identify, assess and address adverse impacts on human rights, the environment and other stakeholders. It offers practical steps, tools and best-practice recommendations for integrating risk-based due diligence into corporate policies and operations.
[dd_1] Step 1 - Embed RBC into policies and management systems -- The entry outlines the first phase of applying the OECD Guidelines, directing companies to incorporate responsible business conduct into their corporate policies, governance frameworks and management procedures. It emphasizes establishing clear commitments, accountability mechanisms and oversight structures so that RBC principles become embedded in everyday decision-making and operational processes.
[dd_2] Step 2 - Identify and assess adverse impacts -- The OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises outline a systematic process for responsible business conduct, and entry dd_2 focuses on the second step: identifying and assessing adverse impacts. Companies are instructed to map how their activities affect human rights, the environment, and communities, evaluate the severity and likelihood of these impacts, and prioritize them for mitigation. This assessment forms the basis for developing strategies to prevent, mitigate, or remediate negative outcomes.
[dd_3] Step 3 - Cease, prevent or mitigate adverse impacts -- Step 3 of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises requires companies to identify any adverse social, environmental or human-rights impacts linked to their activities and to take immediate action to cease, prevent or mitigate those impacts, integrating remediation into their policies and practices.
[dd_4] Step 4 - Track implementation and results -- Step 4 requires companies to monitor how the Guidelines are put into practice, collecting data on actions taken and measuring outcomes against established targets. The findings are documented, used to refine policies, ensure accountability, and demonstrate progress to stakeholders.
[dd_5] Step 5 - Communicate -- Step 5 - Communicate requires enterprises to publicly disclose their policies, actions and results on responsible business conduct, using transparent reporting, disclosures and stakeholder dialogue. This promotes accountability, builds trust and enables stakeholders to assess the company's performance against the OECD Guidelines.
[dd_6] Step 6 - Provide for or cooperate in remediation -- The sixth step of the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises on Responsible Business Conduct calls on companies to set up effective mechanisms to remediate any adverse impacts they cause or contribute to, and to work cooperatively with affected stakeholders, local communities, and authorities in carrying out those remedial actions.
[ncp] National Contact Points (NCPs) -- National Contact Points (NCPs) are bodies designated by governments to promote and implement the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises, offering a forum for resolving alleged breaches of the Guidelines through mediation or conciliation. They also disseminate information, advise enterprises on responsible business conduct, and facilitate dialogue among stakeholders, helping align corporate practices with internationally agreed standards.
[ncp_1] NCP Core criteria for functional equivalence -- The entry "NCP Core criteria for functional equivalence" defines the essential standards that National Contact Points must meet to be considered functionally equivalent to the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises on Responsible Business Conduct, ensuring they can effectively receive, assess, and resolve complaints about multinational enterprises. It outlines the organizational, procedural, and independence requirements that enable NCPs to operate in line with the OECD's responsible business principles.
[ncp_2] NCP Specific instances (complaint mechanism) -- Entry ncp_2 identifies concrete cases where a national contact point has received and processed a complaint under the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises, detailing the steps of the grievance mechanism, the parties involved, and the outcomes or recommendations issued. It serves as a reference for how the NCP's complaint procedure is applied in practice to resolve disputes over responsible business conduct.
