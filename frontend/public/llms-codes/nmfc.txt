[NM] NMFC Classes -- The National Motor Freight Classification (NMFC) groups commodities into 18 freight classes that reflect factors such as density, handling, liability, stowability and equipment requirements. Each class is assigned a numeric code, which carriers use to calculate base rates for truck shipments. The entry NM refers to the overall set of NMFC classes used nationwide.
[NM.100] Class 100 -- Class 100 is the lowest freight class in the National Motor Freight Classification, indicating the highest density and the lowest transportation cost per pound. Items placed in this class are typically heavy, compact and easy to handle, such as metal hardware, bricks or concrete blocks, and they receive the lowest freight rate from carriers.
[NM.110] Class 110 -- Class 110 in the National Motor Freight Classification designates generally non-perishable, non-hazardous commodities with a moderate weight-to-volume ratio, such as household goods, small appliances, and everyday hardware. These items are considered to have average density and handling characteristics, resulting in a mid-range freight rate compared with lower-class, lighter items and higher-class, denser or more valuable cargo.
[NM.125] Class 125 -- NM 125, or Class 125 in the National Motor Freight Classification, designates a high-density, low-risk commodity that occupies little space relative to its weight and is easy to handle and stow. Shippers of items such as electronics, metal parts or packaged goods use this class to calculate freight charges based on the NMFC pricing matrix.
[NM.150] Class 150 -- Class 150 in the National Motor Freight Classification denotes commodities of moderate density and handling characteristics, typically with a weight-to-volume ratio that falls between low-density (Class 125) and higher-density (Class 175) items. Shipments in this class are usually medium-sized, moderately resilient goods that do not require special care but are not as compact as higher-class freight.
[NM.175] Class 175 -- Class 175 in the National Motor Freight Classification designates low-density, bulky or fragile commodities that occupy a large volume relative to their weight, such as foam, insulation, or other lightweight building materials. Because these items are costly to transport per pound, they are assigned a high freight class, resulting in higher shipping rates. This classification helps carriers calculate charges based on the product's handling and space requirements.
[NM.200] Class 200 -- Class 200 is the highest-density category in the National Motor Freight Classification system, applied to freight that is heavy and compact and therefore incurs the lowest charge per pound. Items in this class are typically dense, low-value goods that occupy little space relative to their weight.
[NM.250] Class 250 -- In the NMFC system Class 250 denotes very dense, sturdy items that are easy to handle, usually heavy, low-value products with minimal risk of damage. Because of their high density and low liability they attract the lowest freight rates on the classification scale.
[NM.300] Class 300 -- Class 300 in the National Motor Freight Classification denotes commodities with moderate density, usually 10 to 12 lb per cubic foot, and average stowability, handling and liability characteristics. Shippers use this class for items such as furniture, appliances and other medium-weight goods that do not require special handling but are not as lightweight as lower-class freight. The classification helps carriers calculate freight rates based on these standardized attributes.
[NM.400] Class 400 -- Class 400 in the National Motor Freight Classification covers commodities that are low-density, easy to handle and non-hazardous, such as many household goods and packaged items. Because they occupy more space than weight would suggest, they are assigned a relatively low freight class, resulting in moderate shipping rates. This classification helps carriers determine pricing based on the commodity's density, stowability and liability.
[NM.50] Class 50 (Fits on pallet, densest) -- Class 50 in the National Motor Freight Classification denotes the densest, heaviest shipments that fit on a standard pallet, earning the highest freight-class rating. Because the items are compact and sturdy, they present minimal handling risk and are charged the lowest transportation rate per pound.
[NM.500] Class 500 (Lightest/bulkiest) -- Class 500 in the National Motor Freight Classification identifies the lightest-bulkiest shipments, which have very low density and occupy a large volume relative to their weight. These characteristics place them in the highest freight-rate tier per pound.
[NM.55] Class 55 -- In the National Motor Freight Classification system, Class 55 (code NM.55) is assigned to low-density, high-value or fragile goods such as electronic equipment, jewelry and similar items that take up more space than they weigh. Because of their relatively light weight for size and special handling requirements, shipments in this class are billed at a lower freight rate than denser, heavier classes
[NM.60] Class 60 -- NM.60, Class 60, designates low-density, high-volume general freight in the National Motor Freight Classification system. It is applied to bulky, lightweight items such as furniture, appliances and other non-fragile goods, and results in higher freight rates because the cargo occupies more space relative to its weight.
[NM.65] Class 65 -- Class 65 in the National Motor Freight Classification denotes commodities with relatively low density and moderate liability, resulting in a higher freight rate than higher-density classes. Typical items include furniture, large appliances and other bulky goods that occupy considerable space but are not fragile or high-value. This class reflects the cost to transport such freight safely and efficiently.
[NM.70] Class 70 -- Class 70 in the National Motor Freight Classification covers commodities that are relatively dense, heavy and non-fragile, presenting low liability and handling costs; they are easy to load, secure and transport without special care.
[NM.77] Class 77.5 -- Class 77.5 in the National Motor Freight Classification denotes a commodity with very low density and poor stowability, requiring special handling and incurring high freight rates. Items in this class are typically bulky, lightweight, and fragile, resulting in a high liability factor.
[NM.85] Class 85 -- In the National Motor Freight Classification system, Class 85 is assigned to items that are relatively light for their size, have low stowability, and are easy to handle, resulting in a moderate freight rate.
[NM.92] Class 92.5 -- Class 92.5 in the National Motor Freight Classification covers flat, non-structural glass and glazing products such as architectural glazing, flat glass panels, and similar items that are moderately dense and require standard handling precautions during freight transport.
