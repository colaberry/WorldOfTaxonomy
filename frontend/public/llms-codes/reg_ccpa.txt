[contractor_req] Contractor Requirements (1798.140(j)) -- The contractor requirements under 1798.140(j) obligate businesses to contract only with service providers who will use personal information solely for the purposes specified by the business, prohibit further disclosure except as required by law, and implement reasonable security measures. Contracts must be in writing, enforceable, and include provisions that the contractor will not retain the data longer than necessary.

[def_business] Business (1798.140(d)) -- A "business" under the California Consumer Privacy Act and California Privacy Rights Act is any for-profit or nonprofit legal entity that determines the purposes and means of processing personal information of California residents, and that meets at least one statutory threshold such as annual gross revenues over $25 million, possession of personal data for 50,000 or more consumers, households or devices, or derives 50 percent of its annual revenue from selling personal information. The definition also applies to subsidiaries and service providers acting on its behalf.

[def_sale] Sale of Personal Information (1798.140(ad)) -- The "sale" definition under California's privacy statutes covers any transfer of personal information for monetary consideration, including selling, renting, disclosing, or sharing data for cross-context behavioral advertising, even when the recipient is a service provider. It requires businesses to disclose such transactions and honor consumer opt-out rights.

[duty_data_min] Data Minimization (1798.100(c)) -- The data minimization duty in sections 1798.100(c) of the CCPA and CPRA obligates businesses to collect, retain and use personal information only to the extent necessary for the disclosed purpose and to delete it when it is no longer needed, preventing excessive or irrelevant data gathering and limiting retention periods.

The notice must identify the categories of personal information collected, the purposes for which it will be used, and the consumer's rights under the statutes.

[duty_respond] Responding to Consumer Requests (1798.130) -- Under the CCPA/CPRA, covered businesses are required to acknowledge and act on verified consumer requests to access, delete, or opt out of the sale of personal information. They must provide a clear response within 45 days, extendable once by another 45 days with notice, and must include required disclosures about the request handling process and any decisions made. This duty ensures consumers can exercise their privacy rights effectively.

Penalties may reach $2,500 for each unintentional violation and $7,500 for each intentional violation, with additional fines for data-breach failures that give consumers a private right of action.

[right_correct] Right to Correct (1798.106) -- The Right to Correct (1798.106) lets California consumers request that a business correct inaccurate personal information it maintains about them. Upon a verifiable request, the business must correct the data or provide a means for the consumer to do so, responding within 45 days and noting any limitations on correction. This right is part of the California Consumer Privacy Act of 2018 and its amendment, the California Privacy Rights Act of 2020.
[right_delete] Right to Delete (1798.105) -- The right to delete, codified at California Civil Code §1798.105, permits a consumer to request that a business erase personal information it holds about them, with the business required to comply within 45 days unless an exception applies, such as for data needed to complete a transaction, comply with a legal obligation, or protect against fraud.
[right_know] Right to Know (1798.100, 1798.110) -- The Right to Know provision (sections 1798.100 and 1798.110) gives California residents the ability to request that a business disclose the personal information it collects, the sources of that data, the purposes for which it is used, and any third parties with whom it is shared. This right applies to both the original 2018 act and the expanded 2020 amendment, ensuring transparency and enabling individuals to verify the accuracy of their data.

This right is exercised through a consumer's request to limit the handling of such data.
[right_nondiscrim] Right to Non-Discrimination (1798.125) -- The Right to Non-Discrimination (1798.125) bars businesses from treating consumers differently because they have exercised any CCPA/CPRA privacy right, such as opting out of sale or requesting data access. It forbids actions like denying services, charging higher fees, or providing a lower quality of goods, and violations may result in civil penalties.

This right requires businesses to provide a clear mechanism for opting out and to honor the request within a specified timeframe.
