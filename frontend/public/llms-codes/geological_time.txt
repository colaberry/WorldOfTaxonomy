[GT] Geological Eons/Eras -- GT is the top-level unit in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart that groups Earth's major eons-Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic and Phanerozoic-each of which is further divided into eras. This classification provides the broadest temporal framework for correlating geological events and rock records worldwide.
[GT.01] Hadean Eon -- The Hadean Eon, code GT.01, is the earliest span of Earth's history, covering roughly 4.6 to 4.0 billion years ago. It encompasses planetary formation, the solidification of the crust, and a molten surface with intense bombardment, but lacks a formal rock record, so its definition relies on geochemical and isotopic evidence.
[GT.02] Archean Eon -- The Archean Eon (code GT.02) spans roughly 4.0 to 2.5 billion years ago, a period when Earth's crust cooled enough to form the first stable continents and when the earliest known microbial life, including photosynthetic cyanobacteria, began to generate oxygen, shaping the planet's early atmosphere.
[GT.03] Proterozoic Eon -- The Proterozoic Eon, spanning roughly 2.5 to 0.54 billion years ago, follows the Archean and precedes the Phanerozoic, marking a time of major atmospheric oxygenation, the emergence of eukaryotic cells and the first multicellular organisms, and significant continental reconfiguration.
[GT.04] Phanerozoic Eon -- The Phanerozoic Eon, code GT.04, is the current eon in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart and spans from about 541 million years ago to the present. It encompasses the Paleozoic, Mesozoic and Cenozoic eras and is characterized by the rapid diversification and dominance of complex animal life, including the rise of plants, insects, vertebrates and humans.
[GT.05] Paleozoic Era -- The Paleozoic Era (GT.05) extends from roughly 541 to 252 million years ago, succeeding the Precambrian and preceding the Mesozoic Era. It is characterized by the rapid diversification of marine organisms, the emergence of fish, amphibians, and early reptiles, and the colonization of land by plants and arthropods, ending with the massive Permian extinction event.
[GT.06] Mesozoic Era -- GT.06 in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart designates the Mesozoic Era, spanning roughly 252 to 66 million years ago. It follows the Paleozoic Era and precedes the Cenozoic, comprising the Triassic, Jurassic, and Cretaceous periods, a time noted for the dominance of dinosaurs and the emergence of early mammals.
[GT.07] Cenozoic Era -- The Cenozoic Era, code GT.07 in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, covers the interval from about 66 million years ago to the present. It follows the Mesozoic Era and is divided into the Paleogene, Neogene, and Quaternary periods, marked by the diversification of mammals, birds and flowering plants and the emergence of humans.
[GT.08] Cambrian Period -- The Cambrian Period (≈541-485 million years ago) inaugurates the Paleozoic Era and the Phanerozoic Eon, marking the first abundant appearance of complex, hard-shelled marine organisms in the fossil record, an event known as the Cambrian Explosion. It follows the Ediacaran Period and precedes the Ordovician, encompassing the early development of most major animal phyla.
[GT.09] Ordovician Period -- The Ordovician Period, spanning roughly 485 to 444 million years ago, is the second period of the Paleozoic Era. It followed the Cambrian and preceded the Silurian, marking a major increase in marine biodiversity, the first appearance of coral reefs, and the early development of vertebrate jawless fish. The period ended with a major ice-driven mass extinction.
[GT.10] Silurian Period -- The Silurian Period, spanning roughly 443.8 to 419.2 million years ago, follows the Ordovician and precedes the Devonian in the Paleozoic Era. It is marked by a relatively stable climate, extensive shallow seas, and the diversification of marine life such as brachiopods and early jawed fish, while the first simple vascular plants and terrestrial arthropods began colonising land.
[GT.11] Devonian Period -- The Devonian Period, spanning roughly 419 to 359 million years ago, is the fourth period of the Paleozoic Era and is often called the Age-of-Fishes because of the rapid diversification of marine vertebrates. During this time the first extensive terrestrial plant forests appeared, arthropods colonized land, and the earliest tetrapods began to evolve.
[GT.12] Carboniferous Period -- The Carboniferous Period, designated GT.12 in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, spans roughly 359 to 299 million years ago within the Paleozoic Era. It is known for extensive coal-forming swamp forests, diversification of amphibians, and the appearance of early amniotes.
[GT.13] Permian Period -- The Permian Period, code GT.13 on the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, is the final period of the Paleozoic Era spanning roughly 298.9 to 251.9 million years ago. It followed the Carboniferous and preceded the Triassic of the Mesozoic, marking the assembly of the supercontinent Pangaea and ending with the greatest mass-extinction event in Earth's history.
[GT.14] Triassic Period -- The Triassic Period, identified as GT.14 in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, follows the Permian and precedes the Jurassic within the Mesozoic Era, spanning roughly 252 to 201 million years ago. It marks the rise of the first dinosaurs and early marine reptiles, and features the breakup of the supercontinent Pangaea into separate landmasses.

[GT.16] Cretaceous Period -- The Cretaceous Period, spanning roughly 145 to 66 million years ago, is the final period of the Mesozoic Era characterized by high sea levels, warm climate, and the dominance of dinosaurs, ending with a mass extinction that wiped out most dinosaur species.
[GT.17] Paleogene Period -- The Paleogene Period, the earliest division of the Cenozoic Era, spans roughly 66 to 23 million years ago and follows the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event. It comprises the Paleocene, Eocene and Oligocene epochs, during which mammals diversified rapidly and global climates shifted from greenhouse to cooler conditions.
[GT.18] Neogene Period -- The Neogene Period is the second period of the Cenozoic Era, spanning about 23.03 to 2.58 million years ago, following the Paleogene and preceding the Quaternary. It is marked by global cooling, the spread of grasslands, and significant diversification of mammals and birds. The epoch also includes the emergence of early hominins and the development of modern ecosystems.
[GT.19] Quaternary Period -- The Quaternary Period, code GT.19 in the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, is the most recent division of the Cenozoic Era, extending from about 2.58 million years ago to the present. It comprises the Pleistocene and Holocene epochs, marked by repeated glacial cycles and the evolution and global spread of Homo sapiens.
