[cat_i] Category I - Firearms, Close Assault Weapons -- Category I under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations includes standard firearms and close-assault weapons such as pistols, rifles, shotguns, and personal defense weapons that are designed to fire projectiles. These items are subject to the most restrictive export licensing requirements because they are directly related to combat and personal defense.
[cat_ii] Category II - Guns and Armament -- Category II under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers firearms such as pistols, rifles, shotguns and revolvers, together with their related accessories, ammunition and component parts, and governs the design, production, export and brokering of these items, requiring registration and licensing for any U.S. person involved.
[cat_iii] Category III - Ammunition and Ordnance -- Category III includes all ammunition, rockets, missiles, bombs, mines, grenades and related ordnance, as well as their components, accessories and parts, that are designed for use with weapons listed on the United States Munitions List. Export, reexport or temporary import of these items to foreign persons requires a license from the State Department under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations.
[cat_iv] Category IV - Launch Vehicles, Guided Missiles, Ballistic Missiles -- Category IV encompasses launch vehicles, guided missiles and ballistic missiles, as well as related design, development, production, testing and technical data. Items in this category are tightly controlled under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations and generally require a specific export license before they can be transferred to foreign persons or countries.
[cat_ix] Category IX - Military Training Equipment and Training -- Category IX under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers military training equipment and related training aids, including weapons simulators, target systems, laser trainers, and other devices used to teach personnel how to operate, maintain, or employ defense articles. The category also encompasses associated software, manuals, and technical data that support the use of these training tools.
[cat_v] Category V - Explosives and Energetic Materials -- Category V under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers explosives, propellants, pyrotechnics, and other energetic materials, including their components, precursors, and related manufacturing equipment. Items in this category are subject to licensing and export controls because of their potential use in weapons, munitions, or other defense applications.
[cat_vi] Category VI - Surface Vessels of War and Special Naval Equipment -- Category VI of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations controls surface vessels of war-including warships, combatant craft, and associated special naval equipment such as weapons systems, fire control and propulsion components. Export, re-export, or transfer of these items requires a license from the State Department and is subject to strict end-use and end-user restrictions.
[cat_vii] Category VII - Ground Vehicles -- Category VII under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations governs ground vehicles, including tanks, armored personnel carriers, military trucks and other land-based combat or support vehicles. Items in this category are subject to licensing requirements for export, re-export, and temporary export to ensure they do not fall into the hands of prohibited end-users or destinations. Compliance with ITAR controls is mandatory for manufacturers, brokers, and exporters of such equipment.
[cat_viii] Category VIII - Aircraft and Related Articles -- Category VIII covers aircraft and related articles, including complete airplanes, helicopters, unmanned aerial vehicles, rockets, propellers, engines, flight-control and navigation systems, spare parts, accessories, technical data and related services. The category applies to both military and dual-use aircraft that can be employed for defense purposes and requires an ITAR export license for transfer.

[cat_xii] Category XII - Fire Control, Laser, Imaging, and Guidance Equipment -- Category XII under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers fire-control systems, laser and optical devices, imaging equipment, and guidance components used in weapons and military platforms. Items include laser rangefinders, target trackers, electro-optical sensors, missile guidance kits and related software that enable targeting, tracking or navigation.
[cat_xiii] Category XIII - Materials and Miscellaneous Articles -- Category XIII includes a wide array of items that are not specifically enumerated elsewhere in the ITAR, such as raw materials, chemicals, and other miscellaneous articles that could be used in the design, development, or production of defense articles. Because these items may have a bearing on military applications, their export or re-export generally requires a license under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations.
[cat_xiv] Category XIV - Toxicological Agents and Equipment -- Category XIV under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers toxicological agents, including chemical warfare and riot control substances, and the equipment used to produce, store, deploy, detect, protect against, or decontaminate them. It also includes protective gear, detection systems, and related training or support items.

[cat_xv] Category XV - Spacecraft and Related Articles -- Category XV under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers spacecraft, launch vehicles, satellites and related articles such as payload fairings, propulsion systems, guidance and control equipment, and associated technical data. Items in this category are subject to strict export controls to prevent proliferation of space technology and to protect national security.
[cat_xvi] Category XVI - Nuclear Weapons Related Articles -- Category XVI under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers articles and related technical data that are specifically designed, developed, or adapted for nuclear weapons, including nuclear weapon components, delivery system parts, and related testing equipment. These items are strictly controlled because they contribute directly to the fabrication or use of nuclear explosive devices.
[cat_xvii] Category XVII - Classified Articles, Technical Data, and Defense Services -- Category XVII governs articles, technical data and defense services that are classified for national-security reasons, requiring a specific license before export, reexport or temporary import. It includes weapons, related technology and support activities deemed sensitive, and mandates strict handling, record-keeping and reporting to ensure compliance with ITAR.
[cat_xviii] Category XVIII - Directed Energy Weapons -- Category XVIII of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations covers directed-energy weapons, including high-energy lasers, high-power microwave systems and other devices that emit focused energy to damage, disable or deter targets. Items that incorporate, produce or control such emissions are subject to licensing and export controls under ITAR.
[cat_xx] Category XX - Submersible Vessels and Related Articles -- Category XX controls submersible vessels such as manned submarines, unmanned underwater vehicles, and the related parts, accessories, equipment and technical data. Items in this group are deemed defense articles because they can be used for military underwater operations, so their export or re-export outside the United States requires an ITAR license.
[cat_xxi] Category XXI - Articles, Technical Data, and Defense Services Not Otherwise Enumerated -- Category XXI covers items, technical data and defense services that are not listed elsewhere in the ITAR, serving as a catch-all for weapons-related articles and information that still fall under U.S. export controls. It ensures that any unlisted but defense-related material is subject to licensing and compliance requirements.
[part_120] Part 120 - Purpose and Definitions -- Part 120 sets out the overall purpose of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations and provides the key definitions used throughout the rulebook, establishing the legal framework for controlling the export, import, and brokering of defense articles and services.
[part_121] Part 121 - The United States Munitions List -- Part 121 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations is the United States Munitions List, which enumerates defense articles, services and related technical data subject to export control. It identifies items whose manufacture, transfer or use is regulated for national security and foreign policy reasons, and determines when a license is required for export, re-export or temporary import.
[part_122] Part 122 - Registration of Manufacturers and Exporters -- Part 122 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations mandates that manufacturers and exporters of defense articles register with the Directorate of Defense Trade Controls, supplying detailed information on ownership, location, and business activities. Registration is required for any entity that intends to export, temporarily import, or broker items listed on the United States Munitions List.
[part_123] Part 123 - Licenses for Defense Articles -- Part 123 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations establishes the licensing framework for defense articles, detailing when a license is required, the types of licenses available, and the procedures for application, review, and approval. It also lists exemptions and special conditions for certain transactions.
[part_124] Part 124 - Agreements, Off-Shore Procurement, Other -- Part 124 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations sets out the rules for agreements that involve the export or temporary import of defense articles, including off-shore procurement and related activities. It details licensing requirements, compliance obligations, and record-keeping duties for U.S. persons and foreign contractors engaged in such agreements.
[part_125] Part 125 - Licenses for Technical Data and Services -- Part 125 sets the rules for granting licenses to export, temporarily import, or broker technical data and related services tied to defense articles. It details who may apply, the required documentation, conditions for approval, exemptions, and record-keeping obligations.
[part_126] Part 126 - General Policies and Provisions -- Part 126 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations establishes the overarching policies and procedural framework for controlling defense articles and services. It outlines the Department of State's authority, licensing requirements, compliance obligations, record-keeping rules, and enforcement mechanisms that apply across all ITAR-covered activities.
[part_127] Part 127 - Violations and Penalties -- Part 127 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations outlines civil and criminal sanctions for non-compliance, detailing prohibited actions, fines, imprisonment, and administrative measures such as debarment. It also describes the investigation process and enforcement procedures used by the Department of State.
[part_128] Part 128 - Administrative Procedures -- Part 128 details the administrative procedures that exporters, manufacturers and brokers must follow under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations, including licensing applications, record-keeping requirements, reporting obligations, and compliance reviews to ensure controlled defense articles and services are handled in accordance with U.S. law.
[part_129] Part 129 - Brokering Activities -- Part 129 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations sets out the requirements and licensing rules for individuals and entities that conduct brokering activities involving defense articles, services, or related technical data. It defines brokering, outlines registration, record-keeping, and compliance obligations, and details the circumstances under which a brokering license must be obtained.
[part_130] Part 130 - Political Contributions, Fees, and Commissions -- Part 130 of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations sets rules for political contributions, fees and commissions paid by U.S. persons or entities involved in defense articles or services, requiring prior approval from the State Department and prohibiting certain payments that could influence foreign officials or affect the procurement of defense articles. It also outlines permissible payments, reporting obligations, and penalties for violations.
