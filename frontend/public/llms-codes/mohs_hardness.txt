[MH] Mohs Scale -- The Mohs Scale is a qualitative ordinal scale ranking minerals by their ability to scratch one another, ranging from talc at 1 to diamond at 10. Developed by Friedrich Mohs in 1812, it remains a simple field test for relative hardness.
[MH.01] 1: Talc -- Talc is the softest mineral on the Mohs hardness scale, assigned a value of 1. It can be easily scratched with a fingernail and feels greasy to the touch. Because of its low hardness it is used as a lubricant and in talcum powder.
[MH.02] 2: Gypsum -- Gypsum is a soft sulfate mineral with a Mohs hardness of 2, meaning it can be scratched by a fingernail and is easily powdered. It commonly forms in sedimentary settings and is widely used in plaster and drywall.
[MH.03] 3: Calcite -- Calcite ranks 3 on the Mohs scale of mineral hardness, meaning it can be scratched by a copper penny but will scratch gypsum and talc. It is a common carbonate mineral found in sedimentary rocks and is characterized by its perfect rhombohedral cleavage.
[MH.04] 4: Fluorite -- Fluorite, assigned the code MH.04 on the Mohs scale, rates a hardness of four, meaning it can be scratched by a steel nail but resists scratching by a copper coin. Composed of calcium fluoride, it often forms cubic crystals and is used industrially as a source of fluorine and in optics for its low refractive index.
[MH.05] 5: Apatite -- Apatite is the mineral assigned a hardness of 5 on the Mohs scale. It can scratch glass and is commonly found in phosphate deposits, used in fertilizers and as a source of phosphorus. Its hardness makes it useful for testing medium hardness in mineral identification.
[MH.06] 6: Orthoclase -- Orthoclase is a potassium feldspar mineral that grades a hardness of 6 on the Mohs scale, meaning it can scratch glass but is scratched by harder minerals such as quartz. It typically occurs in pink, white or green hues and is a common constituent of granitic and other igneous rocks.
[MH.07] 7: Quartz -- Quartz ranks at 7 on the Mohs scale, indicating it can readily scratch glass and most common minerals while being scratched only by harder substances such as topaz or diamond. This hardness makes quartz durable for a variety of industrial and decorative applications.
[MH.08] 8: Topaz -- Topaz ranks at eight on the Mohs scale, indicating it can scratch quartz and most common glasses but is scratched by corundum and diamond. Its hardness makes it suitable for jewelry and industrial applications that require resistance to abrasion. The mineral is a silicate of aluminum and fluorine, typically colorless to yellow or blue.
[MH.09] 9: Corundum -- Corundum ranks nine on the Mohs scale of mineral hardness, making it the second hardest natural material after diamond. It is an aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) crystal that, when colored by trace elements, forms the gemstones ruby and sapphire, and it is widely used as an abrasive in industrial applications.
[MH.10] 10: Diamond -- Diamond sits at the top of the Mohs scale with a hardness of 10, making it the hardest naturally occurring material and the benchmark against which all other minerals are measured. Its extreme resistance to scratching allows it to cut virtually any other substance, which is why it is used in industrial drilling and jewelry.
