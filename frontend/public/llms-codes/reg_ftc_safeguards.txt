[access_controls] Access Controls (314.4(c)(1)) -- Access Controls under the FTC Safeguarding Customer Information rule require firms to restrict access to personal data to employees who need it for their job duties. The standard mandates authentication mechanisms, role-based permissions, and periodic reviews to ensure that only authorized personnel can view, modify, or transmit customer information. This helps limit internal exposure and reduces the risk of unauthorized disclosure.
[board_report] Annual Board Report -- The Annual Board Report is a required filing under the FTC Safeguarding Customer Information rule (16 CFR § 314) that summarizes an organization's data-security program, risk assessments, incident responses, and compliance activities for the preceding year, providing the board of directors with a concise overview of protections for customer information.
[change_mgmt] Change Management Procedures (314.4(c)(7)) -- The FTC's Safeguarding Customer Information rules require firms to maintain documented change-management procedures that control and track modifications to system components, software, and configurations. These procedures must ensure that changes are reviewed, approved, tested, and recorded to prevent unauthorized alterations and to preserve the integrity, confidentiality, and availability of protected data.
[data_disposal] Secure Disposal of Customer Information (314.4(c)(6)) -- The FTC's "Secure Disposal of Customer Information" rule (314.4(c)(6)) obligates businesses to destroy or render unreadable any consumer personal information they no longer need for business purposes, using reasonable methods such as shredding, burning, pulverizing, or secure erasure of electronic files, and to maintain policies that ensure disposal is performed promptly and safely.
[def_customer_info] Definition of Customer Information (314.2(d)) -- The entry defines "customer information" under the FTC Safeguarding Customer Information rule (16 CFR § 314.2(d)). It includes any personal data a financial institution collects about an existing or prospective consumer, such as name, address, Social Security number, account numbers, login credentials, and transaction history, whether stored electronically, on paper, or in any other format. This definition guides how firms must protect the data from unauthorized access.
[def_fi] Definition of Financial Institution (314.2(h)) -- The entry defines a "financial institution" for purposes of the FTC Safeguards Rule, covering any entity that regularly conducts financial activities such as lending, deposit taking, or providing credit services, and therefore must comply with the data-protection standards set out in 16 CFR § 314.2(h).
[encryption] Encryption of Customer Information (314.4(c)(3)) -- The FTC rule mandates that any electronic system handling customer data must encrypt the information both during transmission and while stored, rendering it unreadable without the proper decryption key. This protects consumers from unauthorized access and data breaches.
[incident_response] Incident Response Plan (314.4(h)) -- The FTC standards require entities that handle consumer credit-report information to adopt a written incident response plan that outlines steps for identifying, containing, and mitigating a breach of customer data. The plan must designate a response team, establish reporting timelines to the FTC and affected individuals, and include procedures for documenting the incident and preventing future occurrences.
[inventory] Information Asset Inventory (314.4(c)(2)) -- The FTC requires covered entities to keep a documented inventory of every information asset that stores, processes, or transmits customer data, noting its type, location, and purpose of use. This inventory must be regularly updated and made available for inspection under 16 CFR 314.4(c)(2).
[mfa] Multi-Factor Authentication (314.4(c)(5)) -- Multi-factor authentication is a security measure that requires users to provide two or more independent credentials-such as a password, a physical token, or a biometric identifier-to verify their identity before accessing sensitive consumer information, as mandated by the FTC Safeguards Rule under 16 CFR § 314.4(c)(5). This layered approach helps reduce the risk of unauthorized access and data breaches.
[monitoring] Monitoring and Logging (314.4(c)(8)) -- The monitoring and logging provision obliges covered entities to establish procedures that continuously track access to and use of customer information, generate and retain logs of system activity, and regularly review those logs to detect and respond to unauthorized or suspicious behavior. This helps ensure timely identification of potential breaches and supports ongoing risk management under the FTC Safeguard Rule.
[notification] FTC Notification Requirements (314.4(j)) -- The entry outlines the FTC's rule that covered businesses must notify affected consumers in a timely, clear manner when personal information is compromised
[oversight] Oversight and Reporting -- The Oversight and Reporting section of the FTC Safeguarding Customer Information rule requires covered entities to establish written policies and procedures for monitoring compliance with security standards, conduct periodic risk assessments, and document any incidents involving customer data. Entities must submit annual reports to the FTC that describe the safeguards in place, any breaches that occurred, and the corrective actions taken to address identified weaknesses.
[pen_test] Penetration Testing (314.4(d)(2)) -- The FTC Safeguards Rule (16 CFR § 314.4(d)(2)) requires covered businesses to conduct an annual penetration test or a comprehensive security assessment performed by an independent party, evaluating the effectiveness of technical and operational controls against realistic attack scenarios. The test must identify vulnerabilities, assess exploitability, and result in a documented remediation plan to address any findings.
[program] Information Security Program Requirements -- The Information Security Program Requirements under the FTC Safeguards Rule (16 CFR § 314) mandate that covered businesses develop and maintain a written security program that addresses risk assessment, employee training, access controls, data encryption, monitoring, and incident-response procedures to protect customer personal information.
[qi_report] Qualified Individual Reporting to Board (314.4(i)) -- The FTC's Safeguarding Customer Information rule (16 CFR § 314) obliges each covered entity to designate a qualified individual responsible for the security program and to report that person's name, qualifications and authority to the board of directors. Under § 314.4(i) the board must receive this information in writing and retain it for inspection. This ensures top-level oversight of the entity's data-protection efforts.
[qualified_individual] Designated Qualified Individual (314.4(a)) -- A Designated Qualified Individual is the person a covered entity must name to oversee its information-security program under FTC Rule 314.4(a). This individual has authority and responsibility for implementing, maintaining, and monitoring the safeguards required to protect customer credit-card data, and must report on compliance to senior management.
[risk_assessment] Written Risk Assessment (314.4(b)) -- The written risk assessment, required by FTC Safeguarding Customer Information standards at 16 CFR 314.4(b), is a documented analysis that identifies potential threats to personal data, evaluates the likelihood and impact of those threats, and outlines measures the organization will implement to mitigate identified risks.
[safeguards] Required Safeguards -- The "Required Safeguards" entry under the FTC Standards for Safeguarding Customer Information (16 CFR § 314) specifies the technical, physical and administrative measures firms must implement to protect consumer data. It includes access controls, encryption, secure disposal, employee training and regular risk assessments to prevent unauthorized access, use or disclosure.
[scope] Scope and Definitions -- The scope defines the types of entities and activities covered by the FTC's Safeguarding Customer Information rule, applying to any person who maintains consumer-reporting data in electronic form. It also provides key definitions for terms such as "customer information," "electronic storage," and "reasonable security procedures," establishing the baseline for compliance under 16 CFR § 314.
[service_provider] Service Provider Oversight (314.4(f)) -- The FTC's Safeguarding Customer Information rule obligates covered entities to oversee any service provider that processes or stores customer data. They must conduct due-diligence, require written agreements that enforce the standards, monitor performance, and terminate relationships if the provider fails to maintain required safeguards.
[vulnerability] Vulnerability Assessment (314.4(d)) -- The FTC's Safeguarding Customer Information rule (16 CFR § 314) requires covered entities to conduct a vulnerability assessment, meaning they must regularly identify and evaluate weaknesses in their information systems that could be exploited to compromise personal data. This assessment helps ensure appropriate security measures are in place to protect consumer information.
[written_program] Written Information Security Program -- The written information security program is a documented set of policies and procedures that a covered entity must develop and maintain to protect customer information, outlining risk assessments, employee training, access controls, incident response, and oversight responsibilities as required by the FTC Safeguards Rule.
