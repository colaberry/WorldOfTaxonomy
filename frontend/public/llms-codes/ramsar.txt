[RM.01] Marine/Coastal - Shallow Marine Waters -- RM.01 designates shallow marine waters within the Ramsar Wetland Classification System, covering coastal zones where water depth typically does not exceed a few metres and includes intertidal flats, lagoons, estuaries and near-shore habitats that support diverse aquatic life and serve as feeding or breeding grounds for birds and marine species.

[RM.03] Marine/Coastal - Coral Reefs -- RM.03 identifies coral reef ecosystems within the Ramsar framework as marine or coastal wetlands characterized by calcium carbonate structures built by colonial corals. These habitats support high biodiversity, provide coastal protection and important resources for fisheries and tourism, while being vulnerable to climate change, pollution and overfishing.
[RM.04] Marine/Coastal - Rocky Shores -- RM.04 denotes marine and coastal wetland sites dominated by exposed bedrock or boulder coastlines where tidal action creates intertidal zones and splash-zones supporting algae, limpets, barnacles and other specialized organisms, often with limited sediment accumulation. These rocky shore habitats are important for biodiversity, shoreline stability and as feeding grounds for many marine species.
[RM.05] Marine/Coastal - Sand/Shingle Shores -- RM.05 denotes marine and coastal sand or shingle shores, areas of unconsolidated sediment along tidal zones where wave and wind action continuously reshape the substrate. These habitats support specialised plant communities, nesting birds, and a variety of invertebrates, and they play a key role in coastline stability, nutrient cycling and biodiversity within the Ramsar wetland framework.
[RM.06] Marine/Coastal - Estuarine Waters -- RM.06 covers wetland sites where freshwater and seawater intermingle in estuaries, including tidal channels, salt marshes, mangroves and associated intertidal zones, and emphasizes their role in nutrient cycling, habitat provision for fish and wildlife, and flood mitigation.
[RM.07] Marine/Coastal - Intertidal Mud/Sand Flats -- RM.07 designates marine or coastal intertidal mud and sand flats that are regularly flooded and exposed by tidal movements. These habitats provide extensive feeding and breeding grounds for wading birds, fish and invertebrates, and play a key role in nutrient cycling and shoreline stability.

[RM.09] Inland - Seasonal Rivers/Streams -- RM.09 designates inland water bodies that flow only during periods of rainfall or seasonal melt, including temporary rivers and streams that dry up for part of the year. These wetlands support adaptable aquatic and riparian species and provide flood mitigation, groundwater recharge, and seasonal habitat connectivity across the landscape.
[RM.10] Inland - Permanent Freshwater Lakes -- RM.10 designates inland permanent freshwater lakes, ranging from small ponds to large basins, that retain water year-round and support diverse aquatic ecosystems. These lakes are characterized by stable water levels, natural or artificial origins, and provide habitat for fish, waterbirds and vegetation.
[RM.11] Inland - Seasonal Freshwater Lakes -- RM.11 designates inland lakes that hold freshwater only during part of the year, filling in the wet season and often drying out in the dry season. These seasonal water bodies support aquatic plants and wildlife adapted to fluctuating water levels and can provide critical habitat for migratory waterbirds when water is present.
[RM.12] Inland - Permanent Freshwater Marshes -- RM.12 designates inland wetlands that are permanently inundated with fresh water and characterized by shallow water and emergent herbaceous vegetation such as reeds, cattails, and sedges. These marshes retain water year-round, support high biological productivity, and provide habitat for waterfowl, fish, and invertebrates while offering flood control and water-purification functions.
[RM.13] Inland - Peatlands/Bogs -- RM.13 designates inland peatlands and bogs, which are water-logged, organic-rich soils that accumulate peat and host specialized vegetation such as Sphagnum mosses and carnivorous plants. These wetlands store significant carbon, regulate hydrology, and provide habitat for distinctive plant and animal communities.
[RM.14] Inland - Alpine Wetlands -- RM.14 designates inland alpine wetlands, which are high-elevation, non-coastal peatlands and water-filled depressions formed by glacial melt, snow runoff, or permafrost processes. These ecosystems support specialized flora and fauna adapted to cold, nutrient-poor conditions, act as natural water reservoirs, and play a key role in carbon storage and downstream water regulation.
[RM.15] Inland - Freshwater Springs/Oases -- RM.15 designates inland freshwater springs and oases, representing small, naturally occurring groundwater discharge zones that sustain permanent or seasonal water bodies and associated vegetation. These habitats provide critical biodiversity refuges, support unique plant communities, and supply water resources for wildlife and nearby human uses.
[RM.16] Inland - Geothermal Wetlands -- RM.16 identifies inland geothermal wetlands, which are wetland areas created by geothermal activity such as hot springs, fumaroles, or volcanic heating. These sites feature elevated water temperatures, mineral-rich fluids and specialized plant and animal communities adapted to the thermal environment.
[RM.17] Inland - Karst Systems -- RM.17 inland karst systems are wetlands that develop on soluble carbonate rocks where dissolution creates sinkholes, caves, and underground water flow. These habitats often feature surface depressions, springs, and seasonal pools linked to subterranean aquifers, supporting distinctive flora and fauna adapted to fluctuating water levels and high mineral content.
[RM.18] Human-Made - Aquaculture Ponds -- RM.18 designates human-made aquaculture ponds, artificial water bodies constructed and managed for the cultivation of fish, crustaceans, molluscs or other aquatic organisms. These wetlands are typically shallow, may be lined or unlined, and are intensively stocked, fed and harvested for commercial production, often integrating water management with surrounding agricultural or industrial activities.
[RM.19] Human-Made - Irrigated Land -- RM.19 identifies wetlands that are artificial and specifically developed for irrigation, where water is supplied to support crops or other agricultural uses. These sites are managed landscapes that retain water for extended periods, providing aquatic habitats alongside cultivated land.
[RM.20] Human-Made - Reservoirs -- RM.20 classifies reservoirs that are artificial water bodies formed by damming rivers or excavating basins for purposes such as water storage, irrigation, hydroelectric power, flood control or recreation. These human-made wetlands have regulated hydrology yet can support diverse aquatic flora and fauna similar to natural lakes.
