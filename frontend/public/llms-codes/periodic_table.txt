[PT] Element Groups -- PT denotes the categorization of chemical elements into groups on the periodic table, such as alkali metals, halogens and noble gases. These groups share similar valence electron configurations and chemical properties, allowing chemists to predict reactivity trends and behavior across the elements.
[PT.01] Alkali Metals -- Alkali metals are the elements in Group 1 of the periodic table, including lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, cesium and francium. They are soft, highly reactive metals that readily lose their single valence electron to form +1 ions, and they react vigorously with water and air, making them important in both industrial chemistry and biological processes.

[PT.03] Transition Metals -- Transition metals are a set of elements found in the central block of the periodic table, spanning groups 3 to 12. They are characterized by partially filled d electron subshells, which give them high electrical conductivity, malleability, and the ability to form colored compounds and multiple oxidation states. These properties make them essential in industrial catalysis, alloys, and various structural applications.
[PT.04] Post-Transition Metals -- Post-transition metals are the group of metallic elements situated to the right of the transition metals on the periodic table, including aluminum, gallium, indium, tin, lead, bismuth and others. They typically have lower densities and melting points than transition metals, exhibit a mix of metallic and brittle characteristics, and often form covalent compounds rather than the ionic ones common to more reactive metals.
[PT.05] Metalloids -- Metalloids are a set of elements whose physical and chemical characteristics lie between those of metals and non-metals, showing intermediate conductivity and often forming amphoteric oxides. They are typically positioned along the staircase line of the periodic table and include silicon, germanium, arsenic, antimony, tellurium, and occasionally boron and polonium. Their mixed properties make them valuable in semiconductor and alloy applications.
[PT.06] Reactive Nonmetals -- Reactive nonmetals are highly electronegative elements that readily gain electrons during chemical reactions, often forming negative ions or covalent compounds. They include groups such as the halogens and oxygen, and are characterized by strong oxidizing properties and a tendency to form acids and salts.
[PT.07] Noble Gases -- Noble gases are the elements of group 18 of the periodic table, characterized by full outer electron shells that make them chemically inert under most conditions. The series includes helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon and the synthetic oganesson, and they are used in applications such as lighting, cryogenics and scientific instrumentation.
[PT.08] Lanthanides -- The lanthanides are a series of fifteen metallic elements with atomic numbers 57 through 71, positioned in the f-block of the periodic table. They share similar chemical behavior, exhibit high luster, and are commonly referred to as rare-earth elements, playing key roles in high-technology applications such as magnets, lasers and phosphors.
[PT.09] Actinides -- The actinide series comprises the fifteen elements from actinium (atomic number 89) to lawrencium (atomic number 103). These f-block elements are typically radioactive, show a range of oxidation states, and many are key materials in nuclear power and weapons.
[PT.10] Unknown Properties (Super-heavy) -- PT.10 refers to the group of super-heavy elements that have been synthesized beyond the currently known periodic table, typically with atomic numbers greater than 118. Their physical and chemical characteristics remain largely unknown, and research focuses on theoretical predictions and experimental discovery of their stability, decay modes, and potential novel properties.
[PT.11] Period 1 (H, He) -- PT.11 refers to the first period of the periodic table, which includes just hydrogen and helium. This period initiates the s-block, with hydrogen placed in group 1 and helium in group 18, representing the lightest elements.
[PT.12] Period 2 (Li-Ne) -- Period 2 of the periodic table includes the elements from lithium (Li) to neon
[PT.13] Period 3 (Na-Ar) -- PT.13 designates the third horizontal period of the periodic table, covering the eight elements from sodium (Na) through argon (Ar). This sequence includes the s-block metals sodium and magnesium, followed by the p-block elements aluminum, silicon, phosphorus, sulfur, chlorine, and the noble gas argon. The period marks the filling of the third electron shell.
[PT.14] Period 4 (K-Kr) -- Period 4 of the periodic table spans the elements from potassium (K) to krypton (Kr), adding a new electron shell (n = 4) to the atomic structure. This row includes the alkaline-metal potassium, alkaline-earth calcium, the first transition-metal series (scandium through zinc), and the p-block elements ending with the noble gas krypton.
[PT.15] Period 5 (Rb-Xe) -- PT.15 covers the fifth horizontal row of the periodic table, comprising the elements from rubidium (Rb) through xenon (Xe). This period includes the alkali metal Rb, the alkaline-earth metal Sr, a block of transition metals (Y-Cd), post-transition elements and metalloids (In, Sn, Sb, Te, I), and ends with the noble gas Xe.
[PT.16] Period 6 (Cs-Rn) -- Period 6 of the periodic table runs from element 55 Cesium to element 86 Radon, covering a span of 32 elements that include the lanthanide series, transition metals, post-transition metals, metalloids, nonmetals and the noble gases at the end of the row. This period marks the second complete filling of the 6s, 5d and 4f subshells and introduces the heaviest stable gaseous element, radon.
[PT.17] Period 7 (Fr-Og) -- Period 7 of the periodic table spans the seventh row of elements, from francium (Fr, atomic number 87) to oganesson (Og, atomic number 118). It includes the heaviest naturally occurring alkali metal, the complete actinide series, a range of transition metals, and the superheavy synthetic elements that complete the current known table.
