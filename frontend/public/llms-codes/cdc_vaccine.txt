[VAX] CDC Vaccine Categories -- The VAX entry defines the CDC Vaccine Categories, grouping immunizations by target population and disease prevention purpose. It serves as the umbrella classification for the recommended childhood, adolescent, and adult vaccines, helping providers select appropriate shots based on age, risk factors, and health status.
[VAX.01] Hepatitis B (HepB) -- The CDC Vaccine Schedule entry VAX.01 covers hepatitis B vaccination, recommending a three-dose series typically given at birth, 1-2 months of age, and a booster at 6-18 months. It is advised for all infants, unvaccinated children, adolescents, and adults at risk for HBV infection.
[VAX.02] Rotavirus (RV) -- VAX.02 denotes the rotavirus vaccine in the CDC Immunization Schedule. It is given orally to infants in a two- or three-dose series starting at 2 months of age to prevent severe gastroenteritis caused by rotavirus infection.
[VAX.03] DTaP (Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis) -- VAX.03 refers to the DTaP vaccine in the CDC's routine immunization schedule, a combination that protects against diphtheria, tetanus and pertussis. It is administered to infants and young children in a series of five doses, typically at 2, 4, 6, 15-18 months and 4-6 years of age.
[VAX.04] Haemophilus influenzae (Hib) -- The CDC recommends the Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) vaccine for infants beginning at two months of age, with subsequent doses at four and six months and a booster at twelve to fifteen months. It is given to children who have not completed the series and is essential for preventing severe Hib disease such as meningitis and pneumonia.
[VAX.05] Pneumococcal (PCV15/PCV20) -- Pneumococcal (PCV15/PCV20) is listed under CDC Vaccine Schedule code VAX.05 and outlines the recommended use of the newer 15-valent and 20-valent conjugate vaccines for infants, children, and adults, specifying age-appropriate dosing intervals, catch-up schedules, and indications for high-risk populations.
[VAX.06] Inactivated Poliovirus (IPV) -- The Inactivated Poliovirus Vaccine (IPV) is a killed-virus vaccine recommended for all children in the United States. It is administered as a four-dose series at ages 2 months, 4 months, 6-18 months, and a booster at 4-6 years, providing protection against poliomyelitis.
[VAX.07] Influenza (Annual) -- The CDC Vaccine Schedule entry VAX.07 covers the annual influenza vaccine, recommended for everyone six months of age and older. It includes the inactivated injectable form and the live-attenuated nasal spray, to be given before the start of the flu season to reduce illness, complications, and spread of the virus.
[VAX.08] MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella) -- VAX.08 designates the measles-mumps-rubella (MMR) vaccine in the CDC's immunization schedule. It is given as a two-dose series, the first dose at 12-15 months of age and a booster at 4-6 years, providing long-term protection against all three viral diseases.
[VAX.09] Varicella (VAR) -- The CDC Vaccine Schedule entry VAX.09 covers the varicella (chicken-pox) vaccine, recommending a two-dose series for children beginning at 12 months, with catch-up doses for older children and adolescents who have not been vaccinated or lack evidence of immunity. It notes that the vaccine is contraindicated in pregnant women, immunocompromised individuals, and those with severe allergic reactions to previous doses.
[VAX.10] Hepatitis A (HepA) -- VAX.10 refers to the recommended hepatitis A vaccination schedule, which includes a two-dose series administered six months apart for adults and children over one year of age, with accelerated regimens available for travelers or during outbreak control. The schedule also outlines catch-up dosing for those who have not completed the series and contraindications such as severe allergic reactions to the vaccine components.
[VAX.11] Meningococcal (MenACWY) -- The CDC Vaccine Schedule entry VAX.11 for Meningococcal (MenACWY) recommends routine immunization with a conjugate vaccine that protects against serogroups A, C, W, and Y. It is advised for all adolescents at 11-12 years of age with a booster at 16, and for high-risk individuals may be given earlier.
[VAX.12] HPV (Human Papillomavirus) -- VAX.12 designates the CDC-recommended HPV vaccine series, administered to adolescents and young adults to prevent infection with human papillomavirus types that cause cervical, anal, oropharyngeal and other cancers. The schedule calls for two doses if the series starts before age 15, or three doses for those beginning later or who are immunocompromised.
[VAX.13] Tdap (Adolescent/Adult) -- Tdap is a combined tetanus-diphtheria-pertussis vaccine used as a booster for adolescents and adults, replacing the Td booster in the routine schedule. It is routinely administered at age 11-12 years and then every 10 years, and is also recommended for each pregnancy to protect newborns from pertussis.
[VAX.14] COVID-19 -- VAX.14 in the CDC Vaccine Schedule designates the COVID-19 immunization series. It outlines the recommended primary series and booster doses for eligible populations, specifying age groups, interval timing between doses, and product-specific guidance to ensure optimal protection against SARS-CoV-2 infection.
[VAX.15] Shingles (Zoster) -- VAX.15 designates the CDC's recommended immunization for shingles, also called herpes zoster, in adults typically aged 50 years and older. The schedule calls for a two-dose series of the recombinant zoster vaccine administered two to six months apart, with an alternative single-dose live vaccine for those who cannot receive the recombinant product.
[VAX.16] RSV (Respiratory Syncytial) -- VAX.16 refers to the CDC's recommendation for Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) protection, including the administration of the monoclonal antibody nirsevimab for infants at high risk of severe disease and a pending RSV vaccine for adults 60 years and older, with timing aligned to seasonal RSV circulation.
[VAX.17] Mpox -- VAX.17 designates the CDC's Mpox vaccine schedule, recommending the JYNNEOS (MVA-BN) vaccine for individuals at risk of exposure and for post-exposure prophylaxis. The primary series consists of two doses administered four weeks apart, with a booster dose advised for certain high-risk groups after two years. The schedule also outlines timing for vaccination following a known exposure to help prevent infection.
