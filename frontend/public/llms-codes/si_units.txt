[SI] SI Unit Categories -- The SI entry groups all base and derived units into categories that form the worldwide standard for scientific and technical measurement. It defines seven base units-metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole and candela-and derives additional units through algebraic combinations of these fundamentals.
[SI.01] Metre (length) -- The metre is the SI base unit of length, defined as the distance light travels in vacuum during 1⁄299,792,458 of a second. It replaced earlier physical artifact definitions and provides a universal standard for measuring length worldwide.
[SI.02] Kilogram (mass) -- The kilogram is the SI base unit for measuring mass, symbol kg, and is defined by fixing the value of the Planck constant at 6.626 070 15 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s, linking it to fundamental physical constants. It is realized in practice through calibrated reference artifacts and quantum-based implementations such as the Kibble balance.
[SI.03] Second (time) -- The second (symbol s) is the SI base unit of time, defined as the duration of 9 192 631 770 periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the ground state of the cesium-133 atom. It provides the fundamental reference for all time measurements in the International System of Units.
[SI.04] Ampere (electric current) -- The ampere (symbol A) is the SI base unit of electric current, defined by the force exerted between two parallel conductors one metre apart that each carry a current of one ampere, producing a specified magnetic attraction. It serves as a fundamental measure for all electrical phenomena and underpins related units such as the coulomb and the volt.
[SI.05] Kelvin (temperature) -- Kelvin is the SI base unit of thermodynamic temperature, symbol K, defined by fixing the Boltzmann constant at 1.380649 × 10⁻²³ J·K⁻¹. It provides an absolute scale where 0 K is absolute zero, the point at which molecular motion ceases.
[SI.06] Mole (amount of substance) -- The mole is the SI base unit for amount of substance, defined as containing exactly 6.02214076 × 10²³ elementary entities such as atoms, molecules, ions or electrons. It links the macroscopic mass of a material to the number of constituent particles, enabling quantitative chemical calculations.
[SI.07] Candela (luminous intensity) -- The candela (symbol cd) is the SI base unit of luminous intensity, defined as the radiant power of a source emitting monochromatic light of frequency 540 × 10¹² hertz that produces a visual perception of one lumen per steradian. It quantifies the perceived brightness of light in a given direction and forms part of the International System of Units.
[SI.08] Radian (plane angle) -- The radian is the SI derived unit for plane angle, symbol rad. One radian corresponds to the angle formed when the length of an arc on a circle equals the radius of that circle, making it a dimensionless quantity expressed as the ratio of two lengths. It is the standard unit for angular measurement in mathematics, physics, and engineering.
[SI.09] Steradian (solid angle) -- The steradian is the SI derived unit used to quantify solid angle. One steradian subtends an area on the surface of a sphere that is equal to the square of the sphere's radius, corresponding to the angle formed by a conical surface that cuts out a unit-radius spherical cap. It is dimensionless, expressed in rad², and forms part of the International System of Units.
[SI.10] Hertz (frequency) -- The hertz (Hz) is the SI derived unit of frequency, defined as one cycle per second. It measures how many repetitions of a periodic event occur in one second, with one hertz representing a single oscillation in that time.
[SI.11] Newton (force) -- The newton, symbol N, is the SI derived unit of force. It is defined as the force required to accelerate a mass of one kilogram at a rate of one metre per second squared, equivalent to one kilogram-metre per second squared. It is named after Sir Isaac Newton for his laws of motion.
[SI.12] Pascal (pressure) -- The pascal (Pa) is the SI derived unit for pressure, defined as one newton of force applied uniformly over one square metre. It is used to express mechanical stress, fluid pressure, and atmospheric pressure in scientific, engineering, and meteorological measurements.
[SI.13] Joule (energy) -- The joule (J) is the SI derived unit of energy, work and heat. One joule equals the work done when a force of one newton moves an object a distance of one metre, or the energy transferred by one watt of power in one second.
[SI.14] Watt (power) -- The watt (symbol W) is the SI derived unit for power, defined as the rate of energy transfer equal to one joule per second. It quantifies how quickly work is done or heat is generated, and is used across physics, engineering, and everyday contexts such as electrical appliances.
[SI.15] Coulomb (charge) -- The coulomb, symbol C, is the SI unit of electric charge, defined as the amount of charge transferred by a steady current of one ampere flowing for one second. One coulomb corresponds to roughly 6.241 × 10^18 elementary charges such as electrons.
[SI.16] Volt (voltage) -- The volt is the SI derived unit for electric potential difference, electromotive force and electric pressure. One volt equals one joule of energy per coulomb of charge, or the potential that drives one ampere through a resistance of one ohm.
[SI.17] Ohm (resistance) -- The ohm (Ω) is the SI derived unit of electrical resistance, defined as the resistance that permits a constant current of one ampere when a voltage of one volt is applied across it. It is equivalent to one volt-per-ampere.
[SI.18] SI Prefixes (yocto to yotta) -- SI.18 lists the standard SI prefixes from the smallest, yocto (10⁻²⁴), through zepto, atto, femto, pico, nano, micro, milli, centi, deci, deca, hecto, kilo, mega, giga, tera, peta, exa, zetta
