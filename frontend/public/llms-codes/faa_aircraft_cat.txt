[FA] FAA Categories -- FA designates the FAA's broad "fixed-wing aircraft" category, covering all non-rotorcraft airplanes used in civil aviation. It groups these aircraft before they are further divided into specific classes such as single-engine land, multi-engine sea, and others.
[FA.01] Airplane - Single Engine Land (ASEL) -- FA.01 identifies the aircraft category and class of Single Engine Land airplanes, which are fixed-wing aircraft powered by one engine and designed to operate from land runways, encompassing the majority of light general-aviation aircraft.
[FA.02] Airplane - Multi Engine Land (AMEL) -- FA.02 identifies the Airplane - Multi Engine Land category in the FAA aircraft classification system, covering airplanes equipped with two or more reciprocating or turbine engines that operate from land runways. The class defines certification, airworthiness, and operational standards specific to multi-engine land airplanes.
[FA.03] Airplane - Single Engine Sea (ASES) -- This FAA category identifies an airplane equipped with a single engine that is designed for water operations, using floats or a hull for takeoff and landing on seas or lakes. It falls under the Airplane - Single Engine Sea class and is used for general aviation, training, and private transport on water.
[FA.04] Airplane - Multi Engine Sea (AMES) -- FA.04 identifies an Airplane - Multi Engine Sea (AMES), a multi-engine aircraft equipped with floats or a hull for water operations. It is classified under the FAA's Aircraft Category and Class system and must meet specific certification and operational standards for seaplane use.
[FA.05] Rotorcraft - Helicopter -- FA.05 identifies rotorcraft that are helicopters, aircraft that generate lift and thrust with one or more powered rotors and can take off, hover, and land vertically. The FAA uses this class to group helicopters for certification, registration and operational regulation.
[FA.06] Rotorcraft - Gyroplane -- FA.06 identifies the gyroplane within the FAA's Aircraft Category and Class system. It belongs to the rotorcraft category and refers to aircraft that achieve lift with an unpowered, autorotating rotor while a separate engine-driven propeller provides thrust, typically used for sport, recreation, or light utility missions.
[FA.07] Glider -- FA.07 identifies the glider category in the FAA Aircraft Category and Class system, covering unpowered aircraft that are launched by external means and remain aloft using aerodynamic lift alone. Gliders are regulated separately from powered airplanes, with distinct certification, equipment, and operational requirements.
[FA.08] Lighter-Than-Air - Airship -- FA.08 designates the FAA aircraft category for lighter-than-air vehicles, specifically airships. It includes all non-rigid (blimps), semi-rigid and rigid airships used for transport, surveillance, advertising or research, and determines the certification, operating rules and airworthiness standards applicable to these buoyant aircraft.
[FA.09] Lighter-Than-Air - Balloon -- FA.09 identifies the lighter-than-air balloon category in the FAA Aircraft Category and Class system. It covers all non-rigid lighter-than-air vehicles such as hot-air and gas balloons used for sport, commercial flight
[FA.10] Powered-Lift -- FA.10 denotes the powered-lift aircraft category, which includes vehicles that generate lift primarily through thrust from engines, such as tilt-rotor and VTOL aircraft. These aircraft combine features of helicopters and fixed-wing planes and are regulated under specific FAA standards.
[FA.11] Weight-Shift Control -- FA.11 designates aircraft that are controlled by shifting the pilot's weight relative to the wing, altering the center of gravity to change pitch and roll. This category includes hang gliders, trikes and similar ultralight vehicles that lack conventional control surfaces.
[FA.12] Powered Parachute -- FA.12 denotes powered parachutes, a light-sport aircraft category where a small engine and propeller are attached to a flexible parachute-type wing, providing lift for one or two occupants. The FAA classifies them separately from conventional airplanes and rotorcraft for certification and operational rules.
[FA.13] Sport Pilot Category -- The Sport Pilot Category is a certification tier that authorizes pilots to operate light-sport aircraft with a maximum takeoff weight of 1,320 lb (1,430 lb for seaplanes) and limits on speed, seating and performance, allowing reduced training requirements and a medical waiver. It is intended for recreational flying, flight training and certain commercial activities such as aerial photography under specific conditions.
[FA.14] Experimental Category -- The Experimental Category covers aircraft that are not type-certified for standard operations and are authorized for research and development, flight testing, showing, crew training, or other non-commercial uses. These aircraft operate under special conditions and restrictions set by the FAA, reflecting their experimental nature and limited certification status.
[FA.15] Unmanned Aircraft (UAS/Drone) -- FA.15 designates Unmanned Aircraft, also known as UAS or drones, as a separate category in the FAA's aircraft classification system. It includes remotely piloted or autonomous vehicles used for civil, commercial, or governmental purposes and subjects them to specific certification, operational and safety regulations distinct from manned aircraft.
