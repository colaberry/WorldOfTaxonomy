[RFC] RFC Categories -- The RFC classification groups documents published by the Internet Engineering Task Force into categories that indicate their status and purpose. Categories include Standards Track documents that become Internet Standards, Best Current Practice notes, Informational and Experimental RFCs, and Historic records of obsolete specifications. This system helps developers and network operators identify the relevance and authority of each RFC.
[RFC.AR1] Applications and Real-Time -- RFC.AR1 groups IETF documents that define protocols, mechanisms, and guidelines for end-user applications and real-time communications. It covers standards such as SIP, RTP, and other transport or session protocols that enable low-latency, interactive services like voice, video, and online gaming. The category emphasizes functional behavior for application-layer and timing-critical scenarios rather than core network infrastructure.
[RFC.BCP] Best Current Practice -- RFC.BCP identifies IETF documents that are designated as Best Current Practice, offering the most reliable and widely accepted guidance on protocols, deployment, and operational procedures. These publications are intended to be followed by network engineers and developers as the current standard for implementation.
[RFC.DS] Draft Standard -- RFC.DS designates a Draft Standard, a maturity level in the IETF RFC lifecycle indicating a protocol or specification that is stable, has been implemented by at least two independent parties, and is ready for broad deployment, but has not yet reached the final Internet Standard status. This classification follows the Proposed Standard stage and precedes the full Standard designation.
[RFC.EXP] Experimental -- RFC.EXP denotes RFCs classified as Experimental, indicating that the document proposes a concept, protocol, or mechanism that is not yet stable, widely implemented, or officially recommended. These papers are intended for testing, research, or exploration and may be revised or withdrawn as the ideas evolve.
[RFC.HST] Historic -- RFC.HST designates historic Request for Comments that have been obsoleted, superseded, or otherwise withdrawn and are no longer recommended for implementation. These documents are retained solely for archival and reference purposes, illustrating the evolution of Internet standards.
[RFC.INF] Informational -- RFC.INF denotes Informational RFCs, which provide general information, observations, or guidelines but do not define Internet standards. These documents are published by the IETF for reference or educational purposes and are not subject to the strict standard-track consensus process.
[RFC.INT] Internet -- RFC.INT is a category in the IETF RFC index that groups documents dealing with Internet-wide standards, policies, and informational material about the global network. It includes RFCs that discuss overarching architecture, governance, or operational guidelines rather than specific protocol specifications.
[RFC.OPS] Operations and Management -- RFC.OPS covers specifications related to the operation, monitoring, and administration of network services and devices. It includes protocols for configuration, fault detection, performance measurement, and management interfaces, enabling automated and interoperable control of internet scale systems.
[RFC.PS] Proposed Standard -- RFC.PS denotes a Proposed Standard, the initial stage of an IETF standard after a specification has passed rigorous review and is deemed stable and well-documented. It signals that the protocol is ready for implementation and real-world testing, serving as the first formal step toward broader adoption and eventual elevation to Draft and Internet Standard status.
[RFC.RTG] Routing -- RFC.RTG designates the IETF Request for Comments documents that define routing protocols, algorithms, and inter-domain routing policies. It includes standards and informational RFCs covering IP forwarding, path selection, and network topology management.
[RFC.SEC] Security -- RFC.SEC groups Internet-standard documents that define, analyze, or prescribe security mechanisms for network protocols, including encryption, authentication, key management, and secure transport, as well as recommendations for threat mitigation and privacy protection. It serves as the primary reference for designers and implementers seeking vetted security solutions within the IETF framework.
[RFC.STD] Internet Standard -- RFC.STD is the designation for an Internet Standard, a stable, well-vetted protocol or service that has passed thorough review and is widely implemented, forming part of the core standards track of the IETF.
[RFC.TSV] Transport -- RFC.TSV groups IETF RFCs that specify transport-layer protocols and related mechanisms, covering standards such as TCP, UDP, SCTP and their extensions, performance guidelines, and security considerations for end-to-end data delivery across IP networks.
[RFC.WIT] Web and Internet Transport -- RFC.WIT groups IETF RFCs that define protocols and standards governing web and internet transport, such as HTTP, TCP, UDP and related extensions. The category embraces documents on data carriage, framing, congestion control, security and interoperability for global network communications.
