[IUCN] IUCN Red List Categories -- The IUCN Red List Categories are a globally recognized system for assessing the extinction risk of species, ranging from Least Concern to Extinct, and are used by governments, NGOs and researchers to guide conservation priorities.
[IUCN.C1] Criterion A - Population Reduction -- Criterion A of the IUCN Red List evaluates species based on the magnitude of past, projected, or inferred population reduction over a specified time period, typically 10 years or three generations. Thresholds of 90 %, 70 %, and 50 % decline correspond to Critically Endangered, Endangered, and Vulnerable status, respectively, while lower reductions may qualify a species for Near-Threatened or Least Concern.
[IUCN.C2] Criterion B - Geographic Range -- The IUCN.C2 entry defines Criterion B, which evaluates a species' risk of extinction based on its geographic range. It considers extent of occurrence, area of occupancy, and whether the population is severely fragmented, continuing to decline, or subject to extreme fluctuations, applying quantitative thresholds for threatened categories.
[IUCN.C3] Criterion C - Small Population -- Criterion C assesses species with a small total number of mature individuals that are undergoing a continuing decline, contributing to their placement in a threatened category. It applies when the population is below defined thresholds (e.g., ≤20 000 for Vulnerable, ≤10 000 for Endangered, ≤5 000 for Critically Endangered) and meets sub-criteria concerning the rate of decline, population fragmentation or extreme fluctuations.
[IUCN.C4] Criterion D - Very Small/Restricted -- Criterion D in the IUCN Red List categories applies to species with an extremely small population size or a very limited geographic range, making them prone to rapid decline from stochastic events or human impacts. It is used to qualify taxa as Vulnerable, Endangered or Critically Endangered when the numbers or area fall below specific thresholds.
[IUCN.C5] Criterion E - Quantitative Analysis -- Criterion E employs quantitative models that calculate the probability of a species going extinct within a specified time frame, using data such as population trends, demographics and environmental variables. If the analysis predicts a ≥50 % chance of extinction within 10 years or three generations, the species qualifies as Critically Endangered; lower probabilities correspond to Endangered or Vulnerable classifications. This approach provides a statistically based pathway for assigning Red List categories.
[IUCN.CR] Critically Endangered (CR) -- Critically Endangered (CR) is the highest risk category in the IUCN Red List, indicating a species faces an extremely high probability of extinction in the wild. A taxon meets this status when it satisfies quantitative criteria such as a population decline of at least 80 % over ten years or three generations, a severely restricted geographic range, or a very small population size. This designation signals an urgent need for conservation action.
[IUCN.DD] Data Deficient (DD) -- Data Deficient (DD) is an IUCN Red List category assigned to species for which there is inadequate information to assess their risk of extinction. It signals that more research is needed, but does not imply that the species is not threatened.
[IUCN.EN] Endangered (EN) -- Endangered (EN) is a high-risk IUCN Red List category indicating that a species faces a very high probability of extinction in the wild. It applies when populations have declined sharply, have very restricted ranges, or meet quantitative criteria showing severe threats to their long-term survival.
[IUCN.EW] Extinct in the Wild (EW) -- Extinct in the Wild (EW) denotes a species that no longer survives in its natural habitats, with all known individuals existing only in captivity, cultivation, or artificial environments. It is one step away from total extinction, indicating that while the species persists under human care, no self-sustaining wild populations remain.
[IUCN.EX] Extinct (EX) -- Extinct (EX) is the IUCN Red List category assigned when there is no reasonable doubt that the last individual of a species has died. It follows exhaustive surveys in known and expected habitats and represents the highest level of threat on the list.
[IUCN.LC] Least Concern (LC) -- Least Concern (LC) is an IUCN Red List category indicating a species has been evaluated and found to be widespread, abundant, and facing no immediate threat of extinction. Species classified as LC do not meet the criteria for any of the threatened categories and are considered at low risk under current conditions.
[IUCN.NE] Not Evaluated (NE) -- The IUCN.NE code indicates a species that has not been evaluated against the Red List criteria and therefore has no formal conservation status. It may be listed because it has not yet been assessed, lacks sufficient data, or has not been considered for inclusion in the Red List.
[IUCN.NT] Near Threatened (NT) -- Near-Threatened species are those that do not yet meet the criteria for Vulnerable but are close to qualifying or likely to qualify in the near future. The designation signals that current declines or threats could push the species into a higher-risk category if trends continue.
[IUCN.VU] Vulnerable (VU) -- Vulnerable (VU) is an IUCN Red List category indicating that a species faces a high risk of extinction in the wild because of rapid population declines, restricted geographic range, or other significant threats. It stands one step below Endangered and above Near-Threatened in the hierarchy of conservation status.
