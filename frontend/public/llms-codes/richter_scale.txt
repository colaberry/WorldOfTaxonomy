[RS] Earthquake Magnitudes -- RS is the code used to denote earthquake magnitudes measured on the Richter scale, covering the conventional range from low-level tremors to moderate events. It provides a quantitative index of seismic energy release, facilitating comparison across different locations and times.
[RS.01] Micro (< 2.0) -- RS.01 denotes a micro earthquake with a magnitude below 2.0 on the Richter scale. Such events release very low energy and are generally undetectable without sensitive seismographs, causing no noticeable effects at the surface.
[RS.02] Minor (2.0-3.9) -- RS.02 classifies earthquakes with magnitudes ranging from 2.0 to 3.9. These tremors are considered minor, usually felt by a small number of people near the epicenter and rarely cause any structural damage.
[RS.03] Light (4.0-4.9) -- RS.03 identifies a light earthquake with a magnitude ranging from 4.0 to 4.9 on the Richter scale. These tremors are usually felt by people, may cause small, localized damage such as minor cracks in walls or fallen objects, but rarely result in significant structural harm.
[RS.04] Moderate (5.0-5.9) -- RS.04 classifies earthquakes with magnitudes from 5.0 to 5.9 as moderate. Such quakes typically produce noticeable shaking and may cause minor to moderate damage to buildings, especially those lacking seismic reinforcement, but they rarely lead to widespread casualties.
[RS.05] Strong (6.0-6.9) -- RS.05 identifies earthquakes with a magnitude ranging from 6.0 to 6.9, classified as strong. Such events can cause considerable damage to vulnerable buildings, disrupt infrastructure, and be felt over a wide region.
[RS.06] Major (7.0-7.9) -- RS.06 identifies earthquakes with a moment magnitude between 7.0 and 7.9, classified as major. Such events typically produce widespread destruction, can collapse poorly built structures, and are felt over large regions, often triggering secondary hazards like landslides.
[RS.07] Great (8.0+) -- RS.07 identifies earthquakes with a moment magnitude of 8.0 or higher, classified as Great. Such events cause severe damage over large areas, can generate widespread destruction, ground rupture, and secondary hazards like tsunamis.
[RS.08] Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw) -- RS.08 denotes the Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw), a quantitative measure of earthquake size based on seismic moment rather than amplitude. It provides a uniform, scale-independent magnitude applicable to all earthquake sizes, superseding the Richter scale for large events.
[RS.09] Surface Wave Magnitude (Ms) -- RS.09 denotes the Surface Wave Magnitude, abbreviated Ms, which quantifies an earthquake's size using the amplitude of Rayleigh or Love surface waves recorded on seismographs. It is most reliable for moderate to large events (generally magnitude 5.5-8.0) and complements body-wave and moment-magnitude scales.
[RS.10] Body Wave Magnitude (mb) -- RS.10 Body Wave Magnitude (mb) quantifies the size of an earthquake using the amplitude of high-frequency P-waves recorded by seismographs, typically at distances of 100-600 km from the epicenter. It is most reliable for moderate events (mb ≈ 4-6) and helps assess source depth and faulting characteristics, complementing other scales such as Mw or ML.
