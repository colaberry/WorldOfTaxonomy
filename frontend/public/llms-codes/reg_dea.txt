[registration] DEA Registration Requirements -- The DEA registration requirements outline the qualifications, application procedures, and ongoing obligations for individuals and entities that handle, prescribe, or dispense controlled substances. Registrants must submit a completed DEA Form 224, maintain accurate records, and renew their registration biennially, while complying with security, reporting, and inspections mandated by 21 CFR Parts 1301-1321. Non-compliance can result in suspension, revocation, or civil and criminal penalties.
[reg_manufacturer] Manufacturer Registration -- The reg_manufacturer entry designates the mandatory DEA registration for any person or entity that manufactures, processes, or imports a Schedule I-V controlled substance. Registrants must maintain security, recordkeeping, and reporting requirements and renew their registration every two years.
[reg_pharmacy] Pharmacy Registration -- The reg_pharmacy entry designates the DEA requirement that any pharmacy engaged in the receipt, storage, dispensing, or distribution of controlled substances must obtain a DEA registration. This registration authorizes the pharmacy to handle schedules I-V drugs in accordance with 21 CFR Parts 1301-1321 and ensures compliance with federal monitoring, record-keeping, and security standards.
[reg_practitioner] Practitioner Registration (1301.11) -- The DEA requires any individual who prescribes, administers, or dispenses controlled substances to obtain a practitioner registration under 21 CFR 1301.11. Registration involves submitting an application, meeting eligibility criteria, and maintaining compliance through periodic renewals and record-keeping obligations.
[reporting] Reporting and Recordkeeping -- The "reporting" entry outlines the DEA's requirements for registrants to keep accurate records of all controlled-substance activities and to submit periodic inventory, acquisition, disposition and theft reports. Compliance ensures the government can monitor lawful handling and detect diversion, and failure to maintain or file the required documentation can result in enforcement action.
[schedule_i] Schedule I - High Abuse Potential, No Accepted Medical Use -- Schedule I substances are drugs with a high potential for abuse and no currently accepted medical use in treatment in the United States. They are considered unsafe even under medical supervision and are prohibited from prescription or distribution, with examples including heroin, LSD, and MDMA.
[schedule_ii] Schedule II - High Abuse Potential, Accepted Medical Use -- Schedule II substances have a high potential for abuse that can cause severe psychological or physical dependence, yet they are recognized for legitimate medical use and may be prescribed only under strict controls. Examples include oxycodone, fentanyl, methamphetamine, and Adderall, all subject to rigorous monitoring.
[schedule_iii] Schedule III - Moderate Abuse Potential -- Schedule III substances are those with a moderate potential for abuse, lower than Schedule I or II, and recognized medical use in the United States. They may lead to limited physical or psychological dependence, and include certain anabolic steroids, moderate-strength barbiturates, and some opioid formulations such as buprenorphine.
[schedule_iv] Schedule IV - Low Abuse Potential -- Schedule IV substances are drugs that have a low potential for abuse compared with substances in Schedules I-III, possess accepted medical uses, and may lead to limited physical or psychological dependence. They are regulated but are more readily prescribed than higher-schedule drugs.
[schedule_v] Schedule V - Lowest Abuse Potential -- Schedule V is the lowest-level category for controlled substances under the DEA's schedule system, covering drugs with a minimal risk of abuse relative to other schedules. It includes medications such as certain cough suppressants containing limited amounts of codeine and anti-diarrheal agents that are approved for medical use. These substances are subject to the least stringent regulatory controls but still require tracking and prescribing oversight.
[sec_employee] Employee Screening (1301.90-93) -- The "Employee Screening" section (1301.90-93) outlines the DEA's requirements for background checks, licensing verification and other screening procedures that must be completed before a person can handle or dispense controlled substances. It specifies the records that must be maintained and the circumstances under which screening must be repeated or updated. This ensures that only qualified, trustworthy individuals have access to regulated drugs.
[sec_physical] Physical Security Requirements (1301.71-76) -- The DEA's Physical Security Requirements (1301.71-76) set standards for safeguarding controlled substances in any facility, mandating locked storage cabinets, alarm systems, limited access areas, and regular inventory controls. Compliance ensures that drugs are protected from theft, loss, or diversion while maintaining accurate record-keeping.
[security] Security and Control Requirements -- The security entry outlines the mandatory physical safeguards, storage, record-keeping, and monitoring procedures that registrants must follow to prevent diversion, theft, or loss of controlled substances, detailing requirements for vaults, alarm systems, access controls, and periodic inventories.
[si_cannabis] Schedule I Cannabinoids (marijuana - federal) -- Schedule I cannabinoids, including marijuana, are classified by the DEA as substances with a high potential for abuse, no currently accepted medical use in treatment in the United States, and a lack of accepted safety for use under medical supervision. This highest level of restriction places them in the most tightly controlled category of the federal drug schedule.

[si_hallucinogens] Schedule I Hallucinogens (e.g., LSD, psilocybin) -- Schedule I hallucinogens are classified as having a high potential for abuse, no accepted medical use in treatment in the United States, and lack safety under medical supervision; they are illegal to manufacture, distribute, or possess. Examples include lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD) and the naturally occurring compound psilocybin found in certain mushrooms.
[sii_depressants] Schedule II Depressants (e.g., pentobarbital) -- Schedule II depressants are substances with a high potential for abuse that can lead to severe psychological or physical dependence, but they also have recognized medical uses. Pentobarbital, for example, is prescribed for anesthesia and certain seizure disorders, yet its distribution is tightly regulated to prevent diversion and illicit use.
[siii_anabolic] Schedule III Anabolic Steroids -- Schedule III anabolic steroids are controlled substances that have an accepted medical use but a lower potential for abuse and dependence than Schedule I or II drugs. They include synthetic testosterone derivatives used to promote muscle growth and are regulated as prescription-only drugs with strict record-keeping and refill limits.
[siii_compounds] Schedule III Compounds (e.g., buprenorphine, ketamine) -- Schedule III compounds are substances with a moderate to low potential for physical or psychological dependence, placed in the DEA's Controlled Substance Schedules.
[sii_opioids] Schedule II Opioids (e.g., fentanyl, oxycodone, morphine) -- Schedule II opioids are prescription drugs with a high potential for abuse that can lead to severe physical or psychological dependence, yet they are recognized for legitimate medical use in pain management. Examples include fentanyl, oxycodone and morphine, which are subject to strict prescribing controls, limited refills, and rigorous record-keeping requirements under DEA regulations.
[sii_stimulants] Schedule II Stimulants (e.g., amphetamine, methylphenidate) -- Schedule II stimulants are substances with a high potential for abuse that can lead to severe psychological or physical dependence, yet they have accepted medical uses. Examples include amphetamine and methylphenidate, which are prescribed for attention-deficit disorders and certain sleep disorders under strict regulatory controls.

[si_stimulants] Schedule I Stimulants (e.g., MDMA) -- Schedule I stimulants are drugs that the DEA classifies as having a high potential for abuse, no currently accepted medical use in treatment in the United States, and a lack of recognized safety for use under medical supervision; examples include MDMA and related compounds.
[siv_benzo] Schedule IV Benzodiazepines (e.g., alprazolam, diazepam) -- Schedule IV benzodiazepines are prescription sedatives with a relatively low risk of abuse compared with higher schedules, though they can still lead to dependence and misuse. They are used medically to treat anxiety, insomnia, seizures, and muscle spasms, and typical examples include alprazolam and diazepam.
[sv_cough] Schedule V Cough Preparations -- Schedule V cough preparations are medicines that contain small quantities of narcotic ingredients, such as codeine, and are used to relieve coughing. They are subject to the lowest level of DEA control, may be dispensed with a prescription and require limited record-keeping compared with higher schedules.
