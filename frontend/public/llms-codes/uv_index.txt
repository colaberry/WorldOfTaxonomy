[UV] UV Index Levels -- The UV entry defines categories of the solar ultraviolet radiation intensity measured on a scale from 0 to 11+. It provides standard level descriptions
[UV.01] Low (0-2) -- UV.01 denotes the "Low" UV Index category, covering values from 0 to 2. In this range solar radiation poses minimal risk for most people, requiring only basic protection such as sunglasses, sunscreen if needed, and staying hydrated.
[UV.02] Moderate (3-5) -- UV.02 denotes a moderate ultraviolet radiation level, with an index ranging from 3 to 5. At this range, sunlight can cause skin damage after prolonged exposure, so sunscreen, protective clothing, and limiting time outdoors during peak sun are advised.
[UV.03] High (6-7) -- UV.03 denotes a high UV level on the Global Solar UV Index, covering index values 6 to 7. At this range sunburn can occur within 15-30 minutes of unprotected exposure, and precautions such as sunscreen, hats, and limiting time outdoors are recommended.

[UV.05] Extreme (11+) -- UV.05 denotes the Extreme category of the Global Solar UV Index, representing values of 11 or higher. At this level, the ultraviolet radiation is exceptionally intense, causing rapid skin and eye damage, and unprotected exposure can lead to severe burns within minutes. Immediate protective measures such as sunscreen SPF 50+, protective clothing, sunglasses, and limiting outdoor time are strongly advised.
[UV.06] UVA (315-400 nm) -- UV.06 identifies the UVA portion of solar ultraviolet radiation, spanning wavelengths from 315 to 400 nm. This longest UV band penetrates deep into the skin, contributing to photo-aging, DNA damage, and some skin cancers while largely bypassing the ozone layer.
[UV.07] UVB (280-315 nm) -- UV.07 designates the UVB portion of solar radiation, covering wavelengths from 280 to 315 nm. This band contributes to sunburn, vitamin-D production and other biological effects, and is assessed in the Global Solar UV Index to gauge medium to high health risk under strong sunlight.
[UV.08] UVC (100-280 nm) -- UVC (100-280 nm) refers to the shortest ultraviolet wavelengths, which are almost completely absorbed by atmospheric ozone and therefore do not reach the Earth's surface under normal conditions. Though it does not affect the surface UV index, UVC is highly germicidal and can cause severe biological damage if exposure occurs directly.
[UV.09] Erythemal Action Spectrum -- The erythemal action spectrum specifies how efficiently each wavelength of ultraviolet radiation produces erythema, or sunburn, on human skin. By assigning greater weighting to the shorter, more harmful UV wavelengths, it provides the biological weighting function used to calculate the Global Solar UV Index and to assess the risk of skin damage from solar exposure.
