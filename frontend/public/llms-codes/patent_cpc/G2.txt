[G21] NUCLEONICS -- G21 covers technologies and processes involving nucleon-based phenomena such as nuclear reactions, particle accelerators, and related instrumentation. It includes methods for generating, controlling, or detecting neutrons, protons, and other nucleons, as well as applications in energy production, medical therapy, and research.
[G21B] FUSION REACTORS -- **Definition:** This place covers:  Reactors in which energy release is caused by the controlled fusion of atomic nuclei, namely:  Thermonuclear fusion reactors.  Low-temperature nuclear fusion reactors, e.g. alleged cold fusion reactors.  **Limiting references (this place does not cover):** - Uncontrollable fusion reactions; applications thereof -> G21J  **Glossary:** - Confinement: Controlled compression of fusion nuclei to overcome their electrostatic repulsion. - Fusion: Combination of two or more nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, a by-product of which is the release of energy. - Thermonuclear: Relating to fusion at high temperatures.

[G21C] NUCLEAR REACTORS -- **Definition:** This place covers:  All aspects related to nuclear fission reactors and their components, i.e. all aspects related to the reactors per se, in particular all parts strictly related to the primary cooling circuit of the reactor, as well as monitoring or testing of nuclear fission reactor parameters and components, arrangements for handling nuclear fission reactor components or material and apparatuses and processes adapted to manufacture nuclear fission reactors.  **Limiting references (this place does not cover):** - Nuclear fusion reactors -> G21B - Nuclear explosives and their application -> G21J  **Glossary:** - Core: The central region of a reactor containing fuel and moderator elements, where nuclear fission occurs. - Moderator: Material used in a thermal nuclear reactor to reduce the velocity of neutrons produced by nuclear fission in order to facilitate chain reaction. - Shield: Means surrounding a reactor to prevent radiation damage to operators etc.
[G21C 1/00] Reactor types -- **Definition:** This place covers:  Nuclear fission reactors characterized by their type.  Main division is made between fast neutron driven fission and moderated neutron drive fission reactors, the reactors being either critical or subcritical, loop-type or integral.  Dedicated sections are defined for subcritical and integral reactors.
[G21C 1/02] Fast fission reactors, i.e. reactors not using a moderator -- Fast fission reactors are nuclear reactors that achieve fission chain reactions without a neutron-moderating material, relying on high-energy (fast) neutrons to sustain the process. This design enables efficient breeding of fissile material and can improve fuel utilization compared with moderated reactor types.
[G21C 1/022] characterised by the design or properties of the core -- G21C 1/022 is a CPC subgroup for nuclear reactors that are classified according to the design or properties of the core, such as geometry, fuel arrangement, moderation, and materials, rather than by overall reactor type or external features. This grouping captures patents focusing on core-specific innovations that affect performance, safety or efficiency.
[G21C 1/024] where the core is divided in zones with fuel and zones with breeding material -- The entry G21C 1/024 refers to nuclear reactor designs that partition the core into distinct zones, some containing fissile fuel and others containing breeding material that converts fertile isotopes into additional fissile fuel during operation. This zoning enhances fuel utilization and can improve the reactor's neutron economy.
[G21C 1/026] Reactors not needing refuelling, i.e. reactors of the type breed-and-burn, e.g. travelling or deflagration wave reactors or seed-blanket reactors -- G21C 1/026 covers nuclear reactor designs that operate without on-line refuelling, employing a breed-and-burn fuel cycle. Examples include travelling-wave, deflagration-wave and seed-blanket reactors where the core self-sustains fission as the burn front advances.

[G21C 1/088] Inherently safe boiling water reactors -- The CPC subclass G21C 1/088 covers designs of boiling-water nuclear reactors that achieve safety through inherent, passive features rather than active controls, such as self-regulating reactivity, natural circulation cooling, and materials that limit accident progression. The classification applies to arrangements that convert the kinetic energy of beta radiation into electric charge without intermediate converters, enabling direct generation of electrical power from isotopic decay.
[G21H 1/04] Cells using secondary emission induced by alpha radiation, beta radiation, or gamma radiation -- A cell that generates electricity by exploiting secondary emissions triggered when alpha particles, beta particles, or gamma rays from a radioactive source strike a material, causing the emission of electrons or other charged particles that are harvested as electrical current. This technology is used in devices that convert nuclear decay directly into usable power.
[G21H 1/06] Cells wherein radiation is applied to the junction of different semiconductor materials -- Cells that generate electricity by directing radiation onto the junction of two different semiconductor materials, typically using beta particles from a radioactive source to create charge carriers at the interface. This arrangement exploits the semiconductor junction's response to ionising radiation to produce a continuous electric current, often employed in long-life nuclear or betavoltaic power sources.
[G21H 1/08] Cells in which radiation ionises a gas in the presence of a junction of two dissimilar metals, i.e. contact potential difference cells -- G21H 1/08 covers cells that generate electricity by using ionising radiation to ionise a gas located at the junction of two dissimilar metals, creating a contact potential difference. These devices belong to the class of arrangements for obtaining electrical energy from radioactive sources, often referred to as nuclear or betavoltaic batteries.
[G21H 1/10] Cells in which radiation heats a thermoelectric junction or a thermionic converter -- **Definition:** This place covers:  Cells in which radiation of disintegration heat heats a thermoelectric junction or a thermionic converter.
[G21H 1/103] Cells provided with thermo-electric generators -- G21H 1/103 covers cells that incorporate thermoelectric generators to convert heat produced by radioactive decay into electricity. It includes designs where a radioactive source is integrated with thermocouple or thermopile elements within the cell to provide a self-sustaining power supply.
[G21H 1/106] Cells provided with thermionic generators -- Cells provided with thermionic generators are sealed units that convert the heat released by radioactive decay into electric power using thermionic emission. The classification covers designs that integrate a thermionic converter directly into the cell to harvest energy from embedded isotopic sources.
[G21H 1/12] Cells using conversion of the radiation into light combined with subsequent photoelectric conversion into electric energy -- A cell that first converts radiation emitted by a radioactive isotope into light and then uses a photovoltaic element to turn that light into electricity, forming a radioluminescent power source for generating electrical energy.
[G21H 3/00] Arrangements for direct conversion of radiation energy from radioactive sources into forms of energy other than electric energy, e.g. light -- This CPC subgroup covers devices and methods that directly transform the radiation emitted by radioactive materials into non-electric energy such as light, heat or mechanical work. It includes arrangements where the radiation is absorbed by a phosphor, luminescent material or other transducer to produce the desired output without intermediate electrical conversion.

[G21H 5/00] Applications of radiation from radioactive sources or arrangements therefor, not otherwise provided for -- This subgroup covers uses of radiation emitted by radioactive sources and the equipment designed to deliver that radiation when the application does not fall into a more specific category. It includes medical, industrial and scientific implementations that are not otherwise classified, such as novel irradiation processes or custom radiation delivery systems.
[G21H 5/02] as tracers -- G21H 5/02 relates to the use of radioactive substances as tracers, employing emitted radiation to follow the movement or distribution of materials in physical, chemical or biological systems. The entry covers methods where isotopic labeling or radiation detection is used for tracking, without being tied to a specific application.
[G21H 7/00] Use of effects of cosmic radiation -- G21H 7/00 covers methods and devices that exploit the effects of cosmic radiation, such as using high-energy particles from space to generate power, induce nuclear reactions, or alter materials for industrial or scientific purposes. The classification includes systems that capture, convert or otherwise utilize cosmic-ray energy as a source.
