[sec_101] Sec 101 - Objectives (Fishable/Swimmable Waters Goal) -- Section 101 of the Clean Water Act sets the primary objective of restoring and maintaining the chemical, physical, and biological integrity of the nation's waters so they are fishable and swimmable, establishing a national goal for water quality that guides regulatory and pollution-control actions.
[sec_104] Sec 104 - Research, Investigations, and Training -- Section 104 of the Clean Water Act authorizes the EPA and other federal agencies to conduct research, investigations, and training related to water pollution control, including studies on pollutant sources, treatment technologies, and the effectiveness of regulations. The provision also supports cooperative programs with states, tribes, and local entities to improve water quality monitoring and management.
[sec_201] Sec 201 - Purpose of Construction Grants -- Section 201 of the Clean Water Act establishes the purpose of construction grants, authorizing federal funding to help states, municipalities and other eligible entities build or upgrade wastewater treatment works and related infrastructure, thereby reducing water pollution, protecting public health and preserving the quality of the nation's waters.
[sec_301] Sec 301 - Effluent Limitations -- Section 301 of the Clean Water Act establishes the authority for the EPA to set and enforce technology-based effluent limitations for point source discharges into U.S. waters, requiring permits that specify maximum pollutant concentrations, monitoring, and reporting to protect water quality.
[sec_302] Sec 302 - Water Quality Related Effluent Limitations -- Section 302 of the Clean Water Act establishes water-quality-related effluent limitations for point sources, requiring permits that set maximum pollutant discharges based on water-quality standards and applicable technology benchmarks.
[sec_303] Sec 303 - Water Quality Standards and TMDLs -- Section 303 of the Clean Water Act directs states to establish water quality standards that define designated uses, set criteria to protect those uses, and require the development of Total Maximum Daily Loads for pollutants that impair waters, ensuring that impaired waters are restored to meet designated uses.
[sec_304] Sec 304 - Information and Guidelines -- Section 304 of the Clean Water Act directs the EPA to develop and issue information and guidelines on water-quality criteria, technology standards, and pollutant monitoring, and to make these resources publicly available to support state and tribal implementation of the Act. It also requires periodic review and updating of the guidelines to reflect new scientific and technical knowledge.
[sec_306] Sec 306 - National Standards of Performance (New Sources) -- Section 306 obligates EPA to set national performance standards that limit pollutant discharges from new sources of water pollution, targeting specific industrial categories with technically feasible, cost-effective limits. The standards must be adopted and enforced to protect water quality, and compliance is mandatory for any facility that is newly-constructed or newly-modified.
[sec_307] Sec 307 - Toxic and Pretreatment Standards -- Section 307 of the Clean Water Act authorizes the EPA to establish technology-based standards for toxic pollutant discharges and to require pretreatment of industrial wastewater before it enters publicly owned treatment works. The rule aims to protect surface waters by limiting harmful chemicals and ensuring that dischargers meet enforceable effluent limits.
[sec_311] Sec 311 - Oil and Hazardous Substance Liability (Spill Prevention) -- Section 311 of the Clean Water Act imposes civil liability on persons responsible for discharging oil or hazardous substances into navigable waters, requiring them to obtain permits, take preventive measures, and demonstrate financial responsibility for cleanup and damages. It also authorizes the EPA to issue orders for remediation and assess civil penalties for non-compliance.
[sec_316] Sec 316 - Thermal Discharges and Cooling Water Intakes -- Section 316 of the Clean Water Act authorizes the EPA to establish performance standards for the discharge of heated or cooled water and for the design and operation of cooling water intake structures, aiming to protect aquatic life and water quality. It addresses thermal pollution from industrial processes and requires facilities to minimize temperature changes and entrainment of organisms. Compliance may involve technology upgrades, monitoring, and permitting.
[sec_319] Sec 319 - Nonpoint Source Management Programs -- Section 319 of the Clean Water Act establishes a grant program to help states develop and implement nonpoint source pollution management plans, targeting runoff from agriculture, forestry, urban development and other diffuse sources. It requires states to identify impaired waters, set water quality goals, and create best-management practices and outreach activities to reduce pollutant loads and protect water quality.
[sec_401] Sec 401 - Certification (State Water Quality Certification) -- Section 401 of the Clean Water Act requires that any federally-licensed or -funded project likely to discharge pollutants into navigable waters obtain a certification from the state confirming compliance with state water-quality standards. The certification acts as a condition for the federal permit, giving states oversight of the project's impact on water quality.
[sec_402] Sec 402 - National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) -- Section 402 of the Clean Water Act creates the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System, a permit program that regulates the discharge of pollutants from point sources into U.S. waters. It requires permit holders to meet effluent limits, monitor releases, and report compliance, with enforcement authority vested in the EPA and authorized states.
[sec_404] Sec 404 - Permits for Dredged or Fill Material (Wetlands) -- Section 404 of the Clean Water Act authorizes the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers to issue permits for the discharge of dredged or fill material into waters of the United States, including wetlands, to ensure that such activities do not significantly degrade water quality or impair ecosystem functions. The permitting process requires an assessment of alternatives, mitigation, and public involvement before approval.
[sec_405] Sec 405 - Disposal of Sewage Sludge (Biosolids) -- Section 405 of the Clean Water Act sets national standards for the disposal and use of sewage sludge, commonly called biosolids, on land or in water. It requires EPA certification that treatment methods meet pollutant limits, mandates permits for disposal sites, and establishes monitoring, reporting, and public notification requirements to protect water quality and public health.
[sec_501] Sec 501 - Administration (General EPA Authority) -- Section 501 of the Clean Water Act grants the EPA broad authority to administer the program, including developing regulations, issuing permits, and enforcing water-quality standards. It establishes the agency's power to oversee compliance, conduct investigations, and take necessary actions to protect the nation's waters.
[sec_505] Sec 505 - Citizen Suits -- Section 505 authorizes private citizens to bring civil actions against parties alleged to violate the Clean Water Act, including the EPA or state agencies, to enforce compliance, recover damages, and obtain injunctive relief. It empowers individuals to serve as private attorneys general, facilitating public participation in water-pollution enforcement.
[sec_509] Sec 509 - Judicial Review -- Section 509 provides for judicial review of permits, orders and decisions issued under the Clean Water Act, allowing aggrieved parties to challenge agency actions in federal court.
[sec_601] Sec 601 - State Water Pollution Control Revolving Fund (SRF) -- Section 601 creates the State Water Pollution Control Revolving Fund, a federal program that offers low-interest loans and matching grants to states for constructing, upgrading or expanding wastewater treatment works and other water-pollution control projects, helping them meet Clean Water Act standards.
[title_1] Title I - Research and Related Programs -- Title I of the Clean Water Act authorizes federal support for scientific research, monitoring, and development of new technologies aimed at preventing water pollution and improving water quality, including grants to universities, state agencies, and other institutions. It also funds related programs that provide technical assistance and information dissemination to enhance water resource management across the United States.
[title_2] Title II - Grants for Construction of Treatment Works -- Title II of the Clean Water Act authorizes federal grant assistance to states, local governments and other entities for the design, construction and improvement of municipal sewage treatment works, aiming to reduce water pollution. The program outlines eligibility, cost-sharing and project criteria for funding.
[title_3] Title III - Standards and Enforcement -- Title III of the Clean Water Act sets national water quality standards, requires technology-based effluent limits for point sources, and establishes enforcement tools such as permits, inspections, and civil or criminal penalties to ensure compliance and protect the integrity of U.S. waters.
[title_4] Title IV - Permits and Licenses -- Title IV of the Clean Water Act creates the permitting system that regulates discharges of pollutants into U.S. waters. It authorizes the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System and related licenses, setting standards, monitoring requirements, and enforcement mechanisms to protect water quality.
[title_5] Title V - General Provisions -- Title V of the Clean Water Act establishes a national permitting system for point-source discharges of pollutants into U.S. waters. It requires facilities to obtain NPDES permits, sets effluent limits, mandates monitoring and reporting, and provides mechanisms for public participation, enforcement and compliance assurance.
[title_6] Title VI - State Water Pollution Control Revolving Funds -- Title VI establishes State Water Pollution Control Revolving Funds that give states federal-assisted financing for water quality projects, such as wastewater treatment plant upgrades and non-point-source pollution controls. The EPA administers the program, providing low-interest loans and grants that states replenish as loans are repaid, helping to meet Clean Water Act objectives. This mechanism enables ongoing investment in infrastructure to protect and improve surface water resources.
