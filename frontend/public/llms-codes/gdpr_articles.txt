[gdpr_art_1] Article 1 - Subject-matter and objectives -- Article 1 defines the purpose and scope of the GDPR, establishing the protection of personal data and the free movement of such data within the European Union.
[gdpr_art_10] Article 10 - Processing of personal data relating to criminal convictions and offences -- Article 10 of the GDPR sets strict conditions for handling personal data about criminal convictions, related offences, or security measures, allowing processing only when authorised by Union or Member State law that ensures appropriate safeguards for data subjects' rights and freedoms.
[gdpr_art_11] Article 11 - Processing which does not require identification -- Article 11 of the GDPR allows member states to permit processing of personal data without requiring the data subject's identity to be verified, provided the purpose can be achieved without identification. This enables activities such as statistical analysis, research, or public interest tasks where anonymity is essential.
[gdpr_art_12] Article 12 - Transparent information, communication and modalities for the exercise of rights -- Article 12 sets out the requirement that data controllers provide information to data subjects in clear, concise and easily accessible language, using plain terms whenever possible. It obliges controllers to facilitate the exercise of rights by offering free, simple communication channels and to respond to requests without undue delay, typically within one month. The article also mandates that any necessary fees be limited to reasonable administrative costs.
[gdpr_art_13] Article 13 - Information to be provided where personal data are collected from the data subject -- Article 13 of the GDPR requires controllers to give data subjects clear, concise information when personal data are collected directly from them, covering the identity and contact details of the controller, purposes of processing, legal basis, data retention periods, rights of the individual, and any transfers to third countries. The provision ensures transparency at the point of collection and enables subjects to make informed decisions about their data.
[gdpr_art_14] Article 14 - Information to be provided where personal data have not been obtained from the data subject -- Article 14 of the GDPR obliges data controllers to disclose specific information to individuals when their personal data are collected from sources other than the data subject, such as the controller's identity, processing purposes, data categories, recipients, storage limits, rights, and the data source.
[gdpr_art_15] Article 15 - Right of access by the data subject -- Article 15 of the GDPR grants individuals the right to obtain confirmation from a controller about whether their personal data is being processed, and, if so, to access that data along with information on its purpose, retention period, recipients, and the source of the data. Controllers must provide a copy of the data free of charge, unless requests are unfounded or excessive. This right ensures transparency and enables data subjects to verify the lawfulness of processing.
[gdpr_art_16] Article 16 - Right to rectification -- Article 16 of the GDPR gives data subjects the right to have inaccurate personal data corrected without undue delay. Controllers must act on rectification requests promptly and inform any third parties who have received the incorrect data, unless this proves impossible or involves disproportionate effort.
[gdpr_art_17] Article 17 - Right to erasure ('right to be forgotten') -- Article 17 of the GDPR grants individuals the right to have their personal data erased, often called the "right to be forgotten," when it is no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, or processing is unlawful. Data controllers must act without undue delay, subject only to exceptions such as legal obligations, public interest tasks, or the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims.
[gdpr_art_18] Article 18 - Right to restriction of processing -- Article 18 of the GDPR gives data subjects the right to request a temporary restriction on the processing of their personal data when they contest its accuracy, the processing is unlawful, the controller no longer needs the data but the subject requires it for legal claims, or the controller has received an objection to processing. The restriction must be lifted once the controller verifies the data is accurate, resolves the objection, or the purpose for processing no longer applies.
[gdpr_art_19] Article 19 - Notification obligation regarding rectification or erasure or restriction -- Article 19 of the GDPR obliges a data controller to notify the data subject without undue delay when it has rectified, erased, or restricted the processing of their personal data, detailing the nature of the action taken and any relevant information about the outcome.
[gdpr_art_2] Article 2 - Material scope -- Article 2 sets out the material scope of the GDPR, defining the processing activities, personal data, and data subjects to which the regulation applies, as well as the territorial reach covering processing carried out within the EU and certain extraterritorial situations.
[gdpr_art_20] Article 20 - Right to data portability -- Article 20 of the GDPR grants individuals the right to receive personal data concerning them in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format and to transmit that data to another controller without hindrance. The provision applies where processing is based on consent or a contract and is carried out by automated means. It aims to increase user control and facilitate switching between service providers.
[gdpr_art_21] Article 21 - Right to object -- Article 21 of the GDPR grants individuals the right to object to the processing of their personal data, especially when it is used for direct-marketing, scientific or historical research, public-interest tasks, or profiling. An objection may be made at any time, and the controller must stop processing unless it can demonstrate compelling legitimate grounds that override the data subject's interests, rights, and freedoms. This provision ensures a practical safeguard against unwanted data use.
[gdpr_art_22] Article 22 - Automated individual decision-making, including profiling -- Article 22 of the GDPR grants individuals the right to avoid decisions based solely on automated processing, including profiling, that produce legal or similarly significant effects, unless specific exceptions apply. It requires meaningful human involvement, clear information about the logic used, and safeguards to protect data subjects' interests.
[gdpr_art_23] Article 23 - Restrictions -- Article 23 of the GDPR allows EU member states to impose national-law restrictions on the regulation's provisions when necessary for reasons such as national security, defence, public security or the prevention of crime, provided the measures are proportionate and do not undermine the core protection of personal data.
[gdpr_art_24] Article 24 - Responsibility of the controller -- Article 24 obliges the data controller to adopt appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure and be able to demonstrate compliance with the GDPR. The controller must maintain records, monitor processing activities and regularly assess the effectiveness of its data-protection policies. This responsibility includes updating measures as needed to address emerging risks.
[gdpr_art_25] Article 25 - Data protection by design and by default -- Article 25 requires controllers to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures that embed data protection principles into the processing of personal data from the outset, ensuring privacy is built in by design and by default. It mandates that only data necessary for each specific purpose be processed and that default settings safeguard privacy.
[gdpr_art_26] Article 26 - Joint controllers -- Article 26 sets out the rules for joint controllers, meaning two or more entities that together decide the purposes and means of processing personal data. It requires them to transparently allocate their respective responsibilities for complying with the GDPR, to inform data subjects of that arrangement, and to maintain a record of the division of duties.
[gdpr_art_27] Article 27 - Representatives of controllers or processors not established in the Union -- Article 27 obliges controllers or processors that are not established in the EU to designate a legal representative within the Union. The representative must be established in the member state where the controller's or processor's activities are concerned and serves as the contact point for supervisory authorities and data subjects, ensuring compliance with GDPR obligations.
[gdpr_art_28] Article 28 - Processor -- Article 28 sets out the duties of data processors under the GDPR, requiring them to process personal data only on the controller's instructions, adopt appropriate technical and organisational safeguards, and keep records of processing activities. It also obliges controllers and processors to conclude a written contract that details the processor's responsibilities, sub-processor terms and audit rights.
[gdpr_art_29] Article 29 - Processing under the authority of the controller or processor -- Article 29 of the GDPR states that any personal-data processing carried out by a processor or by a controller on behalf of another controller must be governed by a written contract or other legal act that sets out the subject-matter, duration, nature and purpose of the processing, the type of personal data, and the obligations and rights of each party. The agreement must require the processor to act only on the controller's documented instructions and to implement appropriate security measures.
[gdpr_art_3] Article 3 - Territorial scope -- Article 3 sets out the GDPR's territorial scope, applying to any processing of personal data in the Union regardless of where the controller or processor is established. It also reaches entities outside the EU when they offer goods or services to EU residents or monitor their behaviour within the Union, thereby extending the regulation's reach beyond its borders.
[gdpr_art_30] Article 30 - Records of processing activities -- Article 30 of the GDPR obliges data controllers and processors to keep detailed records of their processing activities, documenting purposes, data categories, recipients, international transfers, retention periods, and security measures. The requirement applies to organizations with 250 or more employees, or to any entity whose processing is likely to pose risks to individuals' rights and freedoms. Compliance helps demonstrate accountability and facilitates supervisory authority oversight.
[gdpr_art_31] Article 31 - Cooperation with the supervisory authority -- Article 31 obliges data controllers and processors to cooperate with the supervisory authority on request, supplying any information, documents or access to premises needed for investigations, audits or enforcement actions, and to assist in any remedial measures required to ensure GDPR compliance.
[gdpr_art_32] Article 32 - Security of processing -- Article 32 of the GDPR obliges data controllers and processors to adopt suitable technical and organisational measures that ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. This includes actions such as pseudonymisation, encryption, ensuring confidentiality, integrity, availability and resilience of processing systems, and having the capacity to restore the availability and access to personal data promptly after an incident.

[gdpr_art_34] Article 34 - Communication of a personal data breach to the data subject -- Article 34 of the GDPR obliges data controllers to notify the affected data subject when a personal data breach is likely to result in a high risk to their rights and freedoms. The communication must be made without undue delay and include a clear description of the breach, its likely consequences, and the measures taken to mitigate adverse effects. This ensures transparency and enables individuals to protect themselves against potential harm.
[gdpr_art_35] Article 35 - Data protection impact assessment -- Article 35 of the GDPR requires controllers to conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) when processing is likely to result in a high risk to individuals' rights and freedoms, such as systematic profiling or large-scale processing of sensitive data. The DPIA must describe the processing, assess risks, and outline measures to mitigate them.
[gdpr_art_36] Article 36 - Prior consultation -- Article 36 of the GDPR obliges a data controller to seek prior consultation with the relevant supervisory authority when a proposed processing operation is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals, especially when using new or innovative technologies. The controller must provide a detailed description of the processing, the intended safeguards and the risk assessment, and must await the authority's advice before proceeding.
[gdpr_art_37] Article 37 - Designation of the data protection officer -- Article 37 of the GDPR obliges controllers and processors to appoint a Data Protection Officer when their core activities consist of regular or systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale, or when they process special categories of personal data or criminal convictions, ensuring compliance with the regulation.
[gdpr_art_38] Article 38 - Position of the data protection officer -- Article 38 defines the position of the data protection officer, requiring the appointment of a qualified individual who operates independently, reports directly to senior management, and is tasked with monitoring compliance, advising on data-protection impact assessments, and serving as the liaison with supervisory authorities.
[gdpr_art_39] Article 39 - Tasks of the data protection officer -- Article 39 of the GDPR outlines the responsibilities of the data protection officer, requiring them to monitor compliance, advise on impact assessments, cooperate with supervisory authorities, and ensure training and awareness within the organization.
[gdpr_art_4] Article 4 - Definitions -- Article 4 of the GDPR supplies the core definitions that anchor the regulation, clarifying concepts such as personal data, special categories of data, data subject, processing, controller, processor, consent and legitimate interests, thereby ensuring consistent interpretation across EU member states.
[gdpr_art_40] Article 40 - Codes of conduct -- Article 40 of the GDPR provides for the development of codes of conduct that specify the application of the regulation's provisions to particular sectors, processing activities or technological solutions, and encourages the creation of monitoring bodies to ensure compliance and promote accountability.
[gdpr_art_41] Article 41 - Monitoring of approved codes of conduct -- Article 41 of the GDPR requires the European Data Protection Board to monitor the application of approved codes of conduct, ensuring they remain effective and consistent with the Regulation, and to issue opinions or recommendations when necessary. This oversight helps maintain uniform data-protection standards across sectors and member states.
[gdpr_art_42] Article 42 - Certification -- Article 42 of the GDPR creates a voluntary certification framework that lets data controllers and processors demonstrate compliance with the regulation through approved schemes. It requires certification bodies to be accredited, the schemes to be transparent and regularly reviewed, and the certificates to be recognized across the EU.
[gdpr_art_43] Article 43 - Certification bodies -- Article 43 of the GDPR establishes the framework for certification bodies that issue data-protection certifications. It requires such bodies to be accredited, operate independently, keep certified information confidential, and cooperate with supervisory authorities. The article also mandates monitoring, renewal and possible revocation of certifications to ensure ongoing compliance.
[gdpr_art_44] Article 44 - General principle for transfers -- Article 44 sets the overarching rule that any transfer of personal data to a third country or international organisation must ensure an essentially equivalent level of protection to that guaranteed within the EU and may be achieved through adequacy decisions, appropriate safeguards or binding corporate rules.
[gdpr_art_45] Article 45 - Transfers on the basis of an adequacy decision -- Article 45 of the EU General Data Protection Regulation sets out the conditions for transferring personal data to third countries when the European Commission has issued an adequacy decision, confirming that the destination provides a level of data protection essentially equivalent to that guaranteed in the EU. It allows such transfers without additional safeguards, provided the decision remains in force and the recipient complies with the stipulated requirements.
[gdpr_art_46] Article 46 - Transfers subject to appropriate safeguards -- Article 46 governs transfers of personal data to third countries or international organisations where the EU has not recognised an adequacy decision. It permits such transfers only if the controller or processor provides appropriate safeguards, for example standard contractual clauses, binding corporate rules, approved codes of conduct or certification mechanisms, and requires cooperation with supervisory authorities.
[gdpr_art_47] Article 47 - Binding corporate rules -- Article 47 of the GDPR sets out the conditions for approving Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs), which are internal data-protection policies that multinational groups can adopt to allow lawful transfers of personal data between group entities outside the EU. It requires BCRs to be approved by a competent supervisory authority, to guarantee enforceable rights for data subjects, and to include mechanisms for monitoring, handling complaints and ensuring accountability across the organization.

[gdpr_art_5] Article 5 - Principles relating to processing of personal data -- Article 5 of the GDPR sets out the core principles that must guide any processing of personal data, requiring it to be lawful, fair and transparent, collected for specific purposes, limited to what is necessary, accurate, kept only as long as needed, and protected with appropriate security while being accountable for compliance.
[gdpr_art_50] Article 50 - International cooperation for the protection of personal data -- Article 50 of the GDPR establishes a framework for international cooperation, enabling EU data-protection authorities to work with supervisory bodies in third countries and international organisations. It provides for the exchange of information, assistance in investigations, and the development of consistent safeguards to protect personal data across borders.
[gdpr_art_51] Article 51 - Supervisory authority -- Article 51 creates a supervisory authority in each EU member state, outlining its independent status, duties to oversee GDPR compliance, investigate complaints, and impose corrective measures or penalties.
[gdpr_art_52] Article 52 - Independence -- Article 52 of the GDPR obliges supervisory authorities to operate with independence, shielding them from external influences and ensuring their decisions are impartial. It requires that members and staff are protected in the performance of their duties and that the authorities are free from directives from governments, private interests or any other bodies.
[gdpr_art_53] Article 53 - General conditions for the members of the supervisory authority -- Article 53 sets out the basic eligibility criteria for members of each EU supervisory authority, requiring them to be independent, possess high professional qualifications and relevant experience, and be appointed for a fixed term with clear rules on incompatibility, dismissal and replacement to safeguard impartial oversight of data protection.
[gdpr_art_54] Article 54 - Rules on the establishment of the supervisory authority -- Article 54 sets out the criteria and procedures for establishing independent supervisory authorities in each EU Member State, defining their composition, appointment, tenure, and responsibilities to ensure effective monitoring of data protection compliance.
[gdpr_art_55] Article 55 - Competence -- Article 55 of the GDPR specifies which bodies have the authority to oversee data-protection matters, outlining the competence of national supervisory authorities, the European Data Protection Board and the European Commission to adopt binding decisions, issue guidelines and ensure consistent application of the Regulation throughout the EU.
[gdpr_art_56] Article 56 - Competence of the lead supervisory authority -- Article 56 of the GDPR defines the competence of the lead supervisory authority for cross-border data-processing cases. The lead authority coordinates investigations, ensures consistent application of the regulation and can delegate tasks to other concerned authorities, while also having the power to be replaced if it cannot act.
[gdpr_art_57] Article 57 - Tasks -- Article 57 of the GDPR sets out the tasks of supervisory authorities, requiring them to monitor and enforce the regulation, promote public awareness, advise on data-protection matters, and cooperate with other authorities at national and EU level.
[gdpr_art_58] Article 58 - Powers -- Article 58 of the GDPR sets out the powers of supervisory authorities, including the ability to request information, conduct investigations, issue orders to amend processing, impose temporary or definitive limitations, levy administrative fines and suspend data transfers, as well as to issue opinions and provide guidance on compliance.
[gdpr_art_59] Article 59 - Activity reports -- Article 59 of the GDPR requires the supervisory authority to prepare and publish regular activity reports. These reports summarize enforcement actions, complaints, investigations, and other relevant supervisory activities carried out during the reporting period, providing transparency about the authority's work and the overall application of data-protection rules.
[gdpr_art_6] Article 6 - Lawfulness of processing -- Article 6 defines the legal bases that must exist for personal data to be processed lawfully under the GDPR. It lists six conditions-consent, performance of a contract, compliance with a legal obligation, protection of vital interests, performance of a public task, and legitimate interests-any one of which must apply for processing to be permissible.
[gdpr_art_60] Article 60 - Cooperation between the lead supervisory authority and the other supervisory authorities -- Article 60 of the GDPR sets out the framework for cooperation between the lead supervisory authority and other supervisory authorities in cross-border cases. It requires the lead authority to coordinate investigations, share information, and ensure consistent enforcement while allowing other authorities to participate and contribute to decisions.
[gdpr_art_61] Article 61 - Mutual assistance -- Article 61 of the GDPR sets out the framework for mutual assistance among EU supervisory authorities, allowing them to cooperate in investigations, inspections and the exchange of information concerning cross-border data processing. The article obliges authorities to provide each other with the necessary support and to coordinate actions to ensure consistent enforcement of the regulation across member states.
[gdpr_art_62] Article 62 - Joint operations of supervisory authorities -- Article 62 sets out the rules for joint operations of supervisory authorities, allowing them to cooperate, share information and conduct coordinated investigations across EU Member States to ensure consistent and effective enforcement of the GDPR.
[gdpr_art_63] Article 63 - Consistency mechanism -- Article 63 of the GDPR creates a consistency mechanism that lets the European Commission ensure uniform application of the regulation across Member States. It permits the Commission to adopt implementing acts, issue opinions when national supervisory authorities diverge, and refer disputes to the European Data Protection Board.
[gdpr_art_64] Article 64 - Opinion of the Board -- Article 64 authorises the European Data Protection Board to issue opinions on any dispute concerning the interpretation or application of the GDPR, including conflicts between supervisory authorities. While the opinions are not legally binding, they serve as authoritative guidance for national authorities, the Commission and the European Parliament, helping ensure a consistent approach to data-protection rules across the EU.

The board can issue binding decisions or recommendations within a shortened timeframe to address pressing cross-border data protection issues.
[gdpr_art_67] Article 67 - Exchange of information -- Article 67 of the GDPR establishes the framework for the exchange of information between supervisory authorities and the European Commission, enabling cooperation on cross-border data protection matters. It outlines procedures for sharing details about investigations, decisions and best practices to ensure consistent enforcement throughout the EU. This provision facilitates coordinated responses to privacy breaches and supports the overall effectiveness of data protection oversight.
[gdpr_art_68] Article 68 - European Data Protection Board -- Article 68 establishes the European Data Protection Board, a body that ensures consistent application of the GDPR across EU member states, provides guidance, issues opinions, and resolves disputes between supervisory authorities. It also advises the European Commission on data-protection matters.
[gdpr_art_69] Article 69 - Independence -- Article 69 of the GDPR sets out the requirement that each supervisory authority must act independently, free from external influence, and with adequate resources. It obliges member states to ensure authority members have security of tenure, a single financial, organisational and administrative framework, and protection against dismissal except for just cause.
[gdpr_art_7] Article 7 - Conditions for consent -- Article 7 of the GDPR sets the requirements for valid consent, stating that it must be freely given, specific, informed and unambiguous, demonstrated by a clear affirmative act. It also obliges controllers to provide clear information about the purposes of processing and to keep records of how and when consent was obtained. Consent may be withdrawn at any time and must be as easy to withdraw as to give.
[gdpr_art_70] Article 70 - Tasks of the Board -- Article 70 sets out the tasks of the supervisory authority's board, including establishing internal rules, adopting guidelines, approving budgets, and coordinating the authority's work. It also defines how board members are appointed, removed and may be replaced, and specifies the procedures for decision-making and reporting.
[gdpr_art_71] Article 71 - Reports -- Article 71 of the GDPR obliges each supervisory authority to prepare an annual report describing its activities, staffing, budget, enforcement actions and any significant developments in data-protection law, and to submit that report to the European Parliament, the Council and the Commission. The reports aim to ensure transparency, facilitate coordination among authorities and allow EU institutions to monitor the overall implementation of the regulation.
[gdpr_art_72] Article 72 - Procedure -- Article 72 sets out the procedural framework for cooperation between supervisory authorities and the European Commission in cross-border cases, detailing how complaints are handled, investigations are coordinated, and decisions are adopted to ensure consistent application of the GDPR across Member States.
[gdpr_art_73] Article 73 - Chair -- Article 73 sets out the position of the chair of the European Data Protection Board, describing how the chair is appointed, their term of office and the responsibilities they carry, including representing the Board, convening meetings, ensuring the Board's work proceeds efficiently and reporting to the European Commission.
[gdpr_art_74] Article 74 - Tasks of the Chair -- Article 74 outlines the responsibilities of the chair of a supervisory authority, who must convene and preside over meetings, ensure the authority's decisions are carried out, represent the authority in dealings with the European Data Protection Board and other bodies, and keep appropriate records of its activities.
[gdpr_art_75] Article 75 - Secretariat -- Article 75 of the GDPR creates the secretariat of the European Data Protection Board, assigning it the role of providing administrative and logistical support. It outlines the secretariat's composition, duties and the mechanisms for coordinating actions among supervisory authorities.
[gdpr_art_76] Article 76 - Confidentiality -- Article 76 of the GDPR mandates that supervisory authorities and any body receiving personal data in the performance of their duties must keep that information confidential, disclosing it only when authorised by Union or Member State law or with the data subject's consent. This provision ensures the protection of sensitive data throughout investigations, decision-making and enforcement activities.

[gdpr_art_78] Article 78 - Right to an effective judicial remedy against a supervisory authority -- Article 78 of the GDPR gives data subjects the right to challenge decisions of supervisory authorities before a court, ensuring that any measure affecting their personal data can be reviewed and, if necessary, corrected. It requires that judicial remedies be effective, timely, and provide appropriate compensation for violations.
[gdpr_art_79] Article 79 - Right to an effective judicial remedy against a controller or processor -- Article 79 guarantees data subjects the right to an effective judicial remedy against a controller or processor when the GDPR has been breached. Individuals may bring actions before national courts, seek compensation, or pursue collective redress, and member states must designate competent authorities to enforce these safeguards.
[gdpr_art_8] Article 8 - Conditions applicable to child's consent in relation to information society services -- Article 8 sets the rules for when a child's consent may be used as the legal basis for processing personal data in online services. The representative may exercise all rights of the data subject, including receiving information and filing actions, provided they have proper authorization. This provision facilitates collective redress and simplifies enforcement for groups lacking individual resources.
[gdpr_art_81] Article 81 - Suspension of proceedings -- Article 81 allows a supervisory authority to suspend administrative or judicial proceedings against a data controller or processor when it has taken appropriate corrective measures, such as complying with the GDPR, so that the case can be paused pending verification of those measures.
[gdpr_art_82] Article 82 - Right to compensation and liability -- Article 82 of the GDPR establishes that any person who suffers material or non-material damage as a result of an infringement of the regulation has the right to receive compensation from the controller or processor responsible, and that they may also claim liability for any loss caused by the breach.
[gdpr_art_83] Article 83 - General conditions for imposing administrative fines -- Article 83 outlines the general conditions that supervisory authorities must consider when imposing administrative fines for GDPR violations, including the nature, gravity, duration and intent of the breach, any previous infringements and the degree of responsibility of the controller or processor. The article provides a two-level fine structure, with lower fines for less serious infringements and higher fines for violations such as ignoring data-subject rights or failing to implement required technical and organisational measures.

[gdpr_art_86] Article 86 - Processing and public access to official documents -- Article 86 outlines the rules for handling personal data that appears in official documents, allowing public access to such documents while safeguarding individuals' privacy. [gdpr_art_87] Article 87 - Processing of the national identification number -- Article 87 sets conditions for processing any national identification number, requiring a specific legal basis such as explicit consent, a statutory obligation, or a public-interest task. It limits use to purposes compatible with the identification function and mandates safeguards to protect data subjects.
[gdpr_art_88] Article 88 - Processing in the context of employment -- Article 88 allows member states to enact specific rules for processing personal data in employment relations, covering recruitment, performance monitoring, and termination while ensuring safeguards for workers' rights. It permits limitations on data subjects' rights where necessary for employment obligations, provided the measures are proportionate and transparent.
[gdpr_art_89] Article 89 - Safeguards and derogations relating to processing for archiving, research or statistical purposes -- Article 89 sets out protective measures for processing personal data when it is used for archiving, scientific research, historical research or statistical purposes, allowing certain data-subject rights to be limited if appropriate safeguards are in place. These safeguards may include technical and organisational measures such as pseudonymisation, encryption, or a clear legal basis that ensures the data is used only for the specified purpose.
[gdpr_art_9] Article 9 - Processing of special categories of personal data -- Article 9 of the GDPR sets out strict rules for handling special categories of personal data such as racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, health information, and biometric data. It generally prohibits processing unless a specific exemption applies, for example explicit consent, vital interests, or substantial public interest.
[gdpr_art_90] Article 90 - Obligations of secrecy -- Article 90 of the GDPR outlines the confidentiality obligations for supervisory authorities, bodies, offices and any natural or legal persons who receive personal data in the performance of their tasks. [gdpr_art_92] Article 92 - Exercise of the delegation -- Article 92 sets out the rules for how the European Commission may exercise delegation of powers under the GDPR, specifying the circumstances, procedures and limits for delegating tasks to other bodies, and requiring that any delegated act be subject to appropriate oversight and possible review.

[gdpr_ch_1] Chapter I - General Provisions -- The entry gdpr_ch_1 refers to Chapter I of the General Data Protection Regulation, which contains the General Provisions. It establishes the regulation's scope, objectives and fundamental definitions, setting out the basic principles of data protection and the legal framework that applies across the European Union.
[gdpr_ch_10] Chapter X - Delegated Acts and Implementing Acts -- Chapter X of the GDPR outlines the use of delegated and implementing acts, which are non-legislative measures adopted by the European Commission to detail technical and procedural aspects of the regulation. These acts allow the Commission to clarify definitions, set standards and specify requirements without amending the primary text, ensuring consistent application across the EU.
[gdpr_ch_11] Chapter XI - Final Provisions -- The GDPR Chapter XI contains the final provisions that bring the regulation into force, set the date of application, repeal previous data-protection directives, and outline transitional and binding obligations for Member States and supervisory authorities. It also addresses the relationship with other EU law.
[gdpr_ch_2] Chapter II - Principles -- Chapter II of the GDPR establishes the fundamental data-processing principles that must guide all personal data activities, covering lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity and confidentiality, and accountability.
[gdpr_ch_3] Chapter III - Rights of the Data Subject -- Chapter III of the GDPR sets out the rights of the data subject, granting individuals control over their personal information. It includes the right to access data, obtain rectification, request erasure, restrict processing, receive data in a portable format, object to processing, and safeguards against automated decision-making. These provisions empower people to manage how their data is collected, used, and shared.
[gdpr_ch_4] Chapter IV - Controller and Processor -- Chapter IV of the GDPR defines the roles and responsibilities of data controllers and processors, setting out how personal data must be handled, the legal bases for processing, and obligations for transparency, security and accountability. It also regulates the controller-processor relationship, including contractual requirements and liability provisions.
[gdpr_ch_5] Chapter V - Transfers of Personal Data to Third Countries or International Organisations -- The entry gdpr_ch_5 refers to Chapter V of the GDPR, which governs the transfer of personal data outside the EU to third countries or international organisations. It sets out the legal grounds for such transfers, including adequacy decisions, appropriate safeguards, binding corporate rules and specific derogations for limited situations.
[gdpr_ch_6] Chapter VI - Independent Supervisory Authorities -- The entry gdpr_ch_6 refers to Chapter VI of the EU General Data Protection Regulation, which sets out the framework for independent supervisory authorities. It details their establishment, responsibilities, powers, and cooperation mechanisms to ensure consistent enforcement of data-protection rules across member states.
[gdpr_ch_7] Chapter VII - Cooperation and Consistency -- Chapter VII of the GDPR outlines the framework for cooperation among EU supervisory authorities and the European Data Protection Board, establishing procedures for joint investigations, mutual assistance, and the consistency mechanism that ensures uniform application of the regulation across member states.
[gdpr_ch_8] Chapter VIII - Remedies, Liability and Penalties -- Chapter VIII of the GDPR outlines the enforcement mechanisms, specifying the rights of data subjects to obtain redress, the obligations of controllers and processors to cooperate with supervisory authorities, and the range of administrative fines and penalties that can be imposed for non-compliance. It also sets out criteria for liability, including joint responsibility and indemnification, and provides guidance on mitigating factors and procedural safeguards.
[gdpr_ch_9] Chapter IX - Provisions Relating to Specific Processing Situations -- Chapter IX of the GDPR establishes the rules for particular processing activities, covering the handling of special categories of personal data, data relating to criminal convictions, and processing for scientific, historical, statistical or archival purposes, and outlines the associated safeguards and exceptions.
