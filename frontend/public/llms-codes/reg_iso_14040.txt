[4_1] 4.1 General principles of LCA -- Entry 4.1 outlines the fundamental concepts guiding a life-cycle assessment, including defining the study goal and scope, selecting a functional unit, establishing system boundaries, and ensuring data quality and transparency throughout the inventory, impact assessment, and interpretation phases. These principles provide a consistent framework for evaluating environmental burdens across a product's or service's entire life cycle.
[4_2] 4.2 Phases of an LCA -- Section 4.2 outlines the four sequential phases of a life cycle assessment: goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, impact assessment, and interpretation. It explains how each phase builds on the previous one to ensure comprehensive environmental evaluation.
[4_3] 4.3 Key features -- The 4.3 "Key features" section outlines the essential elements of a life-cycle assessment, emphasizing a defined goal and scope, a systematic inventory of inputs and outputs, impact assessment, and interpretation of results. It stresses that the study must be iterative, transparent, complete, and scientifically justified, ensuring relevance and accuracy throughout the LCA process.
[5_1] 5.1 General -- The 5.1 General section introduces the core concepts of ISO 14040, outlining the purpose and scope of life-cycle assessment, the relationship between its four phases, and the overarching principles that guide consistent, transparent, and comparable environmental evaluations.
[5_2] 5.2 Goal of the study -- Section 5.2 defines the goal of the study as the initial step in a life-cycle assessment, requiring a clear statement of the intended application, target audience, and decision context. It also mandates specification of the functional unit, system boundaries, and any assumptions or limitations to ensure the results are relevant and comparable.
[5_3] 5.3 Scope of the study -- ISO 14040:2006 defines the scope of a Life Cycle Assessment as the boundaries and functional unit that delimit the product system being studied, specifying which processes, life-cycle stages, and environmental impacts are included. It also outlines the assumptions, limitations, and allocation rules that must be documented to ensure the study's relevance, completeness, and comparability.
[5_4] 5.4 Function, functional unit and reference flow -- Section 5.4 defines the function of a product system as the intended purpose that provides the basis for comparison, introduces the functional unit as a quantitative reference that expresses that function, and describes the reference flow as the quantified input or output associated with the functional unit.
[5_5] 5.5 System boundary -- The system boundary defines which processes, material and energy flows are included or excluded in a life-cycle assessment, establishing the scope of the study relative to the functional unit. It delineates the temporal, spatial and technological limits of the product or service system, ensuring that all relevant stages-from raw material extraction to disposal-are consistently accounted for in the LCA model.
[6_1] 6.1 General -- Section 6.1 gives a concise overview of the ISO 14040 LCA framework, outlining its purpose, scope and fundamental concepts, including the four main phases-goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, impact assessment and interpretation-and how they integrate within environmental management systems.
[6_2] 6.2 Data collection -- Section 6.2 of ISO 14040:2006 defines the procedures for gathering life-cycle inventory data, distinguishing primary data directly measured from the studied system and secondary data sourced from literature or databases. It sets requirements for data quality, completeness, representativeness, and documentation to ensure reliable environmental assessments.
[6_3] 6.3 Calculation procedures -- Section 6.3 outlines the calculation procedures used in a life-cycle assessment, defining how inputs and outputs are quantified, how functional units are applied, and the steps for aggregating data across the defined system boundaries to derive environmental impact results.
[6_4] 6.4 Allocation -- In ISO 14040:2006, section 6.4 covers allocation, explaining how inputs and outputs are assigned to product systems when processes produce multiple functions or co-products, and it sets out the principles for selecting allocation methods that ensure consistent and comparable life-cycle assessment results.
[7_1] 7.1 General -- Section 7.1 of ISO 14040:2006 provides a general overview of the requirements for conducting a life-cycle assessment, stating that the study must have a clearly defined goal and scope, appropriate system boundaries, and a functional unit that enables consistent comparison of results. It emphasizes the need for transparent documentation of assumptions, data sources, and methodological choices to ensure credibility and reproducibility of the LCA.
[7_2] 7.2 Selection of impact categories and classification -- Section 7.2 of ISO 14040:2006 guides practitioners in choosing appropriate life-cycle impact categories and assigning them to the study's goal and scope, ensuring that the selected categories reflect the most relevant environmental issues for the product or system under assessment and are classified consistently for transparent interpretation.
[7_3] 7.3 Characterization -- Section 7.3 of ISO 14040:2006 describes the characterization phase of a life-cycle assessment, in which the inventory results are translated into potential environmental impacts. It outlines the use of equivalence factors and impact-category models to quantify contributions to effects such as climate change, acidification or eutrophication, enabling comparison of alternative product or process options.
[7_4] 7.4 Optional elements -- Section 7.4 outlines optional elements that may be incorporated into a life-cycle assessment to enhance its robustness or relevance, such as sensitivity and uncertainty analyses, allocation procedures, and additional impact categories. These elements are not required for compliance but can improve the depth and transparency of the study.
[8_1] 8.1 General -- Entry 8.1 provides an overview of the ISO 14040:2006 Life Cycle Assessment framework, outlining its purpose, scope and the fundamental concepts that underlie LCA studies. It introduces the four main phases-goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, impact assessment and interpretation-and explains how they interrelate to support consistent, transparent environmental decision-making.
[8_2] 8.2 Identification of significant issues -- ISO 14040:2006 section 8.2 guides analysts in identifying significant issues within a life-cycle assessment, requiring the selection of material flows, emissions, and impacts that are most relevant to the defined goal and scope. The process uses criteria such as magnitude, relevance to decision-makers, and data quality to focus the study on key environmental aspects.
[8_3] 8.3 Evaluation (completeness, sensitivity, consistency checks) -- Section 8.3 of ISO 14040:2006 outlines the evaluation phase of a life-cycle assessment, directing practitioners to verify that the study is complete, to conduct sensitivity analyses on key variables, and to perform consistency checks that confirm the logical coherence of data, methods and assumptions, thereby ensuring robust and credible results.
[8_4] 8.4 Conclusions, limitations and recommendations -- The section summarizes the overall findings of the life-cycle assessment, highlights the methodological and data constraints that affect the results, and offers guidance for improving future studies, informing stakeholders, and supporting more sustainable decision-making.
[cl_4] Clause 4 - LCA Framework -- Clause 4 of ISO 14040:2006 establishes the Life Cycle Assessment framework, specifying how to define the study's goal and scope, select a functional unit, set system boundaries, and identify data quality requirements, thereby guiding consistent and transparent LCA practice.
[cl_5] Clause 5 - Goal and Scope Definition -- Clause 5 specifies how to define the goal and scope of a life-cycle assessment, covering the intended application, audience, functional unit, system boundaries, and impact categories. It provides the basis for transparent, reproducible studies and directs the choice of data, methods, and level of detail.
[cl_6] Clause 6 - Life Cycle Inventory Analysis (LCI) -- Clause 6 of ISO 14040:2006 outlines the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) phase, requiring systematic collection and quantification of inputs and outputs for each stage of a product's life cycle. It details how to compile data on material, energy, emissions and waste to establish a comprehensive inventory that supports subsequent impact assessment.
[cl_7] Clause 7 - Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) -- Clause 7 of ISO 14040:2006 establishes the Life Cycle Impact Assessment phase, outlining how to select impact categories, define characterization models, and aggregate results to quantify potential environmental effects of a product or system throughout its life cycle. It provides guidance on classification, characterization, normalization, weighting, and interpretation to support decision-making and improvement.
[cl_8] Clause 8 - Life Cycle Interpretation -- Clause 8 outlines the interpretation phase of a life cycle assessment, guiding analysts to evaluate results, pinpoint significant issues, and develop conclusions and recommendations. It explains how to use LCA findings to support decision-making, improve product design, and communicate environmental performance.
