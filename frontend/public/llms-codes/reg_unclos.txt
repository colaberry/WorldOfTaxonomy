[11_1] Common heritage of mankind principle -- The common heritage of mankind principle, established in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, declares that the seabed and its resources beyond national jurisdiction belong to all humanity. It obliges states to share benefits equitably, protect the environment, and prevent exclusive exploitation by any single nation.
[11_2] International Seabed Authority -- The International Seabed Authority is an intergovernmental organization created under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea to regulate mineral-related activities in the international seabed area beyond national jurisdiction. It issues exploration and exploitation permits, ensures equitable sharing of benefits, and safeguards the marine environment.
[12_1] General obligation to protect and preserve the marine environment -- UNCLOS Article 1 establishes a universal duty for all States to protect and preserve the marine environment, obligating them to prevent, reduce and control pollution from any source and to cooperate internationally in environmental management, reflecting the law of the sea's overarching environmental stewardship principle.
[12_2] Pollution from land-based sources -- The entry addresses the Convention's provisions for preventing and controlling marine pollution that originates on land. It outlines state obligations to monitor, regulate and reduce discharge of harmful substances from rivers, runoff and other land-based activities, and calls for cooperation and the development of national strategies to protect the marine environment.
[12_3] Pollution from seabed activities -- The entry covers provisions in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea that regulate and prevent pollution resulting from activities on the ocean floor, such as seabed mining, construction, and related operations. It outlines the responsibilities of flag and coastal states to assess environmental impacts, adopt preventive measures, and ensure that any discharges do not harm the marine environment.
[15_1] Compulsory dispute settlement procedures -- The entry refers to the mandatory dispute-settlement system established by the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, which requires parties to submit contested maritime issues to compulsory procedures such as the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, arbitration under Annex VII, or a special arbitral panel, ensuring that disputes are resolved through binding legal processes rather than unilateral action.
[15_2] International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea -- The International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea is an independent judicial body created by the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea to settle disputes arising from the interpretation and application of the Convention. Based in Hamburg, it consists of 21 judges elected for nine-year terms and can issue binding rulings on maritime boundary, fisheries, and navigation issues.
[2_1] Limits of the territorial sea (12 nautical miles) -- The entry defines the maximum breadth of a coastal state's territorial sea as 12 nautical miles measured from its baseline, establishing the zone where the state exercises full sovereignty while recognizing the right of innocent passage for foreign vessels.
[2_2] Innocent passage through territorial sea -- Innocent passage through a territorial sea allows foreign vessels to traverse a coastal state's waters so long as the transit is continuous, expeditious and does not threaten the peace, security or good order of the coastal state. The ship must comply with the coastal state's laws on navigation, customs, immigration and pollution, but cannot be used for any hostile or preparatory military activity. This right is codified in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea.
[2_3] Contiguous zone (24 nautical miles) -- The contiguous zone is a maritime area extending up to 24 nautical miles from a coastal state's baseline, where the state may exercise control to prevent or punish infringements of its customs, fiscal, immigration, or sanitary regulations and to enforce laws against pollution. This zone lies beyond the territorial sea but within the exclusive economic zone, allowing the state limited sovereign rights for specific enforcement purposes.
[5_1] Rights and duties in the EEZ (200 nautical miles) -- The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea defines the exclusive economic zone (EEZ) as extending up to 200 nautical miles from a coastal state's baseline, granting that state sovereign rights to explore, exploit, conserve and manage natural resources, both living and non-living, in the water column and seabed. In return, the state must protect the marine environment, respect the rights of other states to navigation and overflight, and cooperate on scientific research and conservation measures.
[5_2] Living resources conservation -- The Living resources conservation provision of UNCLOS obliges States to manage marine biological resources sustainably, requiring that exploitation be based on scientific evidence, precautionary measures, and cooperative research to prevent over-fishing and protect marine ecosystems.
[5_3] Artificial islands and installations in EEZ -- Artificial islands and installations built by a coastal State within its exclusive economic zone are subject to the same jurisdiction and control as the natural resources of the zone, but they do not generate their own maritime zones such as territorial seas or EEZs. The building, operation and removal of such structures must respect the rights of other States and comply with environmental protection obligations under the Convention.
[6_1] Definition and limits of the continental shelf -- Article 76 defines the continental shelf as the natural prolongation of a coastal state's land territory to the outer edge of the continental margin, or where that margin is not discernible, to a distance of 200 nautical miles from the baselines. The shelf may be extended to 350 nautical miles or to 100 nautical miles beyond the 2,500-metre isobath if detailed geological and morphological criteria are satisfied.
[6_2] Rights over the continental shelf -- The entry defines a coastal State's exclusive right to explore and exploit the natural resources of its continental shelf, which may extend 200 nautical miles from the baseline or farther where the shelf naturally continues. It grants jurisdiction over seabed activities while preserving other States' freedom of navigation and overflight above the shelf. These rights are balanced by obligations to protect the marine environment and to cooperate with the International Seabed Authority.
[7_1] Freedom of the high seas -- Entry 7_1 of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea defines the freedom of the high seas, granting all states the right to navigate, overfly, fish, lay submarine cables and pipelines, and conduct scientific research in areas beyond national jurisdiction, while obliging them to respect the rights of others and preserve the marine environment.
[7_2] Conservation of living resources of the high seas -- The UN Convention on the Law of the Sea entry 7_2 addresses the conservation of living resources on the high seas, obligating states to cooperate in the preservation and sustainable use of marine organisms beyond national jurisdiction. It requires the adoption of measures such as catch limits, protected areas, and ecosystem-based management to prevent overexploitation and maintain biodiversity.
[pt_11] Part XI - The Area (Seabed) -- Part XI of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea establishes the legal regime for the seabed area beyond national jurisdiction, known as "the Area." It creates the International Seabed Authority to manage the exploration and exploitation of mineral resources, ensuring activities are carried out for the benefit of all humanity while protecting the marine environment.
[pt_12] Part XII - Protection and Preservation of the Marine Environment -- Part XII of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea establishes the legal framework for protecting and preserving the marine environment, obligating states to prevent, reduce and control pollution from all sources, adopt standards for discharge, conduct monitoring, and cooperate in scientific research and enforcement.
[pt_13] Part XIII - Marine Scientific Research -- Part XIII of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea sets out the framework for marine scientific research, defining the rights of states to conduct investigations and the duties to obtain consent, share results, and protect the marine environment. It establishes procedures for granting research permits, outlines the responsibilities of coastal and flag states, and promotes international cooperation and data exchange among scientific communities.
[pt_15] Part XV - Settlement of Disputes -- Part XV of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea sets out the procedures for resolving disputes over the interpretation or application of the Convention. It provides for settlement through the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, the International Court of Justice, arbitration, or special arbitral tribunals, allowing parties to select their preferred method.
[pt_2] Part II - Territorial Sea and Contiguous Zone -- Part II of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea sets out the legal regime for the territorial sea, establishing a maximum breadth of 12 nautical miles from a coastal baseline where the coastal state exercises sovereignty, subject to the right of innocent passage. It also defines the contiguous zone, extending a further 12 nautical miles, in which the state may enforce customs, fiscal, immigration and sanitary regulations to prevent infringement of its laws within the territorial sea.
