[five_year] Five-Year Reviews -- Under CERCLA, five-year reviews are periodic evaluations of remedial actions at Superfund sites to confirm that the controls in place continue to protect human health and the environment. The reviews assess performance, identify needed modifications, and are documented to maintain compliance with statutory requirements.
[npl_listing] National Priorities List (NPL) Listing -- The National Priorities List (NPL) is the EPA's inventory of hazardous waste sites identified under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act that pose significant risks to health or the environment, guiding the prioritization and funding of Superfund clean-up actions.
[pa_si] Preliminary Assessment / Site Inspection (PA/SI) -- Under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, the Preliminary Assessment/Site Inspection (PA/SI) is the first step in evaluating a contaminated property. It involves gathering information, conducting a visual inspection, and screening for potential hazards to determine whether a more detailed remedial investigation is needed.
[rd_ra] Remedial Design / Remedial Action (RD/RA) -- Under CERCLA, Remedial Design/Remedial Action (RD/RA) is the phase that follows a remedial investigation and feasibility study, where detailed engineering designs are prepared and on-site construction activities are carried out to contain, treat, or remove hazardous substances. The RD/RA process produces a remedial design document and implements the chosen cleanup actions to achieve the site's remedial objectives.
[ri_fs] Remedial Investigation / Feasibility Study (RI/FS) -- The RI/FS is a systematic process required under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act to assess the nature and extent of contamination at a site and evaluate potential remedial options. It combines data collection, risk analysis, and feasibility screening to inform the selection of an appropriate cleanup strategy. This phase forms the technical foundation for a Record of Decision or other remedial actions.
[rod] Record of Decision (ROD) -- A Record of Decision (ROD) is the EPA's formal document that announces the chosen remedial action for a Superfund site under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act. It summarizes the selected cleanup alternative, the scientific and economic rationale, and outlines the schedule and responsibilities for implementation, following a public comment period.
[sec_101] Sec 101 - Definitions -- Sec 101 sets out the core definitions for the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, explaining terms such as "response action," "hazardous substance," "potentially responsible party," and "remedial action," which frame the law's authority, cleanup obligations and liability provisions.
[sec_103] Sec 103 - Notification Requirements -- Section 103 of CERCLA sets out the mandatory notification procedures for releases of hazardous substances, requiring parties to promptly inform the EPA, state agencies, and affected communities about potential threats to health and the environment. The provision establishes timelines, content requirements, and coordination mechanisms to ensure timely public awareness and response.
[sec_104] Sec 104 - Response Authorities (Removal and Remedial Actions) -- Section 104 grants the Environmental Protection Agency and other designated authorities the power to respond to releases of hazardous substances, authorizing both removal actions to address immediate threats and remedial actions for longer-term cleanup. It outlines the agencies' ability to investigate releases, order or undertake cleanup, and coordinate with states, tribes, and responsible parties to protect public health and the environment.
[sec_105] Sec 105 - National Contingency Plan and National Priorities List -- Section 105 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act sets out the National Contingency Plan, the federal framework for responding to hazardous waste sites, and authorizes the National Priorities List, which ranks sites to determine cleanup priority.
[sec_106] Sec 106 - Abatement Actions (Unilateral Administrative Orders) -- Section 106 authorizes the EPA to issue unilateral administrative orders that require responsible parties to conduct or fund cleanup of hazardous substances, known as abatement actions. These orders can be issued without prior consent and are enforceable under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act.
[sec_107] Sec 107 - Liability (Strict, Joint and Several) -- Section 107 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act imposes strict, joint and several liability on any person who generated, transported, stored, treated, or otherwise handled hazardous substances that cause contamination. Liability covers the costs of investigation, cleanup, and damages regardless of fault, and can be enforced against multiple parties simultaneously, ensuring that the financial burden of remediation falls on those responsible for the release.
[sec_111] Sec 111 - Uses of the Superfund -- Section 111 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act sets out the authorized uses of the Superfund, allowing the trust to finance cleanup of hazardous waste sites, conduct emergency response actions, and cover investigation, assessment and remedial costs as well as related administrative expenses.
[sec_113] Sec 113 - Settlements and Contribution -- Section 113 sets out the procedures for the federal government to settle CERCLA claims and to seek contribution from other responsible parties, specifying the use of consent orders, settlement agreements and cost-recovery actions, and detailing the approval, reporting and limitation requirements for such settlements.
[sec_116] Sec 116 - Schedules for Remedial Action -- Section 116 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act establishes the framework for EPA-approved schedules that outline the timing, sequence, and milestones of remedial actions at contaminated sites, ensuring systematic cleanup and progress monitoring to protect public health and the environment.
[sec_120] Sec 120 - Federal Facilities -- Section 120 details the obligations of federal facilities under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, requiring agencies to assess, remediate, and manage hazardous waste releases on their properties. It subjects federal sites to the same cleanup standards and liability provisions that apply to private parties, facilitating coordinated response and funding.
[sec_121] Sec 121 - Cleanup Standards -- Section 121 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act establishes the national cleanup standards that federal, state, and tribal agencies must apply when assessing and remediating hazardous waste sites, outlining requirements for risk assessment, remedial action selection, and protective measures to safeguard human health and the environment.
[sec_211] Sec 211 - Superfund Trust Fund -- Section 211 establishes the Superfund Trust Fund, a financial pool that supports the cleanup of hazardous waste sites under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act. The fund is financed primarily through taxes on petroleum and chemical manufacturers and is administered by the EPA to reimburse responsible parties and cover emergency response costs.
[sec_221] Sec 221 - Tax on Certain Chemicals -- Section 221 imposes an excise tax on manufacturers, importers and producers of certain hazardous chemicals listed by the Environmental Protection Agency, with revenues directed to the Superfund trust for cleanup of contaminated sites. The tax applies annually to specified quantities of each chemical and is part of the financing mechanisms established under CERCLA.
[sec_302] Sec 302 - Emergency Planning and Notification (EPCRA) -- Section 302 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act establishes the Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA), requiring states and localities to develop chemical emergency response plans and obligating facilities to report on hazardous chemicals to help protect public health and the environment.
[sec_311] Sec 311 - Material Safety Data Sheets (EPCRA) -- Section 311 of the Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act, part of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, obligates manufacturers, importers and processors of hazardous chemicals to compile and distribute Material Safety Data Sheets that detail the properties, hazards, handling procedures and emergency measures for each listed substance. These MSDS must be kept on site and made available to employees, emergency responders and the public upon request.
[sec_312] Sec 312 - Emergency and Hazardous Chemical Inventory (EPCRA) -- Section 312 of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, part of EPCRA, requires facilities that store or use hazardous chemicals above threshold quantities to compile and submit annual emergency and hazardous chemical inventory reports, enabling state and local emergency planners to assess and respond to potential chemical releases. The reports must include chemical identities, quantities, and locations to support community right-to-know and emergency planning initiatives.
[sec_313] Sec 313 - Toxic Release Inventory (TRI) (EPCRA) -- Section 313 of the Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act requires facilities that handle listed chemicals above threshold amounts to report annual releases, transfers, and waste management quantities to the EPA, creating the publicly accessible Toxic Release Inventory. The data help communities understand local chemical hazards and support environmental decision-making.
[title_1] Title I - Hazardous Substances Releases, Liability, Compensation -- Title I of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act establishes federal authority to respond to releases of hazardous substances, defines strict liability for parties responsible for contamination, and provides a framework for compensating cleanup costs and damages to affected persons and natural resources. It also outlines the mechanisms for cost recovery, joint and several liability, and the role of the federal government in overseeing remedial actions.
[title_2] Title II - Hazardous Substance Response Revenue -- Title II of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act establishes a revenue system to support hazardous-substance response activities, authorizing the Environmental Protection Agency to assess taxes on chemicals, allocate fees, and collect funds that are deposited into a trust for cleanup and disposal of hazardous waste.
[title_3] Title III - Miscellaneous Provisions -- Title III of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act contains a range of miscellaneous provisions, including grant-making authority, private-party enforcement mechanisms, state participation requirements, reporting duties, and other administrative and procedural rules that support the broader cleanup and liability framework of the statute.
[title_4] Title IV - Pollution Insurance -- Title IV of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act creates a federal pollution-insurance program that obligates owners and operators of facilities handling hazardous substances to secure insurance or other financial assurance for cleanup costs, and establishes mechanisms for insurance pools and risk assessment.
