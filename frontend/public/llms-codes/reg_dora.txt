[art_1] Art 1 - Subject matter -- Article 1 sets out the purpose and scope of the Digital Operational Resilience Act, establishing a framework to ensure that financial entities in the EU maintain robust and secure information-and-communication-technology systems, and to protect the integrity of the financial sector against ICT-related risks. It defines the regulation's aim of harmonising resilience requirements across all relevant market participants.
[art_10] Art 10 - Detection -- Art 10 of the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act requires ICT-risk management frameworks to include systematic detection capabilities for security-related incidents, ensuring continuous monitoring, timely identification of anomalies, and prompt escalation to the appropriate authorities.
[art_11] Art 11 - Response and recovery -- Article 11 of the Digital Operational Resilience Act requires firms to establish and maintain effective response and recovery arrangements for ICT-related incidents. It obliges them to develop documented plans, test them regularly, and ensure the capability to restore services and protect data promptly after a disruption.
[art_17] Art 17 - ICT-related incident management process -- Article 17 of the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act obliges financial entities to set up a documented ICT-related incident management process covering detection, classification, response, recovery and reporting. It requires timely notification to competent authorities, coordination with third-party providers and regular testing to ensure resilience against cyber and system disruptions.
[art_18] Art 18 - Classification of ICT-related incidents -- Article 18 of the Digital Operational Resilience Act requires financial entities to categorize ICT-related incidents by impact and severity, using a standardized set of levels such as minor, major and critical. The classification guides the appropriate response, escalation and reporting obligations under the regulation.
[art_19] Art 19 - Reporting of major ICT-related incidents -- Art 19 obliges financial entities to notify the competent authority of any major ICT-related incident that significantly impacts the continuity or security of their services. The report must be submitted without undue delay, include a description of the incident, its impact, and remedial actions taken, and be followed by a detailed written account within a specified period.
[art_2] Art 2 - Scope -- Art 2 sets out the scope of the Digital Operational Resilience Act, defining the financial entities and ICT service providers to which the regulation applies. It covers credit institutions, investment firms, insurance undertakings, payment institutions and other designated financial market participants operating within the EU.
[art_24] Art 24 - General requirements for digital operational resilience testing -- Art 24 of the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act sets out the mandatory standards for testing the digital resilience of financial entities, requiring regular, comprehensive assessments of ICT systems, processes and dependencies. It obliges firms to adopt well-designed testing methodologies, document results, address identified weaknesses promptly, and ensure that testing outcomes are integrated into overall risk management and governance frameworks.
[art_26] Art 26 - Threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) -- Article 26 of the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act requires financial entities to carry out threat-led penetration testing to evaluate the effectiveness of their security controls against realistic attack scenarios, detailing the methodology, frequency and reporting obligations. It is intended to improve cyber-resilience by identifying vulnerabilities before they can be exploited.
[art_28] Art 28 - Key principles for managing ICT third-party risk -- Article 28 establishes the fundamental principles that financial entities must follow when managing ICT third-party risk, requiring them to identify, assess, monitor and mitigate risks throughout the lifecycle of a service. It mandates that contracts contain clear security, incident-reporting and exit-strategy provisions, that oversight be proportionate to the risk level, and that entities maintain effective governance and documentation of these arrangements.
[art_3] Art 3 - Definitions -- Article 3 establishes the key definitions applied throughout the Digital Operational Resilience Act, clarifying terms such as ICT risk, financial entity, critical third-party provider, and resilience measures. These definitions set the scope and interpretation for the Act's obligations on information-communication technology systems within the EU financial sector.
[art_30] Art 30 - Key contractual provisions -- Article 30 of the Digital Operational Resilience Act sets out the mandatory contractual clauses that financial entities must include in agreements with ICT service providers. It requires clear allocation of responsibilities for security, continuity, risk management, reporting, audit rights and termination conditions, ensuring that the provider's obligations support the entity's overall operational resilience and regulatory compliance.
[art_31] Art 31 - Designation of critical ICT third-party service providers -- Article 31 of the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act requires financial entities to identify and formally designate ICT third-party service providers whose services are deemed critical for the continuity of their operations, ensuring that these providers are subject to enhanced oversight, risk assessments and contractual safeguards to mitigate systemic ICT risks.
[art_33] Art 33 - Tasks of the Lead Overseer -- Article 33 outlines the duties of the lead overseer, who coordinates supervisory activities across national authorities, ensures consistent application of the DORA framework, monitors the digital resilience of covered entities, and reports on compliance and emerging risks to the European Commission and other stakeholders.
[art_35] Art 35 - Oversight powers -- Article 35 of the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (2022/2554) defines the supervisory authorities' oversight powers, granting them the ability to monitor, inspect and enforce compliance by financial entities. It allows regulators to request information, conduct onsite inspections, and impose corrective measures or penalties to ensure operational resilience and security of ICT systems.
[art_5] Art 5 - Governance and organisation -- Article 5 obliges financial entities to establish a clear governance framework that assigns digital-operational-resilience responsibilities to senior management and the board, implements internal controls and reporting procedures, and ensures policies and risk-management processes are regularly reviewed to address ICT risks.
[art_6] Art 6 - ICT risk management framework -- Article 6 obliges financial entities to set up a comprehensive ICT risk-management framework that identifies, protects against, detects, responds to and recovers from ICT risks in a manner proportionate to their size and complexity. The framework must be documented, integrated with overall governance and reviewed regularly.
[art_7] Art 7 - ICT systems, protocols and tools -- Article 7 sets out the obligations for financial entities to ensure that their information-and-communication technology systems, protocols and tools are designed, operated and maintained with sufficient security and resilience. It requires appropriate governance, risk-management measures and the use of reliable, up-to-date technologies to protect against cyber threats and operational disruptions.
[art_8] Art 8 - Identification -- Article 8 obliges financial entities to keep an up-to-date inventory of their ICT assets, services and critical functions, to identify relevant third-party dependencies, and to use this information for risk assessment, monitoring and reporting, thereby establishing a clear digital footprint for supervisory oversight.
[art_9] Art 9 - Protection and prevention -- Article 9 of the Digital Operational Resilience Act requires financial entities to put in place protective and preventive measures for their information-and-communication technology systems, including risk-based safeguards, incident-prevention protocols and regular testing to ensure ongoing operational resilience.
[ch_1] Chapter I - General Provisions -- Chapter I defines the scope, objectives and key terminology of the Digital Operational Resilience Act, establishing the EU's legal framework for ensuring the security, continuity and resilience of information-and-communication technology services provided by financial entities.
[ch_2] Chapter II - ICT Risk Management -- Chapter II of the Digital Operational Resilience Act (EU 2022/2554) establishes mandatory ICT risk-management requirements for financial entities, detailing governance structures, risk assessment processes, and security controls. It also obliges firms to conduct regular testing, maintain detailed documentation, and report significant ICT-related incidents to competent authorities.
[ch_3] Chapter III - ICT-Related Incident Management -- Chapter III of the Digital Operational Resilience Act sets out the framework for managing ICT-related incidents, requiring entities to establish robust detection, response and recovery procedures, classify incidents by severity and timelines, and report significant events to competent authorities to ensure consistent oversight across the EU.
[ch_4] Chapter IV - Digital Operational Resilience Testing -- Chapter IV of the Digital Operational Resilience Act sets out requirements for testing the robustness of ICT systems in the financial sector. It obliges firms to carry out regular, structured testing-including threat-led penetration testing and scenario-based assessments-to verify that they can withstand cyber-incidents and operational disruptions, and to report results to competent authorities.
[ch_5] Chapter V - Managing ICT Third-Party Risk -- The Digital Operational Resilience Act (EU) 2022/2554, Chapter V sets out requirements for financial entities to identify, assess and monitor risks arising from third-party information-and-communication-technology (ICT) providers. It mandates contractual safeguards, ongoing oversight, and contingency planning to ensure that outsourced ICT services do not compromise the institution's operational resilience.
[ch_6] Chapter VI - Information-Sharing Arrangements -- The Digital Operational Resilience Act (EU) 2022/2554 Chapter VI outlines the framework for information-sharing arrangements among financial entities and competent authorities. It defines the purposes, scopes and security safeguards required for voluntary and mandatory data exchanges, aiming to enhance collective cyber-risk awareness while protecting confidentiality and complying with data-privacy rules.
[ch_7] Chapter VII - Oversight Framework for Critical ICT Third-Party Providers -- Chapter VII of the Digital Operational Resilience Act establishes an EU-wide oversight framework for critical ICT third-party providers. It requires designated providers to register with competent authorities, submit regular resilience reports, and undergo continuous supervisory monitoring, while granting regulators powers to enforce compliance, impose sanctions and ensure coordinated cross-border supervision of essential digital services.
