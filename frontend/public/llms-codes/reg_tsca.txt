[ahera] AHERA - Asbestos Hazard Emergency Response Act -- AHERA is an amendment to the Toxic Substances Control Act that requires schools to inspect for asbestos-containing building materials, develop and implement management and response plans, and conduct periodic training and reporting to protect occupants from asbestos exposure. It establishes a national program to ensure safe handling, removal, and monitoring of asbestos hazards in educational facilities.
[ahera_inspection] Inspection and Management Plans for Schools -- The "Inspection and Management Plans for Schools" entry under the Toxic Substances Control Act outlines requirements for schools to conduct regular inspections of chemical inventories, evaluate hazards, and develop corrective action plans to ensure compliance with TSCA regulations. It provides guidance on record-keeping, staff training, and coordination with state and federal agencies to protect students and personnel from toxic substance risks.
[formaldehyde] Formaldehyde Emission Standards for Composite Wood Products -- The TSCA formaldehyde emission standards set maximum permissible levels of formaldehyde released from composite wood products such as particleboard, medium-density fibreboard and hardwood plywood. Manufacturers must test and certify compliance, and the EPA enforces labeling, record-keeping and penalties for violations. The rules aim to reduce indoor air pollution and protect public health.
[lautenberg] Frank R. Lautenberg Chemical Safety for the 21st Century Act (2016 Amendments) -- The Frank R. Lautenberg Chemical Safety for the 21st Century Act amends the Toxic Substances Control Act to modernize chemical review, requiring EPA to evaluate existing chemicals for risk, prioritize them, and implement risk-based management. It also expands authority for public participation, confidential business information protection, and state-level chemical safety programs.
[lead_paint] Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction -- The Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction program sets standards for identifying, assessing, and controlling lead-based paint hazards in homes, especially those built before 1978, to protect occupants-particularly children-from lead exposure. It requires inspections, risk assessments, certified abatement work, and compliance with EPA safety and reporting requirements.
[lead_rrp] Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) Rule -- The lead-RRP rule, issued under the Toxic Substances Control Act, mandates that contractors performing renovation, repair, or painting activities on pre-1978 buildings follow specific lead-based paint safety practices. It requires certification, worker training, and the use of containment and cleaning procedures to minimize lead dust exposure for occupants and workers. Compliance is enforced by the EPA and failure can result in civil penalties.
[sec_11] Sec 11 - Inspections and Subpoenas -- Section 11 of the Toxic Substances Control Act authorizes the EPA to conduct inspections of facilities that manufacture, process, or otherwise handle chemical substances and to issue subpoenas for records, testimony, or other evidence; it also sets out the procedural rules, notice requirements, and penalties for failure to comply.
[sec_14] Sec 14 - Confidential Business Information (CBI) -- Section 14 of the Toxic Substances Control Act protects confidential business information submitted to the EPA, allowing manufacturers and processors to withhold trade secrets, formulas, processes, or other proprietary data from public disclosure, while still permitting the agency to use the information for regulatory purposes.
[sec_16] Sec 16 - Penalties -- Section 16 of the Toxic Substances Control Act sets out the civil and criminal penalties for violations of the law, authorizing the EPA to assess fines, injunctions, and, where appropriate, imprisonment. Civil penalties can reach up to $25,000 per day for each violation, while criminal penalties may include fines of up to $100,000 and up to three years' incarceration, depending on the nature and seriousness of the offense.
[sec_18] Sec 18 - Preemption of State Law -- Section 18 of the Toxic Substances Control Act establishes that state laws may not conflict with federal TSCA regulations, effectively preempting state requirements that duplicate or interfere with the Act's provisions unless Congress expressly permits such state action. This provision limits state authority over chemical manufacturing, testing, and reporting to ensure a uniform national regulatory framework.
[sec_26] Sec 26 - Risk Evaluation Procedures and Criteria -- Section 26 of the Toxic Substances Control Act outlines the procedures the EPA must follow when conducting risk evaluations for chemical substances, including the selection of hazards, exposure pathways, and vulnerable populations. It also sets criteria for determining whether a substance presents an unreasonable risk, guiding decisions on potential regulatory actions.

The agency may issue orders for specific tests, set testing schedules, and enforce compliance through penalties.

It sets out procedures for identifying, assessing and responding to such imminent hazards, including enforcement measures to mitigate risks.

[sec_8e] Sec 8(e) - Substantial Risk Notification -- Section 8(e) of the Toxic Substances Control Act requires manufacturers or importers to notify the EPA when they have reason to believe a chemical poses a substantial risk of injury to health or the environment, prompting agency evaluation and possible risk-management actions.
[title_1] Title I - Control of Toxic Substances -- Title I of the Toxic Substances Control Act authorizes the EPA to evaluate, regulate, and restrict the manufacture, import, processing, distribution, use and disposal of chemical substances that may present unreasonable risks to health or the environment, establishing testing requirements and permitting mechanisms for new and existing chemicals. It also provides the framework for reporting, record-keeping, and enforcement actions to ensure compliance with safety standards.
[title_2] Title II - Asbestos Hazard Emergency Response -- Title II of the Toxic Substances Control Act establishes the federal framework for responding to asbestos emergencies, directing the EPA to develop response plans, training programs, and cleanup protocols to protect public health and the environment when asbestos is released.
[title_3] Title III - Indoor Radon Abatement -- Title III of the Toxic Substances Control Act authorizes the EPA to address indoor radon hazards, directing the agency to develop a national program for radon testing, public education, and mitigation strategies to reduce exposure in homes and workplaces. It establishes guidelines for radon measurement, reporting, and the implementation of abatement techniques to protect public health.
