[PS] Pain Scale -- PS denotes the Pain Scale within the Numeric Pain Rating Scale Categories, a standardized tool where patients assign a number-typically 0 for no pain to 10 for the worst imaginable pain-to quantify their current pain intensity. The scale aids clinicians in assessing, monitoring, and managing pain across a variety of medical contexts.
[PS.00] 0: No Pain -- PS.00 designates the "0: No Pain" category on the Numeric Pain Rating Scale, indicating the individual reports a pain level of zero. It serves as the baseline reference point for pain assessment in clinical and research settings.
[PS.01] 1-3: Mild Pain -- PS.01 designates the mild-pain category on the numeric pain rating scale, covering scores from 1 to 3 on a 0-10 range. It indicates low-level discomfort that typically does not significantly limit daily activities or require strong analgesics. This classification helps clinicians differentiate mild pain from moderate or severe levels for appropriate treatment planning.
[PS.04] 4-6: Moderate Pain -- PS.04 identifies a score of 4 to 6 on the Numeric Pain Rating Scale, signifying moderate pain that may be distracting or limit certain activities but does not usually prevent most daily functions.
[PS.07] 7-9: Severe Pain -- PS.07 denotes the severe pain category on the Numeric Pain Rating Scale, encompassing scores from 7 to 9 where patients report intense, distressing discomfort that may limit function and often requires stronger analgesic interventions.
[PS.10] 10: Worst Possible Pain -- PS.10 marks the top tier of the Numeric Pain Rating Scale, indicating the worst possible pain a patient can report, usually scored as 10 out of 10. It provides clinicians with a reference point for assessing pain severity and informing treatment choices.
[PS.11] NRS (Numeric Rating Scale) -- PS.11 refers to the Numeric Rating Scale (NRS) used for pain assessment, where patients rate their pain intensity on a scale from 0 (no pain) to 10 (worst possible pain). The scale provides a simple, quantifiable measure for clinicians to track changes in pain over time and guide treatment decisions. It is commonly employed in clinical settings, research, and patient self-reporting.
[PS.12] VAS (Visual Analog Scale) -- PS.12 denotes the Visual Analog Scale, a numeric pain rating method that asks patients to indicate their current pain intensity on a 10-centimetre line anchored by "no pain" and "worst pain imaginable." The distance marked from the "no pain" end is measured in millimetres, yielding a score from 0 to 100 that quantifies subjective pain levels for clinical and research use.
[PS.13] Wong-Baker FACES Scale -- The PS.13 entry classifies the Wong-Baker FACES Scale, a pediatric pain-assessment tool that uses a series of six cartoon faces ranging from a happy face (no pain) to a crying face (worst pain). Each face corresponds to a numeric rating from 0 to 10, allowing patients, especially children, to communicate pain intensity visually.
[PS.14] FLACC Scale (Pediatric) -- PS.14 refers to the FLACC Scale, a pediatric pain assessment tool that observes five behavioral categories-Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, and Consolability-each scored from 0 to 2. The summed score ranges from 0 to 10, providing a quantitative estimate of pain for infants, toddlers, and children unable to self-report verbally.
[PS.15] McGill Pain Questionnaire -- PS.15 denotes the McGill Pain Questionnaire, a multidimensional instrument that captures sensory, affective and evaluative aspects of pain through a set of descriptive words and a numeric rating component, offering a richer profile of pain experience than simple numeric scales alone.
[PS.16] Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) -- The Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) is a self-report questionnaire that gathers both pain intensity ratings on a 0-10 numeric scale and the degree to which pain interferes with daily activities such as mood, walking, work and sleep. It is widely used in clinical and research settings to track treatment outcomes and functional impact.
