[part_1] Part 1 - Federal Acquisition Regulations System -- Part 1 defines the structure, purpose, and authority of the Federal Acquisition Regulation system, outlines the relationship among the FAR, agency supplements, and other regulations, and provides definitions and abbreviations used throughout the procurement community.
[part_12] Part 12 - Acquisition of Commercial Products and Services -- Part 12 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth policies and procedures for acquiring commercial items, aiming to streamline procurement by treating commercially available products and services as they are offered in the marketplace. It defines commercial items, outlines simplified contract terms, and encourages the use of commercial practices to reduce administrative burden and foster competition.
[part_13] Part 13 - Simplified Acquisition Procedures -- Part 13 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation outlines simplified acquisition procedures for federal agencies to procure goods and services below the simplified acquisition threshold. It authorizes streamlined methods such as purchase orders, simplified solicitations, and limited competition to reduce administrative burden and accelerate procurement while still ensuring fairness and accountability.
[part_14] Part 14 - Sealed Bidding -- Part 14 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets out the rules for sealed-bidding procurements, detailing how agencies must solicit, receive, and evaluate competitive bids, award contracts to the lowest responsive and responsible offer, and handle related procedural requirements to promote fairness and transparency.
[part_15] Part 15 - Contracting by Negotiation -- Part 15 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth the policies and procedures for awarding contracts through negotiated processes, detailing how solicitations are issued, proposals evaluated, and contracts awarded. It also covers requirements for cost or price analysis, source-selection criteria, contractor debriefings and related documentation to ensure fair and transparent negotiations.
[part_16] Part 16 - Types of Contracts -- Part 16 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation defines the government's contract types, such as fixed-price, cost-reimbursement, incentive, and time-and-materials contracts, and explains when each is appropriate. It also sets out related pricing rules, clause requirements, and administrative guidance for using these contracts.
[part_19] Part 19 - Small Business Programs -- Part 19 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation outlines the policies and procedures for government contracts that support small businesses, including set-aside programs, subcontracting requirements, and assistance initiatives for veteran-owned, HUBZone, and women-owned enterprises. It defines eligibility criteria, reporting obligations, and the mechanisms used to ensure a fair share of contracting opportunities for qualifying small-business firms.
[part_2] Part 2 - Definitions of Words and Terms -- Part 2 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation, found in 48 CFR Chapter 1, compiles the official definitions of words and terms used throughout the FAR. It establishes a common vocabulary for contracting officials, contractors, and legal practitioners, ensuring consistent interpretation of acquisition policies and procedures.
[part_22] Part 22 - Application of Labor Laws to Government Acquisitions -- Part 22 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth how federal labor statutes apply to government contracts, detailing requirements such as the Davis-Bacon Act, Service Contract Act, and other prevailing-wage rules, and providing guidance on exemptions, certifications and contractor responsibilities.

[part_25] Part 25 - Foreign Acquisition (Buy American Act) -- Part 25 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation establishes policies and procedures for the Buy American Act, requiring that the United States government give preference to domestic end products and first-tier components in solicitations and contracts. It defines "domestic" and "foreign" goods, outlines allowable exceptions, and sets thresholds and documentation needed to determine compliance with the foreign acquisition restrictions.
[part_27] Part 27 - Patents, Data, and Copyrights -- Part 27 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation establishes the government's rights and responsibilities concerning patents, data, and copyrights arising under federal contracts. It defines how contractors must disclose inventions, how the government may use, reproduce, and license patented and data items, and the terms for protecting and sharing copyrighted material, including procedures for decompilation and licensing.

It defines prohibited conduct such as fraud, bribery, kickbacks and self-dealing, outlines reporting requirements, and prescribes penalties and corrective actions to protect the integrity of the procurement process.
[part_30] Part 30 - Cost Accounting Standards Administration -- Part 30 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth the administration of Cost Accounting Standards for contracts subject to CAS, describing the duties of the contracting agency to enforce compliance, the procedures contractors must follow to report cost-accounting practices, and the remedies for non-compliance, including withholding payments and contract termination.
[part_31] Part 31 - Contract Cost Principles and Procedures -- Part 31 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation establishes the cost principles and procedures that govern the allowability, allocability, and reasonableness of costs incurred by contractors on government contracts, covering direct, indirect, and overhead expenses as well as specific treatment of labor, materials, and profit. It provides guidance for auditors, contractors, and contracting officers to ensure consistent cost accounting and reimbursement practices across federal acquisitions.
[part_32] Part 32 - Contract Financing -- Part 32 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets out the policies and procedures for contract financing, covering advance payments, progress payments and reimbursement of costs. It defines the authority, limits and documentation required for contractors to receive government funds under a contract.

[part_37] Part 37 - Service Contracting -- Part 37 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation provides the policies and procedures for acquiring services, defining the types of service contracts, and setting standards for contract formation, performance, and administration. It guides agencies on evaluating proposals, pricing, and contractor responsibility for service work.
[part_39] Part 39 - Acquisition of Information Technology -- Part 39 sets forth the Federal Acquisition Regulation's policies and procedures for acquiring information technology, covering planning, acquisition strategies, contract types, and compliance with cybersecurity, sustainability and lifecycle-management requirements.
[part_4] Part 4 - Administrative and Information Matters -- Part 4 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation, found in 48 CFR Chapter 1, establishes the administrative framework for government procurement. It outlines policies on contract clauses, procurement methods, record-keeping, reporting requirements, and the dissemination of acquisition information to ensure consistency, transparency, and compliance across all federal agencies.
[part_42] Part 42 - Contract Administration and Audit Services -- Part 42 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth the policies and procedures for contract administration, covering contract modifications, closeouts, and the responsibilities of the Defense Contract Audit Agency in auditing contractor costs and performance.
[part_45] Part 45 - Government Property -- Part 45 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth policies and procedures for government-owned property that is acquired, used, or managed by contractors. It defines the responsibilities of the government and contractors for identification, control, accounting, use, and disposal of such property, and establishes the administrative processes required to ensure proper stewardship throughout the contract lifecycle.
[part_46] Part 46 - Quality Assurance -- Part 46 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation establishes the government's quality-assurance requirements for contracts, defining contractor and agency responsibilities for inspection, acceptance, and corrective actions. It prescribes the development and implementation of quality control plans, specifies standards for workmanship and materials, and outlines procedures for reporting and resolving nonconformities.
[part_49] Part 49 - Termination of Contracts -- Part 49 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets out the policies and procedures for ending government contracts, covering termination for default and termination for convenience. It details the rights and obligations of contractors and agencies, how outstanding claims are settled, and the steps for contract closeout.

[part_52] Part 52 - Solicitation Provisions and Contract Clauses -- Part 52 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation compiles the standardized solicitation provisions and contract clauses that agencies insert into federal contracts. It supplies the language for mandatory and optional terms, covering topics such as payment, changes, disputes and contract termination, ensuring consistency across government procurements.
[part_53] Part 53 - Forms -- Part 53 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation provides the standardized forms used throughout the procurement process, detailing the purpose, content, and submission requirements for each form. It includes templates for solicitations, contracts, modifications, and reporting, ensuring uniform documentation and compliance across federal agencies.
[part_6] Part 6 - Competition Requirements -- Part 6 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation (48 CFR Chapter 1) establishes the government's competition policies for acquisitions, defining when full and other-than-full competition are required, the circumstances that justify exceptions, and the procedures agencies must follow to document and approve those exceptions.
[part_8] Part 8 - Required Sources of Supplies and Services -- Part 8 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth the required sources of supplies and services that contracting officers must consider when acquiring items for the government. It details mandatory sources such as government-furnished property, mandatory contractor-provided items, and specified commercial or government-wide acquisition contracts, establishing the hierarchy and procedures for sourcing.
[part_9] Part 9 - Contractor Qualifications -- Part 9 of the Federal Acquisition Regulation sets forth the policies and procedures for determining whether a contractor is responsible and eligible to receive government contracts, covering factors such as financial resources, past performance, and compliance with laws.
