[app] Appendix - Special Provisions for Developing Countries -- The "app" entry refers to the Appendix of the Berne Convention that contains special provisions aimed at developing countries, granting them extended protection periods, simplified registration procedures, and technical assistance to help align their national laws with the Convention's standards.
[app_1] Compulsory license for translation -- Under the Berne Convention, a compulsory license for translation permits a government to authorize the translation of a copyrighted work without the author's consent when public interest or cultural dissemination requires it, provided that the translator pays a reasonable royalty and the author receives adequate compensation.
[app_2] Compulsory license for reproduction -- The Berne Convention allows a state to issue a compulsory licence for the reproduction of a work when the right-holder does not make the work available on reasonable terms, provided that the user pays a fair and equitable remuneration. This provision balances public access to cultural works with the protection of authors' rights.
[art_1] Article 1 - Establishment of a Union -- Article 1 of the Berne Convention establishes the Union of the contracting parties, creating an international association that ensures reciprocal protection of literary and artistic works among all member states.
[art_10] Article 10 - Exceptions (Quotations, Teaching) -- Article 10 of the Berne Convention sets out limited exceptions to exclusive rights, allowing quotations and use of works in teaching, provided the source is identified and the use does not conflict with normal exploitation. It balances author rights with public interest
[art_11] Article 11 - Right of Public Performance -- Article 11 of the Berne Convention gives authors an exclusive right to control public performances of their works, covering live presentations, broadcasts and other public renditions. It requires member states to protect this right and to provide legal remedies against unauthorized performances.
[art_11bis] Article 11bis - Right of Broadcasting -- Article 11bis of the Berne Convention grants broadcasters an exclusive right to authorize or prohibit the public communication of their broadcasts, including rebroadcast, fixation and making the broadcast available to the public, for a term of fifty years from the first transmission. The provision applies to both analog and digital broadcasts of works protected under the Convention.
[art_12] Article 12 - Right of Adaptation -- Article 12 of the Berne Convention grants authors the exclusive right to authorize adaptations of their works, including translations, dramatizations, and other transformations, ensuring they receive remuneration and control over how their creations are modified. The provision also obliges member states to protect this right in their national legislation.
[art_14] Article 14 - Cinematographic Rights -- Article 14 of the Berne Convention grants authors the right to control the making of cinematographic adaptations of their works, ensuring they receive appropriate remuneration and consent for such uses. It also obliges member states to protect these rights, extending the same moral and economic safeguards to cinematographic works as to the original creations.
[art_14bis] Article 14bis - Special Provisions for Cinematographic Works -- Article 14bis of the Berne Convention provides special protection for cinematographic works, extending the general rights of authors to include the right to claim authorship and to object to any distortion, mutilation or other modification that would prejudice the author's reputation. It obliges member states to grant these rights to cinematographers and to ensure that they can enforce them against any unauthorized exploitation of the film. The provision also allows for the recognition of co-authors in collaborative productions.
[art_14ter] Article 14ter - Droit de Suite (Resale Right) -- Article 14ter of the Berne Convention establishes the "droit de suite," granting visual artists a royalty each time their original works are resold on the secondary market, typically through auctions or galleries. The provision aims to ensure creators receive a share of the increased value of their works as they change hands over time.
[art_2] Article 2 - Protected Works -- Article 2 of the Berne Convention defines the scope of protection, stating that literary, scientific and artistic creations-including books, musical compositions, paintings, sculptures, films, photographs and architectural designs-are covered regardless of the medium in which they are expressed, thereby granting them the same international copyright rights.
[art_3] Article 3 - Criteria of Eligibility for Protection -- Article 3 of the Berne Convention sets out the basic requirement for a work to receive protection: it must be an original literary or artistic creation that is expressed in a tangible form. The provision emphasizes that protection does not depend on the work's subject, purpose, or the nationality of its author.
[art_5] Article 5 - Rights Guaranteed (National Treatment and Automatic Protection) -- Article 5 of the Berne Convention obliges each member state to grant foreign works the same rights as its own nationals, ensuring national treatment and automatic protection without any formalities. It requires protection of both moral and economic rights-such as reproduction, translation, performance and adaptation-from the moment a work is created.
[art_6bis] Article 6bis - Moral Rights -- Article 6bis of the Berne Convention establishes the moral rights of authors, granting them the right to claim authorship of their works and to object to any distortion, mutilation or other modification that would prejudice their honour or reputation. These rights are independent of economic rights, are inalienable, and last for the author's lifetime.
[art_7] Article 7 - Term of Protection -- Article 7 of the Berne Convention establishes the minimum term of protection for literary and artistic works, requiring each member state to protect a work for the life of its author plus at least fifty years after the author's death, with analogous periods for works of joint authorship and post-humous publications. The article also allows for longer terms, provided they do not fall below this baseline.
[art_8] Article 8 - Right of Translation -- Article 8 of the Berne Convention grants authors the exclusive right to authorize translations of their literary or artistic works, requiring member states to protect that right and give authors control over any translation as they would a reproduction. The provision ensures that translations receive the same level of protection as the original work.
[art_9] Article 9 - Right of Reproduction -- Article 9 of the Berne Convention grants authors an exclusive right to authorize the reproduction of their works in any form, including making copies or adaptations, while allowing limited exceptions for lawful uses. It obliges member states to protect against unauthorized reproduction and to uphold the author's moral rights.
